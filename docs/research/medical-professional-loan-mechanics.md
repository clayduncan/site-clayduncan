## Section 2. Plain-English Summary (For the Medical Buyer)

A medical professional mortgage, often marketed as a "physician loan," "doctor loan," or "medical professional loan," is a category of non-agency, lender-specific portfolio mortgage products. The originating bank typically holds these loans on its own balance sheet rather than selling them to Fannie Mae or Freddie Mac. Because these are portfolio products, every detail of every program is set by the individual lender. There is no single "physician loan rule." Eligible professions, eligible degrees, training-status windows, student loan treatment, down payment tiers, loan amount ceilings, reserve requirements, and refinance availability all vary materially by program. The first practical implication: a Bank of America doctor loan, a Truist doctor loan, a Regions doctor loan, and a Huntington physician loan are different products with different rules. The second practical implication: every guideline below should be read as "common in the segment, varies by program, verify at the lender."

The category exists because clinical training delays the income launch by years, accumulates substantial student loan debt, and creates relocation pressure that does not fit standard agency rules well. Lenders that specialize in this segment treat the category as low-risk because clinical professionals have predictable long-term income trajectories, low default rates, and high banking-relationship value. To make the products work, lenders typically waive private mortgage insurance even at low or zero down payment, allow qualification on a signed offer letter or employment contract before the borrower's start date, and treat student loans more flexibly than the agency baseline.

The features that recur across most programs are: zero or low down payment at lower loan amounts (commonly up to a tier somewhere between $750,000 and $1 million), 5 percent down at mid tiers (commonly through $1.5 million to $2 million), 10 percent or more above that, no PMI even at high LTV, qualification on a signed and fully executed employment contract before the start date (commonly 60 to 90 days, sometimes longer), and student loan treatment that exceeds the conventional baseline (Income-Driven Repayment payment used at face value, deferred loans excluded under specific documentation, or a fixed percentage of balance applied that is more favorable than the conventional 1 percent of balance default). Most are 30-year fully amortizing fixed or ARM products without prepayment penalties, restricted to primary residences, with credit floors typically in the 680 to 720 range and DTI ceilings in the 43 to 50 percent range.

The category that varies most is eligibility. Medical doctors (MD) and doctors of osteopathic medicine (DO) are universally included. Dentists (DDS, DMD) are commonly included. Veterinarians (DVM), podiatrists (DPM), and optometrists (OD) are included by many programs but not all. Pharmacists (PharmD), CRNAs, nurse practitioners, physician assistants, and physical therapists are included by some programs. Some lenders (notably Regions through its "Emerging Professionals" program) extend further to attorneys, CPAs, and select other professionals. Marketing terminology like "doctor loan" or "physician loan" does not establish that a specific borrower's profession qualifies under any specific program. The eligibility check is always lender-program-specific.

The category that is most volatile right now is student loan treatment. The federal Saving on a Valuable Education (SAVE) plan was officially terminated by the U.S. Court of Appeals for the 8th Circuit on 03-10-2026, and the Department of Education began transitioning roughly 7.5 million SAVE-enrolled borrowers into legal repayment plans on 03-27-2026. The new Repayment Assistance Plan (RAP) launches 07-01-2026 under the Working Families Tax Cuts Act (sometimes referred to as One Big Beautiful Bill Act provisions), and PAYE and ICR will sunset 07-01-2028 for loans disbursed before 07-01-2026. Many physician borrowers in IDR are currently in a forbearance or transition status that mortgage underwriters must navigate carefully. Conventional, FHA, and VA underwriting all have specific placeholder rules for $0 or undocumented student loan payments, and several medical professional programs explicitly accommodate the IDR documentation that conventional rules treat less generously.

The pre-offer planning sequence for a medical buyer is: pull and review credit 6 to 12 months out; document the student loan portfolio and current repayment status; engage a loan officer for a planning conversation 3 to 6 months before shopping; identify two or three lender programs the borrower's profile actually fits; verify the offer letter or employment contract is fully executed with all required elements; confirm the start date in writing; confirm the cash-to-close target and the reserve target with the lender's specific requirements applied; and complete a verified-income, verified-asset pre-approval with the chosen lender before writing an offer. Tax, legal, and student-loan-strategy questions route to a CPA, attorney, and financial advisor or specialized student loan counselor respectively.

## Section 3. The Medical Professional Mortgage Program Landscape

### 3.1 Plain-English Definition

A medical professional mortgage is a residential mortgage product made by a bank or credit union to a clinical professional under that lender's portfolio underwriting standards rather than the agency standards of Fannie Mae or Freddie Mac. The defining commercial feature is that the lender retains the credit risk on its own balance sheet (or sells into a private investor channel under the lender's specifications), which gives the lender flexibility on guidelines that the agency channel does not allow.

Common features that distinguish this category from the agency channel:

- Low or zero down payment at significant loan amounts without private mortgage insurance
- Future-income qualification on a signed and fully executed employment contract before the borrower's start date
- Alternative student loan treatment that can exceed the agency baseline in flexibility
- Loan amount tiers that extend beyond conforming loan limits without entering jumbo pricing for many programs

These programs are typically Qualified Mortgages under 12 CFR 1026.43(e), but specific structural features (such as interest-only ARMs offered by some programs) can move a particular product into non-QM territory. The QM/non-QM status of any specific lender's specific product must be verified directly with the lender. The CFPB ATR/QM rule itself is found at 12 CFR 1026.43, with the General QM definition revised effective 03-01-2021 and mandatory compliance from 10-01-2022.

### 3.2 Why the Programs Exist

The economic case for the lender:

- Clinical professionals have predictable long-term income trajectories that justify forward-looking underwriting
- Default rates in this segment are low relative to the typical debt-to-income profile
- Long-term banking relationship value is high (mortgage, deposits, wealth management, practice financing, retirement planning)
- Student loan balances and IDR plan participation are common across the segment, so accommodating them is a feature, not an exception

The economic case for the borrower:

- Clinical training extends the income launch by 3 to 7 or more years (residency plus optional fellowship)
- Student loan debt is substantial. The AAMC reports the median 2024 medical school graduate debt at approximately $205,000 (covering tuition, fees, and living expenses, exclusive of undergraduate debt). Industry estimates put the average physician total educational debt at roughly $264,519, with dentists at approximately $296,700 and veterinarians at approximately $183,302.
- Federal Direct Unsubsidized loans for graduate or professional students disbursed 07-01-2024 to 06-30-2025 carried an interest rate of 8.08 percent; Direct PLUS loans carried 9.08 percent (AAMC FIRST data).
- Relocation pressure is high. Residents and fellows match into specific programs, attending physicians often relocate at the end of training, and the timing of moves rarely aligns cleanly with the agency-standard requirement that the borrower has already started employment.

### 3.3 The Marketing Terminology and the Compliance Flag

Common marketing terms used across the segment:

- "Physician loan" is the most common and most general
- "Doctor loan" is interchangeable with "physician loan"
- "Medical professional loan" signals (but does not guarantee) extended eligibility beyond MD/DO
- "Healthcare professional loan" signals (but does not guarantee) further extension to non-physician roles
- "Professional loan" or "Emerging Professionals" signals (but does not guarantee) coverage of attorneys, CPAs, and others alongside medical
- "Specialty mortgage" and "portfolio mortgage" are broader umbrellas under which medical professional programs are classified internally

The compliance flag (this is the single highest-risk content category in this packet): marketing terminology does not establish actual eligibility. A "physician loan" page will not necessarily lend to every physician, and a "medical professional loan" page will not necessarily lend to every medical professional. The lender's specific written program guidelines determine eligibility. Borrower-facing content must be hedged accordingly with vocabulary like "many programs cover," "varies by lender," "subject to program-specific overlay," "common across the segment," and "verify with the specific lender." Borrower-facing content must never assert that a profession qualifies for "the" physician loan, that "physicians get" specific terms, or that any program guarantees outcomes.

### 3.4 The Variability Problem (Core Frame for the Entire Packet)

What varies materially by lender program:

- Eligible professions (MD/DO universally; everything else lender-specific)
- Eligible degree types (some programs require an active license; some accept students in their final year of training)
- Resident, fellow, and attending status eligibility and pricing
- Time-since-completion-of-training windows (some programs are within-10-years-of-training; some are within-15-years; some have no time limit; some price differently based on years out)
- Student loan treatment (covered in Section 6)
- Pre-start-date employment contract rules (60 days, 90 days, longer)
- Down payment tiers by loan amount
- Loan amount ceilings ($1 million, $1.5 million, $2 million, $2.5 million, $3 million, higher)
- Reserve requirements
- Whether the program is purchase-only or includes refinance
- Whether the program allows rate-and-term refinance, cash-out refinance, or construction-to-permanent
- Whether the program allows second homes (most do not, some do)
- Whether non-occupant co-borrowers or co-signers are allowed
- Whether visa status (H-1B, J-1 with HSC waiver, O-1, EB-2) is acceptable
- Geographic availability (most programs are state-restricted or footprint-restricted)
- Whether a depository relationship with the bank is required at origination or at closing
- Interest rate discount for autopay or relationship pricing
- Property type allowances (single-family, attached, condo with warrantability rules, townhouse, sometimes 2-unit)
- Whether the program permits log homes (Truist, for example, caps log-home LTV at 80 percent)
- Whether deferred student loans can be excluded entirely from DTI under specific documentation (varies)

The recurring frame for all borrower-facing content built from this research: lender-specific program details determine the actual offer. A pre-approval letter from one lender does not translate into the same terms at another lender.

## Section 4. Eligible Medical Professionals: Common Eligibility and the Overclaim Risk

### 4.1 Professions Most Commonly Eligible

This list is illustrative, not authoritative. Any specific lender's program may include or exclude any profession on this list. The presence of a profession on this list signals that "many programs cover" but does not establish that any specific program covers it.

Universally included where the program covers physicians at all:

- MD (Doctor of Medicine)
- DO (Doctor of Osteopathic Medicine)

Commonly included:

- DDS (Doctor of Dental Surgery)
- DMD (Doctor of Dental Medicine)
- DPM (Doctor of Podiatric Medicine)

Included by many but not all programs:

- DVM / VMD (Doctor of Veterinary Medicine)
- OD (Doctor of Optometry)

Included by some programs, excluded by others:

- PharmD (Doctor of Pharmacy)
- CRNA (Certified Registered Nurse Anesthetist)
- DNAP (Doctor of Nurse Anesthesia Practice)
- AA / CAA (Anesthesiologist Assistant / Certified Anesthesiologist Assistant)
- NP / APRN (Nurse Practitioner / Advanced Practice Registered Nurse)
- PA (Physician Assistant)
- DPT (Doctor of Physical Therapy)
- DC (Doctor of Chiropractic)
- DNP (Doctor of Nursing Practice)
- PsyD or PhD in Clinical Psychology
- SLP (Speech-Language Pathologist)
- RN, LPN (Registered Nurse, Licensed Practical Nurse) under specific programs (rare)
- CNM (Certified Nurse Midwife)

Examples of how this varies in practice:

- Bank of America Doctor Loan: cited across multiple sources as available to MDs, DOs, dentists; specific tier details (down payment at given loan amounts) are quoted to the borrower by the bank's practice specialists rather than published in detail on the consumer page.
- Truist Doctor Loan (per Truist's product flyer and consumer page): available to licensed Residents, Interns, and Fellows in MD, DO, and DPM programs, and to licensed Physicians and Dentists holding MD, DO, DPM, DDS, or DMD; doctors with more than 15 years post-training need a Truist Wealth client relationship; the program is not available in Alaska, Arizona, Hawaii, and Oregon as of recent disclosure.
- Regions Doctor Loan Program: covers MD, DO, DDS, DMD regardless of how long the borrower has been practicing.
- Regions Emerging Professionals Program: covers attorneys, certified pharmacists, nurse anesthetists (CRNA), physician assistants, nurse practitioners, veterinarians, chiropractors, optometrists, and podiatrists with no more than 7 years in their profession.
- BMO Physicians' Mortgage Program: covers MD, DDS, DMD, DO, residents, and fellows; physicians with more than 10 years post-training are not eligible for the program; the program is not offered in New York; states CA, DC, FL, HI, ID, MD, NV, and RI are limited to 95 percent maximum LTV; an autopay discount of 0.125 percent is available.
- First Horizon Doctor Loan: more restrictive on profession; cited as available to MD, DO, OMS (oral and maxillofacial surgeons), and DPM; available in 14 states (AL, AR, CT, GA, FL, LA, MS, NC, SC, TN, TX, VA, NJ, NY) per disclosure as of mid-2025; minimum credit score 670; up to $2.5 million loan amount with tiered down payment.
- Fifth Third Bank physician loan: covers MD, DO, DDS, DMD, DPM, DVM, OD per disclosed program details; available in approximately 12 states (AL, FL, GA, IL, IN, KY, MI, NC, OH, SC, TN, WV); 100 percent financing up to $1.25 million, 95 percent up to $1.75 million, 90 percent up to $2.5 million per current public summaries.
- Citizens Bank Doctor Loan: covers MD, MD-PhD, DO, DDS, DMD; borrower must have completed residency within last 10 years, be a newly licensed medical resident in residency, or begin residency within 60 days of mortgage closing; primary residence purchase only with rate-and-term refinance also eligible.
- TD Bank Medical Professional Mortgage: covers MD, DO, DPM, DDS, DMD, oral surgeons, residents, and fellows; eligibility limited to physicians less than 10 years out of residency; available in TD Bank operating states (15 states plus DC).
- Huntington physician loan: covers MD, DO, DPM, DDS, DMD, DVM per cited program summaries; offers tiered down payment based on credit score (700-plus tier and 680-to-699 tier); maximum DTI 50 percent.
- KeyBank Medical Professional Mortgage: covers MD, DO, DPM, DDS, DMD; residents, fellows, and attending physicians eligible; available in 37 states plus DC; loan amounts up to $3.5 million with tiered down payment; primary residence and second home both permitted (an unusual feature in the segment).
- First National Bank of Omaha (FNBO) Physician's Mortgage: covers MD, DO, dentists, veterinarians, DPM, PA, NP, CRNA per cited summary; minimum credit score 720; loan amounts up to $850,000 with no down payment, up to $1.5 million with 5 to 10 percent down.
- First Merchants Bank physician loan: covers MD, DO, DDS, DMD, PharmD, DVM per the bank's published page.

The pattern: the marketing label is broader than the underwriting reality. A "physician loan" program may exclude veterinarians; a "medical professional loan" page may not extend to nurse practitioners; a "doctor loan" may require an MD specifically rather than any clinical doctorate. Always read the lender's program guidelines, not the page title.

### 4.2 Training Status Eligibility

Common patterns across the segment:

- Medical, dental, or veterinary student in the final year of training, with confirmed match or executed contract: included by some programs (commonly with lower loan amount caps, sometimes $500,000 to $750,000)
- Resident (PGY-1 through PGY-7 or longer): commonly included
- Fellow: commonly included (programs that include residents almost always include fellows)
- Attending physician within X years of training completion: universally included where the program covers physicians
- Attending physician beyond the program's "years post-training" window: varies; some programs end eligibility at 10 years post-residency, some at 15 years, some have no limit, some require a wealth-management or private banking relationship beyond a specific year threshold
- Recent graduate without confirmed employment: typically excluded
- Locum-tenens-only physician with no permanent contract: typically excluded; some programs require permanent W-2 or guaranteed contract
- Self-employed private practice physician: included by some programs with additional documentation (typically two years of personal and business tax returns plus year-to-date P&L and balance sheet)

Common documentation tied to training status:

- Resident or fellow: program appointment letter on residency or fellowship program letterhead, current pay stubs from the residency or fellowship stipend, residency program contact information
- Attending pre-start-date: fully executed employment contract, departmental confirmation of the start date in writing, prior-employer documentation if transitioning from prior employment
- Attending post-start-date: most recent two pay stubs, two years of W-2s, employer verification of employment

### 4.3 Common Eligibility Caveats by Program

- U.S. citizenship or permanent residency status: required by most programs; some programs accept eligible visa types (H-1B, J-1 with HSC waiver, O-1, EB-2) at the lender's discretion. Verify each program's visa policy individually. International medical graduates (IMGs) on visas should expect to compare two or three programs because policies vary.
- License status: most programs require an active license, in-process license, or training-program license. Programs differ on whether the borrower can close prior to license issuance.
- Board certification: rarely required for the loan itself; sometimes required for highest-tier pricing.
- Self-employment for new private practice: most programs require additional documentation; some programs require two years of self-employed history before allowing self-employment income to qualify.
- Spouse income: spousal income is universally allowed where the spouse is on the loan.
- Same-sex couple and domestic partner treatment: subject to lender nondiscrimination compliance; verify the specific lender's stated practice.
- Co-borrower / non-occupant co-borrower: most programs prohibit non-occupant co-borrowers; some allow non-occupant co-signers for residents (commonly a parent).

### 4.4 The Overclaim Risk (Critical Compliance Section)

The single highest-risk pattern in medical professional mortgage marketing is the eligibility overclaim. Examples to avoid:

- Marketing that claims a profession qualifies when the lender's actual program excludes it (for instance, asserting that "veterinarians can use a physician loan" without verifying that a veterinarian-eligible program is actually available to the borrower in their state)
- Marketing that promises terms (zero down, no PMI, jumbo allowance) that the lender only extends to certain degree types or training stages
- Marketing that implies all "doctors" qualify when a specific lender means MD/DO only
- Marketing that omits training-status or years-post-training restrictions
- Marketing that omits state geographic restrictions
- Marketing that names a specific dollar tier ("up to $2 million with no down payment") without the borrower-profile and credit-floor caveats that make that tier achievable

The CFPB's general advertising standards under TILA Regulation Z and the FTC's general truth-in-advertising standards apply. The medical professional mortgage segment is not a special carve-out from those rules. Borrower-facing content must accurately describe the variability and must avoid describing programs in absolute terms.

### 4.5 How to Verify Eligibility for a Specific Borrower

A single conversation with a single lender does not establish "your eligibility" across the medical professional mortgage market. The realistic process:

1. Document the borrower's profile: profession (degree, licensure), training status, years since residency completion (if applicable), citizenship or visa status, state of property purchase, target loan amount, target purchase price, credit profile, current student loan portfolio.
2. Identify two or three lender programs whose published guidelines fit the borrower's profile.
3. Pre-qualify against each program. The pre-qualification tells the borrower the loan amount range, the rate range, the down payment requirement, and the cash-to-close range.
4. Compare pricing and terms across the two or three programs. Pricing differences are real and often material.
5. Choose the lender program that fits the borrower's purchase plan, timeline, and target home.

The role of the loan officer in this process is to map borrower profession, degree, training status, visa status, geography, and timeline to the appropriate two or three lender programs. A loan officer who only offers one lender's program cannot perform this matching function across the segment.

## Section 5. Universal Features of Medical Professional Loan Programs

This section documents features that recur across the majority of programs. Use range-based language. Specific tiers cited are illustrative, sourced to specific lenders, and not "the rule."

### 5.1 Down Payment Structure

The recurring tier pattern across the segment:

- 0 percent down at lower loan amounts. Common ceilings for the zero-down tier include $750,000, $850,000, $1,000,000, $1,250,000, and $1,500,000. The specific zero-down ceiling varies by program. Examples: First Horizon (zero down up to $1.5 million); KeyBank (zero down up to $1 million); Fifth Third (100 percent up to $1.25 million); BMO (100 percent up to $1 million); Bank of America (cited variously across reviews; specific tiers vary by relationship and credit profile and are quoted by the practice specialist).
- 5 percent down at mid-tier loan amounts. Common mid-tier ceilings include $1.5 million and $2 million. Examples: BMO (95 percent up to $1.5 million); Fifth Third (95 percent up to $1.75 million); Truist (95 percent up to $1.5 million); Citizens (5 percent down up to $850,000 in some published tiers, with a larger tier at higher down payment).
- 10 percent down at higher tiers. Common higher-tier ceilings include $2 million, $2.5 million, and $3 million. Examples: BMO (89.99 percent up to $2 million); Fifth Third (90 percent up to $2.5 million); Truist (90 percent up to $2 million for practicing doctors); First Horizon (10 percent down up to $2.5 million with appropriate profile).
- Above $2.5 million: tiers progressively expand the required down payment. KeyBank caps loan amounts at $3.5 million with tiered down payment.

The recurring frame: down payment tiers are inversely correlated with loan amount within a single program, and they vary materially across programs. A borrower buying a $1.4 million home will see a different required down payment at Truist than at BMO than at Fifth Third than at First Horizon.

### 5.2 No Private Mortgage Insurance (PMI)

The structural feature that distinguishes medical professional programs from low-down-payment conventional loans is the absence of PMI even at high LTV. Why portfolio lenders can do this: there is no agency MI requirement on a non-agency portfolio loan. The lender retains the full credit risk and prices the loan accordingly.

The pricing implication: rates on medical professional loans may be modestly higher than rates on agency loans at the same loan amount with PMI, to compensate for the absence of MI. The net cost comparison is borrower-specific and depends on the rate spread, the PMI cost, and the time horizon. Worked examples in Section 9 illustrate the comparison framework.

A common related feature: many medical professional programs offer a small autopay rate discount (often 0.125 percent) when the borrower authorizes ACH from a depository account at the same bank. BMO, Truist, Citizens, and several others offer this kind of discount.

### 5.3 Loan Amount Ceilings

Common ceilings: $1 million, $1.5 million, $2 million, $2.5 million, $3 million, and higher in select programs (KeyBank up to $3.5 million; Fulton Mortgage up to $3 million for select professions). The ceiling correlates directly with the lender's appetite for portfolio risk in the high-balance jumbo space, the borrower's profession and training stage, and the lender's preferred credit profile.

The relationship between loan amount tier and down payment tier is the core trade-off in this segment. A borrower at the upper end of a program's ceiling will typically face a higher required down payment than a borrower at the lower end.

### 5.4 Property and Occupancy Requirements

- Primary residence: universally allowed
- Second home: allowed by some programs (KeyBank explicitly; most others not). Verify each lender's policy directly. Borrowers planning to retain a prior residence as a rental should clarify rental conversion rules at the time of pre-approval.
- Investment property: typically excluded; some programs allow with significant overlay or only after a seasoning period as primary
- Property types: single-family detached, attached, planned unit developments (PUD), townhouses are universally allowed where the program covers primary residences; condos vary by warrantability rules; co-operatives are allowed by select programs (Citizens covers co-ops in select New York counties); 2-unit residences allowed by some (BMO covers 1-2 unit single-family, condominium, and townhomes); log homes allowed by Truist with 80 percent maximum LTV
- Owner-occupancy rules: most programs require the borrower to occupy the property as a primary residence, with a typical 12-month minimum occupancy period

### 5.5 Borrower Status Requirements

- Single borrower or joint: both allowed
- Non-occupant co-borrower: typically not allowed
- Non-occupant co-signer: allowed by select programs (Fulton Mortgage allows parent co-signers, particularly for residents)
- Spousal income: universally allowed where the spouse is on the loan and on title
- Same-sex couple and domestic partner: subject to lender's stated nondiscrimination practice; verify

### 5.6 Refinance Availability

- Rate-and-term refinance: included in most programs; primary residence only
- Cash-out refinance: less common; offered by a subset (Truist allows cash-out up to 80 percent LTV with up to $50,000 cash; First National Bank allows cash-out up to 80 percent; KeyBank allows up to $1,000,000 maximum cash out under specific tiers)
- Construction-to-permanent: offered by select lenders for medical professionals building custom (Citizens construction-to-permanent up to 85 percent LTV; First Citizens has a construction loan option)

### 5.7 Common Restrictions and Overlays

- Maximum DTI: 43 to 50 percent across the segment, with 45 percent the most common ceiling. Some programs allow up to 50 percent in specific scenarios (Truist up to 50 percent in certain situations; Huntington up to 50 percent).
- Minimum credit score: 680, 700, 720, or 740 most common. Bank of America generally requires solid credit (sources cite 700-plus as common). First Horizon minimum 670. BMO not publicly published but commonly cited as 700-plus. Citizens 700. Fifth Third 700-plus. Truist 700-plus. The lower the credit score, the more limited the borrower's program options.
- Reserve requirements: covered in Section 8.
- First-time-buyer-only programs: rare in this segment.
- "Within X years of completing training" eligibility windows: common (Truist 15 years; BMO 10 years; TD Bank 10 years; Citizens 10 years; First Horizon variable).
- Prepayment penalty: most programs are no-prepay.

## Section 6. Student Loan Treatment in Medical Professional Programs

This is the single highest-impact differentiator from agency lending for the medical buyer. It is also the most volatile area in 2026 due to the SAVE plan termination, the 07-01-2026 RAP launch, and the 07-01-2028 sunset of PAYE and ICR for loans disbursed before 07-01-2026. Lenders are still adapting their underwriting practices to the new federal student loan landscape. Verify current treatment with each specific lender.

### 6.1 The Conventional Baseline

Fannie Mae Selling Guide B3-6-05 (Monthly Debt Obligations, Deferred Installment Debt, Student Loans) sets the conventional baseline through Fannie Mae's automated underwriting. The current rule, restated by the underwriting community since the 2016 SEL-2016-04 announcement and through the most recent 05-04-2022 revision visible in the Selling Guide:

For all student loans (deferred, in forbearance, or in repayment), the lender must include a monthly payment in the borrower's recurring monthly debt obligation when qualifying the borrower. The lender uses one of the following options:

- 1 percent of the outstanding balance, or
- The actual payment that will fully amortize the loan as documented in the credit report, by the student loan lender, or in documentation supplied by the borrower, or
- A calculated payment that will fully amortize the loan based on the documented loan repayment terms, or
- If the repayment terms are unknown, a calculated payment that will fully amortize the loan based on the current prevailing student loan interest rate and the allowable repayment period

The actual credit report payment (including a documented Income-Driven Repayment payment) may be used for qualifying when it meets one of the above options. Where the credit report shows $0 (consistent with $0 IDR), the actual $0 payment can typically be used with documentation.

Freddie Mac's treatment under its Single-Family Seller/Servicer Guide differs at the margin:

- For student loans in repayment with a documented monthly payment, use the actual documented payment.
- For loans in deferment, forbearance, or with $0 reported payment under IBR or similar IDR plan, Freddie Mac allows 0.5 percent of the outstanding balance as the placeholder calculation (more favorable than Fannie's 1 percent).
- Freddie allows lenders to ignore student loan payments for self-employed borrowers who provide documentation that their business has paid student loans on time for at least 12 months (this is a self-employment specific rule).
- Freddie allows lenders to ignore student loans with 10 months or fewer of payments remaining when forgiveness, cancellation, discharge, or employment-contingent repayment is documented. Fannie does not allow this for student loans pending forgiveness; Fannie requires forgiveness, cancellation, or discharge to be effective as of the loan's closing date.

The key point for the medical buyer: under conventional rules, the borrower's actual IDR payment can be used for DTI as long as it is documented and reported correctly. Where it is not documented, or where the loan is in deferment or forbearance, the placeholder calculations of 1 percent (Fannie) or 0.5 percent (Freddie) of balance can dramatically inflate the borrower's DTI and reduce the eligible loan amount.

For a resident with $250,000 in student loans and a documented IBR payment of $300 per month, conventional underwriting can use $300 in DTI. For the same borrower with no documented IBR (or with the loan in SAVE forbearance during the recent transition), conventional underwriting under Fannie Mae rules can apply $2,500 (1 percent of $250,000) as the placeholder, which alone constitutes the entirety of the resident's typical DTI capacity.

### 6.2 The FHA Baseline

HUD 4000.1 establishes the FHA rule. Per Mortgagee Letter 2021-13 and the current handbook:

- Use the actual payment amount on the credit report or bill, where the payment will fully amortize the loan over its term, or
- Use 0.5 percent of the outstanding balance as the placeholder when the credit report shows $0 or the loan is in deferment or forbearance, or when the payment will not fully amortize the loan.

Note: under the current FHA rule, a $0 IBR payment is not acceptable; the FHA placeholder of 0.5 percent of balance applies in that scenario. This is an important distinction from the conventional rule. Industry secondary sources have repeatedly highlighted the problem this creates for borrowers in SAVE forbearance, who saw 0.5 percent of balance applied to their DTI and were rejected from FHA pre-approval despite no actual payment requirement during the forbearance.

### 6.3 The VA Baseline

VA Pamphlet 26-7 Chapter 4 (Credit Underwriting), supplemented by VA Circular 26-17-02 (Clarification and New Policy for Student Loan Debts and Obligations, 01-23-2017):

- If the student loan is documented as deferred at least 12 months beyond the closing date, no payment is counted in the borrower's recurring monthly obligations (this is a meaningful advantage for residents and fellows whose deferments extend beyond closing).
- If the borrower is in repayment, the lender uses the higher of (a) the actual monthly payment shown on the credit report or documented via servicer statement, or (b) 5 percent of the outstanding loan balance divided by 12.
- Private student loans are treated under the same rules.

The VA approach is structurally different from the conventional approach. The 5 percent of balance divided by 12 calculation produces a placeholder of approximately 0.417 percent of balance per month, which is between the Fannie and Freddie placeholders, but the deferment-beyond-12-months exclusion is a strong advantage for a borrower in active deferment.

VA underwriting also weighs residual income (the dollars left after the mortgage, debts, taxes, and estimated living expenses) by region and family size, which can offset elevated DTI in some cases. A military physician (or military veterinarian, or military dentist) who is VA-eligible should compare the VA path to the medical professional program before choosing.

### 6.4 USDA Baseline (Brief Note)

USDA Rural Development student loan rules are similar to FHA in placeholder structure but are typically not relevant for medical professional buyers in metro and exurban markets. USDA is excluded from extended discussion in this packet because the typical medical buyer's target market is urban or suburban.

### 6.5 Medical Professional Program Approaches

Across the segment, medical professional loan programs use one or more of the following approaches. The specific approach varies by lender. All of the following must be read with strong "varies by program" caveats.

#### 6.5.1 Income-Driven Repayment (IDR) Actual Payment

Many programs accept the borrower's actual IBR, PAYE, REPAYE, or SAVE payment as documented on the most recent student loan servicer statement, with confirmation of enrollment in the IDR plan. Examples cited in lender materials:

- Citizens Bank Doctor Loan: allows use of IBR payment.
- Fifth Third Bank: explicitly states student loans are not considered in DTI under specific tier conditions; at minimum, IBR payment is accepted.
- BMO Physicians' Mortgage Program: treats student loan payments flexibly with documented payment.
- Regions Doctor Loan: can exclude (with review) some deferred student loan payments from DTI.
- Truist Doctor Loan: allows flexible underwriting on student loans for medical professionals.

The borrower must provide a recent servicer statement (typically within 30 to 60 days of application) showing the IDR payment and confirming the borrower's enrollment in an IDR plan.

#### 6.5.2 Deferred Student Loan Exclusion During Training

Several programs exclude student loan payments from DTI when the borrower is in training (resident or fellow) and the loans are documented as deferred. The deferment must be documented to extend beyond a specific window relative to closing (commonly 12 months past closing). Examples:

- Regions Bank: excludes some deferred student loan debt from DTI with documentation.
- Citizens Bank: allows exclusion of student loans deferred greater than 12 months from closing date.
- Fifth Third Bank: established physicians need 12 months employed; student loans in deferment or forbearance for at least 12 months after closing are excluded from DTI calculations.
- First Horizon: cited as allowing student loans to be excluded from DTI under specific conditions.

This is the single highest-impact feature for a resident or fellow with high six-figure loans. Conventional underwriting will apply 1 percent of balance (Fannie) or 0.5 percent (Freddie) as a placeholder; FHA will apply 0.5 percent of balance; VA will apply the higher of actual payment or 5 percent divided by 12. A medical professional program that excludes the deferred loan entirely is materially more favorable for a resident with $250,000 to $400,000 in deferred loans.

#### 6.5.3 Income-Based Repayment Ramped Projection

A small number of programs accept a projected post-training IBR payment as the qualifying figure. This requires the borrower to provide the projected post-training income, the projected IDR plan, and a calculation of the projected payment. This is the least common approach and typically applies to specific resident-to-attending or fellow-to-attending transition scenarios.

#### 6.5.4 Fixed Percentage of Balance

Some programs use a fixed percentage of the outstanding balance as a hypothetical payment. Common percentages cited in lender materials: 0.5 percent, 1 percent. The choice of percentage materially affects the DTI math.

#### 6.5.5 Fully Amortized Standard Repayment

Some programs require the standard 10-year amortized payment regardless of the borrower's actual repayment plan. This is the least favorable approach for a borrower in IDR. Few of the major medical professional programs use this approach by default.

### 6.6 Documentation Requirements for Each Approach

- IDR documentation: most recent statement from the loan servicer showing the IDR payment, with confirmation that the borrower is enrolled in the IDR plan; recertification confirmations may also satisfy. Statements typically must be dated within 30 to 60 days of closing.
- Deferred loan documentation: most recent statement showing deferred status, with documented deferral end date that extends beyond the program's required window (commonly 12 months past closing).
- IBR projection (uncommon): borrower-provided projected post-training income and the corresponding payment calculation, often supported by the servicer's projection tool.
- Fixed percentage: statement of outstanding balance only.
- Fully amortized: most recent loan statement plus the program's amortization assumption.

### 6.7 PSLF and Loan Forgiveness Considerations

The Public Service Loan Forgiveness program forgives the remaining federal Direct Loan balance after 120 qualifying monthly payments while the borrower works full-time for a qualifying public service employer (501(c)(3) nonprofit, federal/state/local/tribal government, or contracted nonprofit physician roles in Texas and California per the 10-2022 Department of Education update).

Mortgage underwriting implications:

- The fact that a borrower is pursuing PSLF does not, by itself, change the loan's status during the qualifying period. The loan still exists. Underwriting therefore typically does not differ based on PSLF status.
- The borrower's chosen IDR plan (because PSLF requires repayment under a qualifying plan) does affect the DTI calculation, since the IDR payment is what gets used by lenders that accept IDR.
- A borrower expecting PSLF forgiveness in the near future (within 10 months) may benefit from Freddie Mac's payment-exclusion rule for debts with 10 months or fewer remaining, but this does not apply on Fannie's side unless the forgiveness is effective by closing.

The packet does not provide PSLF strategy advice. Borrowers with PSLF questions route to a financial advisor or specialized student loan counselor.

The 2025 to 2026 PSLF environment specifically:

- The Working Families Tax Cuts Act (referenced in some media as One Big Beautiful Bill Act provisions) preserved residency and fellowship time-counting toward PSLF for borrowers with loans disbursed before 07-01-2026, after AAFP-led pushback against an earlier proposal that would have excluded residency from PSLF.
- Effective 07-01-2026, the Department of Education has new authority under a final rule (published 10-31-2025) to revoke PSLF eligibility for employers found to have a "substantial illegal purpose." Employer eligibility for PSLF is therefore subject to a new layer of scrutiny.
- SAVE plan forbearance time does not count toward PSLF or IDR forgiveness. Borrowers who were in SAVE during the litigation period have lost potential forgiveness credit for that time and may pursue PSLF buyback after reaching 120 months of qualifying employment.

The mortgage takeaway: PSLF strategy is downstream of mortgage underwriting; the mortgage qualifies on the borrower's documented IDR or deferred status at application. Strategy questions about PSLF buyback, plan switching, or forgiveness timing route to the federal student loan servicer or a specialized counselor.

### 6.8 Refinanced Student Loans

Some borrowers refinance federal student loans into private student loans to obtain a lower rate or simpler payment structure. Mortgage implications:

- A refinanced loan has a fixed payment that some medical professional programs treat more favorably than an IDR payment with $0 reported.
- A refinanced loan loses access to IDR plans and PSLF. This is a significant trade-off that should not be made primarily for mortgage purposes.

Routing language: questions about whether to refinance federal student loans before a mortgage application route to a financial advisor or student loan counselor. The mortgage qualification analysis is one of several inputs to that decision; PSLF eligibility, future income trajectory, and total interest cost are typically more important.

### 6.9 Spouse and Co-Borrower Student Loans

- Where the spouse is on the loan, the spouse's student loans are included in the application's combined DTI.
- Where the spouse is not on the loan, the spouse's student loans are not included in DTI for the loan-applying spouse.
- The strategic question of whether to apply jointly or solo depends on income, credit, student loan profile, state property law (community property vs. separate property), and the specific lender's program rules. The decision routes to a CPA and financial advisor for tax and asset-protection implications. The mortgage analysis is one input, not the deciding factor.

### 6.10 The 2026 Federal Student Loan Transition (Critical Time-Sensitive Section)

This section will date most quickly. Verify against the Department of Education and major lender announcements at any specific borrower's pre-approval timing.

- 03-10-2026: 8th Circuit Court of Appeals permanently ended the SAVE Plan.
- 03-27-2026: U.S. Department of Education began transitioning the approximately 7.5 million SAVE-enrolled borrowers into legal repayment plans, with at least 90 days to choose a new plan.
- Through approximately 07-01-2026: borrowers transition to IBR, PAYE (sunset for new loans 07-01-2026), ICR (sunset 07-01-2028 for pre-07-01-2026 loans), or wait for RAP.
- 07-01-2026: Repayment Assistance Plan (RAP) launches as the new IDR plan for loans disbursed on or after 07-01-2026, alongside the new Tiered Standard Plan. RAP payments are 1 to 10 percent of adjusted gross income with a $10 minimum; up to 30 years to forgiveness.
- 07-01-2026: Grad PLUS loans eliminated for new borrowing. Direct Unsubsidized professional student loans capped at $50,000 per year and $200,000 lifetime for professional students (medical, dental, veterinary).
- 07-01-2028: PAYE and ICR sunset for loans disbursed before 07-01-2026. IBR remains an option.

Mortgage underwriting implications during the transition:

- Borrowers in SAVE forbearance during 2024 to 2026 had loans accruing interest from 08-01-2025 onward. Their DTI calculation under conventional, FHA, and VA rules used the placeholder (1 percent Fannie, 0.5 percent Freddie or FHA, 5 percent / 12 VA) rather than $0, even though no payment was due.
- Medical professional programs that allow IBR or deferred-loan exclusion based on specific documentation provided a path through this period.
- As borrowers transition out of SAVE in 2026, the documented monthly payment becomes available again. Mortgage underwriters will use the new documented payment.
- Borrowers timing a mortgage application around the SAVE-to-IBR transition should expect underwriters to want the most recent documentation showing the new plan, the new payment, and the new servicer-confirmed status.

The 2026 student loan landscape is the highest-volatility area in this packet. Refresh cadence for content built from this section: quarterly minimum, with re-checks tied to announcements from the Department of Education and major lender circulars.

## Section 7. Future-Income Qualification and the Offer Letter / Employment Contract Path

### 7.1 The Conventional Baseline

Fannie Mae Selling Guide B3-3.3-03 (Employment Offers or Contracts, dated 03-04-2026) governs conventional treatment of borrowers scheduled to begin new employment under the terms of an employment offer or contract. The lender may deliver the loan under one of two options:

- Option 1: Borrower has started employment by closing. The lender obtains a paystub from the borrower providing sufficient information supporting the income used to qualify based on the offer or contract before delivery of the loan.
- Option 2: Borrower has not started employment by closing. The lender qualifies based on a fully executed contract for future employment. The contract must clearly identify the employer and borrower and be signed by both, must clearly identify the terms of employment (position, type and rate of pay, start date), and must be non-contingent (if conditions of employment exist, the lender must confirm all conditions are satisfied prior to closing). This option applies only to one-unit, principal residence, purchase transactions.

Fannie Mae Selling Guide B3-3.1-04 (Verbal Verification of Employment) requires a verbal VOE within 10 business days prior to the note date for employment income, and within 120 calendar days prior to the note date for self-employment income. The verbal VOE confirms continued employment. A change in employment status close to closing must be re-evaluated.

Freddie Mac's treatment under its Single-Family Seller/Servicer Guide is parallel, with the structural difference that Freddie's framework references the same kinds of executed contracts and start-date documentation.

The conventional rule effectively allows pre-start-date qualification for a one-unit primary residence with a fully executed contract. The Fannie Option 2 path is functional for many medical buyers without requiring a medical professional program, especially where the borrower's other underwriting factors (DTI with student loans, credit, reserves) fit the agency channel cleanly.

### 7.2 The Medical Professional Program Approach

The medical professional segment universally accepts a signed offer letter or employment contract as the basis for income qualification before the start date. Common time windows:

- 60 days before start: most programs (Citizens Bank cites 60 days from closing within which residency must begin)
- 90 days before start: many programs (BMO requires evidence that employment will start within 90 days of loan closing; TD Bank cited 90 days; First Merchants 90 days; Truist 90 days; Bank of America commonly cited as up to 90 days)
- 120 days, 150 days, 6 months: select programs allow longer windows. First Horizon allows up to 6 months pre-start-date with a signed employment agreement.

Required contract elements (consistently across the segment):

- Signed by both employer and physician (employee)
- Stated start date
- Stated guaranteed compensation (base salary; if compensation is partly based on production or RVU, the guaranteed minimum is what the lender can typically count)
- Stated employment terms (position, type of pay, location)
- Non-contingent on the date of underwriting or closing (or with all contingencies satisfied)

Reserve requirement during the gap: many programs require additional reserves to cover the period between closing and the borrower's first paycheck (commonly 3 to 6 months of PITIA, sometimes more for residents and fellows with limited current income; covered in Section 8).

### 7.3 Documentation Requirements

- Fully executed offer letter or employment contract on employer letterhead, signed by both parties
- A written statement from the employer's HR or department confirming the contract is binding and the start date is firm; some programs accept a verbal HR confirmation captured in the lender's notes
- Verbal Verification of Employment (VVOE) close to closing per the lender's policy (Fannie's standard is 10 business days prior to note date; medical professional programs typically follow a similar window)
- Resident or fellow programs: appointment letter from the residency or fellowship program on program letterhead, with start date and stipend
- For 1099 contract physicians: signed contract, two years of 1099s and tax returns commonly required (some programs are more flexible per the Section 4 self-employment notes)
- For self-employed private practice: two years of personal and business tax returns, year-to-date P&L and balance sheet, business license, articles of incorporation or LLC operating agreement

### 7.4 Contract Language That Triggers Scrutiny

- "At-will" employment clauses: universally present in U.S. employment contracts; lenders typically accept these without issue, but may request additional documentation in some scenarios.
- Productivity bonus structures with no guaranteed minimum: lenders typically can only count the guaranteed portion of compensation. A contract with high RVU upside but no guaranteed minimum bonus will qualify on the base salary alone in most cases.
- Clawback clauses on signing bonuses: affect how the bonus is treated. Where the bonus is repayable on early termination, lenders typically cannot count it as qualifying income.
- Non-compete clauses: rarely affect mortgage underwriting directly; may indirectly affect future job mobility but do not affect current qualification.
- Probationary periods: some lenders require waiting until probation completes before counting the income. Other lenders accept the contract on its face.
- Conditions precedent (such as completion of credentialing, license activation, drug screen, background check): lenders typically require all conditions to be satisfied before closing. A pending condition can delay closing or require a documented confirmation from HR.

### 7.5 Common Pitfalls

- Closing too far in advance of the start date: the lender's window may not accommodate, or the gap reserve requirement increases dramatically.
- Closing with an unsigned letter of intent (LOI) rather than an executed contract: not acceptable.
- Closing with a contract that lacks required elements (start date, compensation, signatures): not acceptable.
- A start-date change between contract signing and closing: requires a re-signed or amended contract and re-documentation.
- A change in employment terms (compensation, location, role) between contract signing and closing: requires re-underwriting.
- Employer pushes back the start date due to credentialing or licensing delays: common; the lender's window may not accommodate. Where the new start date pushes outside the program's window, the borrower may need to switch programs or postpone closing.

## Section 8. Buying Before the Start Date: The Practical Mechanics

### 8.1 The Cash Flow Gap

For a medical buyer closing before their start date, three cash flow questions converge:

- Cash to close (down payment plus closing costs plus prepaids)
- Reserve requirement (covered in Section 9; typically 3 to 6 months of PITIA, sometimes more for pre-start-date scenarios)
- The gap between closing and first paycheck (pay periods at the new employer plus typical lag for new-hire payroll setup; commonly 30 to 60 days from start date to first paycheck, sometimes longer)

Conservative modeling at the start of the search:

- Assume 60 to 90 days between closing and first paycheck (the start date plus the first full pay cycle plus payroll setup lag)
- Assume the borrower makes mortgage payment(s) and household expense payments out of reserves during the gap
- Assume zero income arrives during the gap

### 8.2 Funding the Gap

Sources commonly used to bridge the gap:

- Personal savings (cleanest, no DTI impact, most preferred by lenders)
- Signing bonus paid before start date (varies by employer; if paid before closing, the funds must be sourced and seasoned per the lender's documentation rules; the signing bonus itself typically cannot be counted as qualifying income on its own per Section 7)
- Relocation package (lump sum, reimbursement, or gross-up; tax implications discussed in Section 9 and 16)
- Family gift funds (universally allowed in the segment with documentation per Section 9)
- Bridge loan from prior residence sale (some programs allow; the bridge payment becomes a debt obligation)
- HELOC on prior residence (subject to the HELOC's own qualification; the HELOC payment becomes a DTI item)
- 401(k) loan (covered in Section 9; many medical professional programs include the 401(k) loan payment in DTI, which is different from the agency rule that often excludes it)
- Personal lines of credit (DTI impact applies; the payment is included)

### 8.3 Timing the Move

- 30 days before start date: most common; fits within most lender windows; modest gap reserve requirement
- 60 days before start date: allowed by many programs; moderate gap reserve requirement
- 90 days before start date: allowed by most major medical professional programs; higher gap reserve requirement
- 120-plus days, up to 6 months: select programs (First Horizon up to 6 months); highest gap reserve requirement
- Closing immediately after match for residents: typically structured as a resident program with the residency program appointment letter as the documentation basis

The role of temporary housing: in markets where pre-start-date closing isn't viable (because the start-date timing exceeds the program's window, or because the borrower needs to rent for credentialing or fellowship-to-attending transition), short-term rental or extended-stay arrangements bridge the gap. Some borrowers close on a primary residence after the move while paying short-term housing in the interim.

### 8.4 Spouse and Family Considerations

- One spouse arriving early to close and start: common for dual-medical households or for households where one spouse moves first to set up the home
- The other spouse remaining at the prior location during the transition: requires careful coordination of HOA, utilities, insurance, mail forwarding, and prior-residence sale or rental
- Children's school timing: a frequent driver of move timing. Mid-year school transitions vs. summer transitions affect the urgency of pre-start-date closing.
- Pet relocation: not directly mortgage-related but a real cost line for households with pets
- Spouse continuing income from prior location during transition: can be helpful for combined DTI; verify continuance of income under the lender's rules

### 8.5 Contract Physicians and Locum Roles

- W-2 contract physicians: typically eligible for medical professional programs on the same documentation basis as direct-employed physicians.
- 1099 contract physicians: varies by program. Some programs require two years of 1099 history; some accept a guaranteed-minimum 1099 contract with shorter history. BMO has been cited as accepting 1099 contract income for doctors with a guaranteed minimum salary even without a 2-year established 1099 history, provided the borrower is actively seeing patients.
- Locum-only physicians: typically excluded from medical professional programs; some programs require permanent placement before qualification.
- Travel nurse and locum CRNA dynamics: program-specific. The travel-pay structure and the per-diem treatment under tax law can complicate the income documentation.

### 8.6 Career Transition Scenarios

- Resident to fellow (continuing training): same lender continuity often available; the new fellowship appointment letter replaces the residency appointment letter as the income documentation.
- Fellow to attending (the major transition): the new attending contract qualifies the loan; this is the highest-impact transition because attending compensation is typically 3 to 5 times resident or fellow compensation.
- Attending changing jobs: new contract qualification; verbal VOE from the new employer.
- Employed physician transitioning to private practice ownership: the most complex scenario. Self-employment income documentation challenges apply (typically two years of business and personal tax returns, P&L, balance sheet). Some programs make exceptions for physicians who joined an established practice with a guaranteed minimum (treated as W-2 or 1099 with specific documentation).
- Academic physician moving to private practice: similar to attending changing jobs; the academic salary is replaced with private practice compensation documentation.
- Private practice physician selling into a hospital system: the sale event creates one-time income that lenders typically do not count as qualifying income; the resulting employment contract is what qualifies the loan.

## Section 9. Reserve, Asset, and Documentation Requirements

### 9.1 Common Reserve Requirements

Range across the segment: 2 months to 12 months of PITIA (Principal, Interest, Taxes, Insurance, plus HOA where applicable). The specific requirement varies by:

- Loan amount (higher loan amounts typically require more reserves)
- Borrower stage (residents and fellows typically require more reserves)
- Pre-start-date status (closing pre-start-date typically increases the reserve requirement to cover the gap)
- Program (some programs require less, some require more)

Common patterns:

- 2 to 3 months: well-qualified attending physician, post-start-date, lower loan amount
- 6 months: common median across the segment for pre-start-date scenarios
- 9 to 12 months: pre-start-date scenarios at higher loan amounts, residents and fellows, programs with more conservative overlay

The trade-off: cash going to down payment vs. cash held in reserves. Borrowers commonly want to put more cash to down payment to reduce the loan amount and monthly payment; lenders want enough cash held to cover unexpected gaps.

### 9.2 What Counts as Reserves

- Checking and savings: 100 percent of balance (after subtracting earnest money already paid and any pending wires)
- Money market accounts: 100 percent of balance
- Brokerage and non-retirement investment accounts: typically 60 to 80 percent of value (programs vary; the discount accounts for market volatility and liquidation cost)
- Retirement accounts (401(k), 403(b), IRA, pension): typically 60 to 70 percent of vested balance (programs vary; some programs do not count retirement at all)
- Pension and 403(b) for academic physicians: counted under retirement rules
- HSA balances: typically not counted as reserves; the funds are the borrower's but earmarked for healthcare expenses
- Restricted Stock Units (RSUs): relevant for physicians with biotech, pharma, or hospital system equity; typically counted at vested portion only and discounted
- Crypto: varies wildly; many lenders exclude entirely; some count after liquidation to a documented cash account
- Family-trust assets: vary by trust accessibility; revocable trusts where the borrower is grantor or trustee with full access typically count

### 9.3 Asset Documentation Window

- Standard 60-day asset documentation window
- Two months of full-page bank statements (all pages, including blank pages, with account numbers visible)
- Year-to-date documentation for accounts where the most recent full statement is more than 60 days old (typically a screen-capture or supplemental letter)
- Sourcing of large deposits (any deposit greater than 50 percent of monthly income, per common Fannie Mae standard, must be sourced)
- Earnest money source: the EMD must be documented from the borrower's seasoned account; transfers between borrower accounts in the documentation window must be paper-trailed

### 9.4 Down Payment Sources

- Personal savings (most common, cleanest documentation)
- Signing bonus (when paid before closing): the funds must be deposited and sourced; some programs require the bonus to be in the borrower's account for the documentation window
- Family gift funds (universally allowed in the segment): requires a gift letter signed by donor, donor's relationship to borrower, statement that no repayment is expected, and documentation of the donor's ability to give (donor's bank statement showing the gift funds were available)
- Sale of prior residence: requires settlement statement (HUD-1 or Closing Disclosure for the sale) showing the borrower's net proceeds; if the sale closes before the new purchase, the proceeds must be sourced; if simultaneous closings, the proceeds flow at closing
- Liquidated investments: requires sale documentation (brokerage statement showing the sale and the deposit into the borrower's bank account)
- Tuition refunds or stipend savings (residents): documented with the residency program letter or paystub history; may have specific seasoning requirements
- 401(k) loan: many medical professional programs allow 401(k) loans for cash to close. The 401(k) loan payment then enters DTI in most medical professional programs. This is a difference from the agency rule, which often excludes 401(k) loan payments from DTI under the rationale that the borrower is paying themselves back. Verify the specific lender's treatment of 401(k) loans.

### 9.5 Income Documentation

- W-2 attending (post-start-date): most recent two pay stubs, two years of W-2s, employment verification (verbal VOE within 10 business days of note date)
- W-2 attending (pre-start-date): signed and fully executed offer letter or employment contract, departmental verification of start date, prior-employer documentation if applicable, no current paystubs required
- Resident and fellow: program appointment letter on residency or fellowship program letterhead, current pay stubs from stipend, residency program contact information
- 1099 contract physician: signed contract, two years of 1099s and tax returns (some programs more flexible per Section 4)
- Self-employed and private practice: two years of personal tax returns and business tax returns, year-to-date P&L, year-to-date balance sheet, business license, articles of incorporation or LLC operating agreement
- Moonlighting income: documentation of regular vs. irregular moonlighting; many lenders require a two-year history before counting moonlighting; some lenders do not count moonlighting at all
- Bonus and RVU income: employer documentation of the structure, history of receipt where possible; for guaranteed bonuses, the contract typically suffices; for production-based bonuses, two years of history is commonly required

### 9.6 Credit Documentation

- Standard tri-merge credit report (Equifax, Experian, TransUnion) pulled by the lender
- Resident thin-file dynamics: residency stipends often do not generate strong tradeline depth. A resident with limited credit history can still qualify if the credit score itself meets the program floor (commonly 680 to 720) and the file is otherwise clean.
- Authorized-user tradelines: allowed with documentation; the underwriter may require evidence that the borrower is using the tradeline (not just listed as an authorized user)
- Recent credit events: medical professional programs typically follow standard residency and seasoning rules (4 years post-bankruptcy for most programs; 4 years post-foreclosure; specific seasoning periods for short sale, deed-in-lieu, and other events; verify with each program)

## Section 10. Medical Professional Loan vs. Conventional vs. FHA vs. VA vs. Jumbo

### 10.1 The Comparison Framework

A structured comparison the writer can convert into a table on a borrower-facing page. For each loan type, the comparison axes are:

- Down payment requirement
- Loan amount ceiling (or lack thereof)
- Mortgage insurance / PMI / MIP / funding fee
- Student loan treatment (Section 6)
- Future-income / pre-start-date qualification (Section 7)
- DTI ceiling
- Credit floor
- Reserve requirements
- Property type allowances
- Refinance availability
- Rate level (general direction; no specific quotes)
- Long-term cost over 5, 10, 30 years

#### 10.1.1 Conventional (Fannie Mae / Freddie Mac)

- Down payment: 3 percent minimum (HomeReady, Home Possible, HomeOne); 5 percent typical; 10 to 20 percent for jumbo-conforming high-balance loans in many counties.
- Loan amount ceiling: 2026 baseline conforming $832,750; high-cost ceiling $1,249,125; baseline 2-unit $1,066,000, 3-unit $1,288,875, 4-unit $1,601,450 (FHFA).
- Mortgage insurance: PMI required when LTV exceeds 80 percent; cancellable at 80 percent LTV (request) or 78 percent LTV (automatic) under HPA.
- Student loan treatment: Fannie B3-6-05 rules (1 percent of balance fallback, IDR payment with documentation, fully amortizing payment); Freddie similar with 0.5 percent fallback.
- Future-income / pre-start-date qualification: Fannie B3-3.3-03 allows pre-start-date qualification on a fully executed contract for one-unit primary residence purchase; Freddie parallel.
- DTI ceiling: 45 to 50 percent typical; AUS-driven; HomeReady allows up to 50 percent.
- Credit floor: 620 typical for Fannie; 660 to 700 typical for jumbo-conforming high-balance.
- Reserve requirements: 0 to 6 months typical; varies by AUS findings.
- Property type allowances: 1- to 4-unit primary residence, second homes, investment property all eligible under separate rules.
- Refinance availability: rate-and-term and cash-out both available.
- Rate level: typically the lowest rates in the conforming market.
- Long-term cost: lowest interest rate combined with PMI cost; PMI drops off at 80 percent or 78 percent LTV.

#### 10.1.2 FHA

- Down payment: 3.5 percent for credit scores 580 and above; 10 percent for 500 to 579.
- Loan amount ceiling: county-specific; FHA loan limits set by HUD annually; lower than conforming in most counties.
- Mortgage insurance: Up-Front Mortgage Insurance Premium (UFMIP) of 1.75 percent at closing; annual MIP for the life of the loan in most cases (cancels only when LTV at origination is 90 percent or less, after 11 years).
- Student loan treatment: HUD 4000.1 and ML 2021-13; actual payment from credit report or 0.5 percent of balance placeholder when payment is $0 or unknown.
- Future-income / pre-start-date qualification: HUD 4000.1 II.A.4.c.iii allows pre-start-date qualification under specific documentation; less flexible than the medical professional segment.
- DTI ceiling: 43 percent typical; up to 56.99 percent with strong compensating factors and AUS approval.
- Credit floor: 580 (some lenders apply 620 to 640 overlay).
- Reserve requirements: 1 month typical for 1-2 unit; 3 months for 3-4 unit.
- Property type allowances: 1- to 4-unit primary residence; condo eligibility requires FHA project approval.
- Refinance availability: rate-and-term, cash-out, and FHA Streamline (no appraisal) available.
- Rate level: similar to or modestly above conventional.
- Long-term cost: low rate, but lifetime MIP is a significant ongoing cost; total cost over 30 years often exceeds conventional with PMI dropping off.

#### 10.1.3 VA

- Down payment: $0 for full entitlement borrowers; full entitlement under post-2020 rules (Blue Water Navy Vietnam Veterans Act of 2019) allows zero-down at any loan amount.
- Loan amount ceiling: no VA limit for full-entitlement borrowers; reduced-entitlement borrowers limited to county conforming loan limit for guaranty purposes.
- Mortgage insurance: no PMI; VA funding fee 0.5 to 3.6 percent depending on use of entitlement, down payment, and service type; exempt for service-connected disability rated borrowers.
- Student loan treatment: VA Pamphlet 26-7 Chapter 4, VA Circular 26-17-02; deferred 12-plus months excluded; otherwise the higher of actual payment or 5 percent of balance / 12.
- Future-income / pre-start-date qualification: VA pamphlet 26-7 allows future income under specific documentation; framework parallel to conventional.
- DTI ceiling: 41 percent baseline; higher allowed with strong residual income.
- Credit floor: no VA minimum; lender overlays typically 580 to 620.
- Reserve requirements: residual income standard varies by region and family size; reserves not required in dollar terms beyond residual.
- Property type allowances: 1- to 4-unit primary residence; condo requires VA approval.
- Refinance availability: rate-and-term, cash-out, and VA IRRRL (Interest Rate Reduction Refinance Loan, no appraisal in many cases) available.
- Rate level: typically below or comparable to conventional.
- Long-term cost: zero down with no PMI is structurally favorable for full-entitlement borrowers; funding fee adds upfront cost (financeable into loan).

#### 10.1.4 Medical Professional (Lender-Specific Portfolio)

- Down payment: 0 percent typical at lower tiers; 5 percent at mid; 10 percent or more at higher (per Section 5).
- Loan amount ceiling: $1 million to $3.5 million depending on program (per Section 5).
- Mortgage insurance: no PMI universally where the program covers the loan amount tier (per Section 5).
- Student loan treatment: program-specific; commonly more favorable than conventional baseline (per Section 6).
- Future-income / pre-start-date qualification: 30 to 180 days pre-start-date; signed and fully executed contract required (per Section 7).
- DTI ceiling: 43 to 50 percent typical (per Section 5.7).
- Credit floor: 680 to 740 typical (per Section 5.7).
- Reserve requirements: 2 to 12 months PITIA (per Section 9.1).
- Property type allowances: primary residence almost always; second home and investment varies (per Section 5.4).
- Refinance availability: rate-and-term yes; cash-out varies; construction-to-permanent select programs (per Section 5.6).
- Rate level: typically modestly above conventional to compensate for absence of MI and absence of agency take-out.
- Long-term cost: depends on the rate spread, the time horizon, and the alternative cost of waiting to save a 20 percent down payment.

#### 10.1.5 Jumbo (Non-Agency, Non-Medical-Professional)

- Down payment: typically 10 to 20 percent (some programs to 5 percent for premier borrowers); above the conforming loan limit.
- Loan amount ceiling: program-specific; commonly $5 million, $10 million, higher.
- Mortgage insurance: typically not required at 80 percent LTV or below; PMI may apply above 80 percent depending on lender.
- Student loan treatment: lender-specific; commonly conservative; some lenders apply 1 percent of balance fallback where payment is unknown.
- Future-income / pre-start-date qualification: lender-specific; fewer programs offer pre-start-date qualification.
- DTI ceiling: 43 percent typical; some programs up to 50 percent with overlays.
- Credit floor: 700 to 740 typical.
- Reserve requirements: 6 to 24 months PITIA typical.
- Property type allowances: 1- to 4-unit primary residence, second homes, investment property under separate rules.
- Refinance availability: rate-and-term, cash-out available.
- Rate level: typically modestly above conforming, sometimes below conforming when the lender is competing for high-balance loans.
- Long-term cost: depends on the rate spread; a borrower with 25 to 30 percent down and strong credit may find jumbo cheaper than a low-down-payment medical professional program at the same loan amount.

### 10.2 When Conventional May Still Be Better

- When the medical professional has 20 percent or more down (no PMI either way; rate may be lower on conventional)
- When the loan amount is well within conforming limits and student loan treatment doesn't constrain the DTI math
- When the borrower has substantial post-training W-2 history and student loans on standard repayment with documented payments
- When the spouse's income carries qualification regardless of student loan treatment
- When the conventional-to-medical-professional rate spread is meaningful enough to outweigh the medical program features

### 10.3 When FHA May Still Be Better

- When credit profile sits below medical professional program floors (residents with thin files who still need an MI-eligible product)
- When loan amount fits within FHA limits and the medical professional program would price worse net of UFMIP and ongoing MIP
- When down payment is genuinely 3.5 percent and the medical program's zero-down pricing is worse net-of-MIP

The FHA ongoing MIP for the life of the loan in most cases is a significant offsetting cost that often makes FHA less attractive than the medical professional program when both are available. The FHA is a backstop, not typically a preferred path for a medical buyer with a clean profile and access to a medical professional program.

### 10.4 When VA May Still Be Better

- Military physicians (active-duty, veterans, or eligible Reserve / Guard) eligible for VA. This includes military veterinarians, military dentists, military pharmacists, military CRNAs, and military nurse practitioners or physician assistants where the borrower has VA eligibility from prior service.
- Full entitlement allows zero-down at any loan amount under post-2020 rules.
- Funding fee exemption for service-connected disability rated borrowers (a meaningful upfront savings).
- The VA-vs.-medical-professional decision for military medical professionals is rare but high-value when present. Compare the funding fee to the rate differential and the program differences.

### 10.5 When Jumbo May Be the Better Path

- When loan amount exceeds the medical professional program's ceiling ($3 million in many programs, $3.5 million at KeyBank, $5 million in select programs)
- When the medical professional program's available terms at the high tier don't beat jumbo pricing
- When the borrower's compensation profile and assets meet jumbo requirements without needing the medical program features

A separate jumbo mechanics file (cross-reference) covers the broader jumbo segment.

### 10.6 Long-Term Cost Modeling (Illustrative Worked Example Framework)

The model the writer can convert to a worked example: same borrower, same property, same loan amount, modeled across loan types.

Example inputs (illustrative):

- Borrower: PGY-7 fellow transitioning to attending in 60 days; $250,000 student loans on IBR with documented $0 payment; salary $375,000 in attending contract; FICO 740; $80,000 in liquid assets after closing
- Property: $750,000 primary residence in an Alabama suburb; property tax $7,500/year; homeowner's insurance $1,800/year
- Loan amount targets: $750,000 zero down, $675,000 with 10 percent down, $600,000 with 20 percent down

Comparison axes for the worked example:

- Down payment required
- Loan amount
- Estimated rate (general direction; no specific quotes)
- Estimated monthly P&I plus T&I (and PMI or MIP where applicable)
- Cash to close
- Reserve requirement after cash to close
- Total interest paid over 5 years, 10 years, 30 years

The output for each loan type:

- Conventional 5 percent down: $712,500 loan, plus PMI; rate slightly below medical professional; cancellable PMI at 78 percent LTV
- Conventional 10 percent down: $675,000 loan, with PMI but cheaper than at 5 percent down
- Conventional 20 percent down: $600,000 loan, no PMI; lowest rate
- FHA 3.5 percent down: $723,750 loan, with UFMIP of 1.75 percent rolled into loan and lifetime MIP; rate similar to conventional
- VA zero down (if eligible): $750,000 loan, no MI, with funding fee 1.4 to 3.6 percent (exempt for SCD-rated borrowers); rate competitive with conventional
- Medical professional zero down: $750,000 loan, no PMI, rate modestly above conventional; reserves required (3 to 6 months PITIA pre-start-date if applicable)
- Medical professional 10 percent down: $675,000 loan, no PMI, rate slightly better than zero-down tier
- Jumbo 20 percent down: $600,000 loan, no PMI, rate competitive with conventional at this size (note: $600,000 is below conforming limit so jumbo doesn't strictly apply at this loan amount in this example)

The output framing: side-by-side comparison of monthly payment, cash to close, and total cost over a stated time horizon, presented as illustrative not predictive. The writer should never quote specific rates; the writer should describe the rate-direction trade-off and the structural cost trade-off.

The conclusion the writer can draw from a worked example: there is no universally best loan type. The best loan type for a specific borrower depends on the borrower's down payment, credit, student loan profile, target loan amount, time horizon in the home, and access to specific programs. A loan officer who can run all five comparison paths is more valuable to a medical buyer than a loan officer who only offers one path.

## Section 11. The Pre-Offer Planning Checklist (Synthesized)

This section pulls everything above into a sequential pre-offer checklist a medical professional can actually use. Structured as actionable line items by time horizon.

### 11.1 Six to Twelve Months Before Shopping

- Pull full credit report from all three bureaus (Annual Credit Report.com is the federally authorized free source) and review for accuracy; remediate errors via the bureaus' dispute processes
- Review credit utilization, recent inquiries, and tradeline depth; for residents and fellows with thin files, address gaps deliberately (avoid closing old accounts; consider responsible authorized-user tradelines if available)
- Begin organizing two years of tax returns (personal and business if applicable)
- Begin organizing W-2s, 1099s, K-1s
- Review the student loan portfolio: federal vs. private, repayment plan (IDR vs. standard), deferment status, current servicer, and most recent statement
- Run an IDR scenario with the federal student loan servicer if not currently in IDR; document the projected post-residency or post-fellowship payment under the borrower's anticipated plan
- Review retirement and brokerage account statements; identify which accounts could serve as reserves
- Identify whether private student loan refinance is even on the table; pause that decision pending mortgage qualification analysis and student loan strategy guidance from a financial advisor or specialized counselor
- Note that 2026 federal student loan transitions (SAVE termination, RAP launch 07-01-2026, PAYE/ICR sunset 07-01-2028) may affect the borrower's plan choice; route plan-choice questions to the federal student loan servicer or a student loan counselor

### 11.2 Three to Six Months Before Shopping

- Engage a loan officer for a planning conversation (not yet a formal pre-approval). The conversation maps the borrower's profile to two or three suitable lender programs.
- Identify two or three medical professional loan programs the borrower's profile fits. Verify profession, training-status window, geography, and target loan amount against each program's published guidelines.
- Identify whether the conventional, FHA, VA, or jumbo path competes with the medical program for this scenario. For VA-eligible military medical professionals, run the VA comparison. For borrowers with 20 percent or more down, run the conventional comparison.
- Identify the realistic loan amount range (not the maximum the lender will approve, but the amount that fits the borrower's financial plan).
- Identify the cash-to-close target and reserve target.
- Confirm the start date timing and the program's pre-start-date eligibility window.
- Avoid new credit, large purchases, or job changes from this point forward (changes during the underwriting window can re-trigger underwriting and delay or unwind the loan).
- Begin the asset seasoning clock; funds need a 60-day paper trail.

### 11.3 One to Three Months Before Shopping

- Complete a verified-income, verified-asset pre-approval through the chosen lender. A verified pre-approval is materially stronger in negotiation than a pre-qualification.
- Have the offer letter or employment contract fully executed (signed by both parties, with start date, compensation, and all required elements).
- Have departmental or HR confirmation of the start date in writing (a separate document or email; some lenders require this on the employer's letterhead).
- Confirm reserve target is met after cash-to-close is set aside.
- Confirm DTI math at the target purchase price using the lender's specific student loan treatment.
- Identify a buyer's agent with experience in the target submarket and price range.
- Verify property tax assumptions for the target submarket (Alabama Madison County context is in the companion local file).
- Verify homeowner's insurance availability and approximate cost (a rising concern in many markets due to insurer pullback; check for any coverage limits before writing the offer).

### 11.4 At Offer

- Pre-approval letter ready, current within the lender's freshness window (typically 60 to 90 days)
- Cash-to-close confirmed
- Earnest money source seasoned
- Realtor briefed on contingency strategy (financing, inspection, appraisal contingencies) and on the lender's required closing timeline
- Closing timeline negotiated to allow full underwriting (typically 30 to 45 days from acceptance to closing for medical professional programs; some programs are faster)
- Start-date timing confirmed in writing

### 11.5 Under Contract

- Order inspection promptly (typically within 7 to 10 days of contract acceptance)
- Order appraisal through the lender's AMC (the lender initiates this; the borrower does not order separately)
- Submit final documentation to underwriting promptly (the lender will request specific items; respond within 24 to 48 hours)
- Keep employment, credit, and assets stable through closing (do not change jobs, do not open new credit, do not move money between accounts without paper-trailing)
- Notify the lender immediately of any change in start date, compensation, role, or location
- Confirm wire instructions in person or by verified phone before sending closing funds (wire fraud is a real and increasing risk; never trust wire instructions received only by email)

## Section 12. Real Borrower Questions (AEO-Targeted)

This section surfaces questions in the borrower's own voice, paired with the underlying intent and the precise factual answer. Sources for the questions: Google "People Also Ask," Reddit (r/Residency, r/medicalschool, r/whitecoatinvestor, r/personalfinance), physician finance blogs, AAMC FIRST resources, and search-trend tools. Organized by topic area for retrieval. The questions are AEO-formatted (answer-first, specific, factual).

### 12.1 What Is a Medical Professional Mortgage / Physician Loan / Doctor Loan?

**Q: What is a physician loan?** A physician loan is a non-agency, lender-specific portfolio mortgage product designed for clinical professionals. It is held in portfolio by the originating bank rather than sold to Fannie Mae or Freddie Mac, which lets the lender offer features like low or zero down payment without private mortgage insurance, qualification on a signed employment contract before the start date, and student loan treatment that often exceeds agency rules. Every program detail varies by lender.

**Q: What is a medical professional mortgage?** "Medical professional mortgage" is a broader marketing term used by some lenders to signal that the program covers professions beyond MD and DO (such as dentists, veterinarians, podiatrists, optometrists, pharmacists, CRNAs, or physician assistants). The actual eligibility under any specific program is set by that lender's written guidelines, not by the marketing name.

**Q: Do I need to be a doctor to get a doctor loan?** No, but you must be in a profession that the specific lender's program covers. MD and DO are universally covered where the program covers physicians at all. Dentists (DDS, DMD), veterinarians (DVM), podiatrists (DPM), optometrists (OD), pharmacists (PharmD), CRNAs, and others are covered by some programs and excluded by others. Always verify your specific profession against the specific lender's published guidelines.

### 12.2 Eligibility by Profession and Status

**Q: Are residents eligible for physician loans?** Yes, residents in MD, DO, DDS, DMD, and several other tracks are eligible at most major medical professional programs. Residents typically qualify on the residency program appointment letter and current stipend pay stubs.

**Q: Are fellows eligible?** Yes, fellows are commonly eligible at programs that include residents. The fellowship appointment letter and current stipend documentation qualify the loan.

**Q: Are dentists eligible for physician loans?** Dentists holding DDS or DMD are commonly eligible at most programs marketed as "physician loans" or "doctor loans." Pediatric dentists, orthodontists, and oral surgeons fall under the DDS or DMD designation typically. Confirm with each lender.

**Q: Are CRNAs eligible?** CRNAs are eligible at some programs and excluded at others. Programs cited as accepting CRNA include First Horizon, First National Bank, Regions (under Emerging Professionals), Fifth Third, BMO at certain branches. Bank of America and Truist programs typically do not accept CRNAs as of recent disclosure. Verify directly.

**Q: Are pharmacists eligible?** Pharmacists holding PharmD are eligible at some programs (First Horizon, First Merchants, Regions Emerging Professionals, Fulton Mortgage, others). Many "doctor loan" programs do not include pharmacists. Verify directly.

**Q: Are veterinarians eligible?** Veterinarians holding DVM are eligible at many programs (Fifth Third, Huntington, First National Bank, Regions Emerging Professionals, others). Some "physician loan" programs do not include veterinarians. Verify directly.

**Q: Are nurse practitioners or physician assistants eligible?** NP and PA are eligible at fewer programs than MD/DO. Regions Emerging Professionals covers NP and PA with no more than 7 years post-licensure. First National Bank cites NP and PA eligibility. Most "physician loan" programs do not include NP or PA. Verify directly.

**Q: Can I get a physician loan if I'm on an H-1B or J-1 visa?** Some programs accept eligible visa holders (H-1B, J-1 with HSC waiver, O-1, EB-2). Eligibility varies by lender and by visa type. Citizenship or permanent residency is the most common requirement. International medical graduates should compare two or three programs to find one that accepts the borrower's specific visa status.

**Q: Can I do a physician loan if I'm starting a private practice?** Some programs allow private practice physicians, but typically with additional documentation: two years of personal and business tax returns, year-to-date P&L, balance sheet, and business license. New private practice owners with less than two years of self-employed history may face restrictions. Some programs make exceptions for physicians joining established practices with guaranteed-minimum compensation.

### 12.3 Pre-Start-Date and Future Income

**Q: Can I buy a house before I start my new job?** Yes, most medical professional programs allow purchase before the borrower's employment start date. The window varies: 30 days (most common minimum), 60 days, 90 days (BMO, Truist, TD Bank, others), up to 6 months (First Horizon). The program must allow it; the contract must be fully executed; and reserves typically must cover the gap to first paycheck.

**Q: How early before my start date can I close?** The earliest is determined by the specific lender's program window. 30 to 90 days is the most common range. First Horizon allows up to 6 months. BMO requires the start to occur within 90 days of closing.

**Q: What documents do I need to qualify on a future job offer?** A signed and fully executed employment contract or offer letter on the employer's letterhead, signed by both the employer and the borrower, with stated start date, stated compensation (base salary at minimum; guaranteed bonus where applicable), stated employment terms (position, location), and either non-contingent or with all conditions documented as satisfied. Plus a written confirmation from the employer's HR or department of the start date being firm.

**Q: Can I use my signing bonus for the down payment?** If the signing bonus is paid before closing and the funds are deposited and seasoned in the borrower's account, yes. The bonus itself typically cannot be counted as qualifying income for DTI purposes; it is a one-time payment. The contract's guaranteed compensation is what qualifies the loan.

### 12.4 Student Loan Treatment

**Q: How are my student loans treated on a physician loan?** Treatment varies by lender. Common approaches: actual IBR/PAYE/REPAYE/SAVE/IBR payment from the servicer statement; deferred-loan exclusion when documented as deferred for at least 12 months past closing; fixed percentage of balance (often 0.5 to 1 percent); or fully amortized standard payment. The most favorable approach for a borrower in IDR or active deferment is the actual-payment or deferred-exclusion approach.

**Q: How are IBR student loan payments treated?** Most medical professional programs accept the borrower's actual IBR (or PAYE, SAVE while it existed, REPAYE) payment as documented on the servicer statement, with confirmation of enrollment in the IDR plan. Conventional rules also accept the documented IBR payment with similar documentation. FHA does not accept $0 IBR; FHA uses 0.5 percent of balance as a placeholder when the payment is $0.

**Q: What if my student loans are deferred?** Medical professional programs vary. Some programs exclude deferred loans entirely from DTI when the deferment extends at least 12 months past closing (Citizens, Fifth Third, Regions cited examples). Conventional programs apply the placeholder calculation (1 percent Fannie, 0.5 percent Freddie). FHA applies 0.5 percent of balance. VA excludes loans deferred at least 12 months past closing.

**Q: Will the SAVE plan situation affect my mortgage qualification?** Yes, if your loans were in SAVE forbearance during the 2024 to 2026 litigation period, your most recent documented payment is what mortgage underwriters use. As of 03-2026, the Department of Education is transitioning SAVE-enrolled borrowers into legal repayment plans (IBR, ICR, the standard plan, or in some cases waiting until RAP launches 07-01-2026). Get your most recent IDR plan re-enrollment confirmation and servicer statement before applying for a mortgage, and provide it to your lender. Loan officers in the medical professional segment are accustomed to navigating IDR documentation and can guide you on what specific documentation each program requires.

**Q: Can I refinance my federal student loans before the mortgage to lower my DTI?** Sometimes, but not without trade-offs. Refinancing federal student loans into private loans loses access to IDR plans and PSLF eligibility. The mortgage qualification benefit is one input to that decision, not the deciding factor. Route this question to a financial advisor or specialized student loan counselor before acting.

### 12.5 Down Payment, PMI, Loan Amount

**Q: Do I need a down payment for a physician loan?** No down payment is required at the lower loan amount tier of most programs (commonly up to $750,000 to $1.5 million depending on the program). Higher loan amounts require 5 to 10 percent or more down. Some lenders are tougher; verify with each program.

**Q: Is there PMI on a physician loan?** No. Programs in this segment universally waive PMI. The rate is typically modestly higher than conventional with PMI to compensate for the absence of MI.

**Q: How much can I borrow with a physician loan?** Loan amount ceilings vary by program: $1 million (some small programs), $1.5 million, $2 million, $2.5 million, $3 million, up to $3.5 million at KeyBank, with select programs reaching $5 million or higher.

**Q: Are physician loan rates higher than conventional?** Often, but not always, and the difference is rarely large. Programs price their rates to compete with conventional plus PMI cost on a low-down conventional loan. The all-in monthly cost (rate plus MI) is typically the right comparison, not rate alone.

**Q: Are there any prepayment penalties on a physician loan?** Most programs are no-prepay. Verify the specific lender's note terms.

### 12.6 Property and Use

**Q: Can I use the physician loan for a second home?** Most programs are primary residence only. KeyBank explicitly allows second homes. A few other programs allow second homes under specific conditions. Verify with each program.

**Q: Can I use a physician loan for an investment property?** Typically not. Most programs are primary residence only. Some programs allow rental conversion of a former primary residence after a seasoning period.

**Q: Can I use a physician loan to build a custom home?** Some programs offer construction-to-permanent financing for medical professionals (Citizens, First Citizens, others). Most "doctor loan" programs are purchase or refinance only.

**Q: Can I refinance my physician loan later?** Yes, you can refinance into another loan (conventional, jumbo, or another medical professional program). Check whether the new program requires private mortgage insurance based on the current LTV.

### 12.7 Comparison Questions

**Q: Should I use a physician loan or a conventional loan?** It depends on your down payment, your student loan profile, your credit, and the rate spread. With 20 percent or more down and clean documented student loan payments, conventional often wins on rate. With less than 10 percent down, no PMI, and constraint from student loans, the medical professional path often wins net of MI cost. Run both at pre-approval.

**Q: Should I use a physician loan or a VA loan?** If you're VA-eligible (military physician, military veterinarian, military dentist, or have prior service that establishes VA eligibility), the VA loan is structurally favorable: zero down, no PMI, no funding fee for service-connected disability rated borrowers. Compare the VA path to the medical professional program at pre-approval. The VA student loan rule (deferred 12-plus months excluded; otherwise higher of actual or 5 percent / 12) and the residual income standard differ from the medical professional program.

**Q: How does the physician loan compare to a jumbo loan?** Jumbo is non-agency like the medical professional loan, but jumbo is typically priced for borrowers with substantial down payment (10 to 30 percent) and strong reserves. Medical professional loans price for low-down-payment borrowers with strong income trajectories. A borrower with 25 percent down at a $1.5 million purchase may find jumbo cheaper; a borrower with 5 percent down at the same purchase will find medical professional cheaper net of MI cost.

**Q: What credit score do I need for a physician loan?** 680 to 740 minimum depending on the program. First Horizon is among the most flexible at 670 minimum. Most programs are 700 to 720. Bank of America, Truist, BMO, Citizens, Fifth Third typically are 700 plus.

**Q: What is the maximum DTI for a physician loan?** 43 to 50 percent across the segment; 45 percent is the most common ceiling. Truist allows up to 50 percent in specific situations. Huntington allows up to 50 percent. The DTI math is heavily affected by student loan treatment, which is the high-leverage variable.

### 12.8 Practical / Mechanical

**Q: How long does a physician loan take to close?** 30 to 45 days from contract acceptance is typical. Some programs are faster (20 to 30 days). The pre-start-date and student-loan-documentation requirements can extend the timeline.

**Q: What happens if my start date changes after I lock my loan?** The loan must be re-evaluated. If the new start date stays within the program's window, the loan typically proceeds with re-documentation. If it pushes outside the window, the loan may need to be re-priced, switched to a different program, or postponed.

**Q: Can my parents help with the down payment?** Yes, family gift funds are universally allowed in this segment. Required: a gift letter signed by the donor, stating the donor's relationship to the borrower, that the funds are a gift not a loan, and documentation of the donor's ability (donor's bank statement showing the funds were available).

**Q: Will my recent move or job change hurt my qualification?** It can. The verbal VOE close to closing confirms continued employment. A change in employment between application and closing requires re-evaluation. The stronger the documentation of the employment continuity, the smoother the process.

## Section 13. Suggested FAQ Questions and Answers (FAQPage Schema-Ready)

The following FAQ entries are written in answer-first AEO format, ready to drop into Schema.org FAQPage markup. Organized by topic area.

### 13.1 What and Why

**Q: What is a medical professional mortgage?** A medical professional mortgage is a non-agency, lender-specific portfolio mortgage product designed for clinical professionals (physicians, dentists, veterinarians, and others depending on the specific lender's program). It typically allows zero or low down payment without private mortgage insurance, qualification on a signed employment contract before the start date, and more flexible student loan treatment than conventional underwriting. Eligible professions, loan amount tiers, and other terms vary by lender.

**Q: Why do these programs exist?** Lenders developed medical professional mortgage programs because clinical professionals have predictable long-term income trajectories, low default rates relative to their typical debt levels, and high banking-relationship value. The programs accommodate the realities of clinical training: extended income launch, substantial student loan debt, and frequent relocation tied to residency, fellowship, and attending transitions.

### 13.2 Eligibility

**Q: Which professions qualify for a physician loan?** Eligibility varies by lender. MDs and DOs are universally included where the program covers physicians. Dentists (DDS, DMD), podiatrists (DPM), veterinarians (DVM), and optometrists (OD) are commonly included. Pharmacists, CRNAs, nurse practitioners, physician assistants, and physical therapists are included by some programs. Always verify your specific profession against the specific lender's program guidelines.

**Q: Do residents qualify?** Yes, most medical professional programs include MD, DO, DDS, and DMD residents. Documentation typically includes the residency program appointment letter and current stipend pay stubs.

**Q: Do fellows qualify?** Yes, fellows are commonly eligible at programs that include residents, with the fellowship appointment letter as the documentation basis.

**Q: Do international medical graduates on visas qualify?** Some programs accept H-1B, J-1 with HSC waiver, O-1, and EB-2 visa holders. Eligibility varies. International medical graduates should verify each program's visa policy directly.

### 13.3 Down Payment and Cost

**Q: Can I buy with no money down?** Many programs offer zero-down financing at the lower loan amount tier (commonly up to $750,000 to $1.5 million). Higher loan amounts require 5 to 10 percent or more down. The specific tiers vary by lender.

**Q: Do I have to pay private mortgage insurance?** No. Programs in this segment universally waive PMI even at high LTV. The rate may be modestly higher than a conventional loan with PMI to compensate for the absence of mortgage insurance.

**Q: How much can I borrow?** Loan amount ceilings range from $1 million to $3.5 million at major lenders, with select programs reaching higher. The ceiling correlates with the lender's appetite for portfolio risk in the high-balance space and the borrower's profession, training stage, and credit profile.

**Q: Are physician loan rates higher than conventional?** Sometimes; often by a small margin. The full cost comparison includes the rate, the PMI cost on a low-down-payment conventional loan, the loan size, and the borrower's time horizon. Run both at pre-approval.

### 13.4 Pre-Start-Date

**Q: Can I close on a home before I start my new job?** Yes, at most major medical professional programs. The window varies: 30 to 90 days is most common, with select programs allowing up to 6 months. The contract must be fully executed and the lender's pre-start-date documentation requirements must be met.

**Q: What contract documentation do I need?** A fully executed employment contract or offer letter signed by both the employer and the borrower, with start date, compensation, and employment terms clearly stated. Plus a written confirmation from the employer's HR or department of the start date being firm.

**Q: Can I use my signing bonus toward the down payment?** Yes, if the signing bonus is paid before closing and the funds are deposited and seasoned in your account. The bonus itself typically does not count as qualifying income; the contract's guaranteed compensation is what qualifies.

### 13.5 Student Loans

**Q: How are student loans treated on a physician loan?** Treatment varies by lender. Common approaches include using the documented Income-Driven Repayment payment, excluding deferred loans documented as deferred for at least 12 months past closing, or applying a fixed percentage of balance (often 0.5 percent) as a placeholder. The medical professional segment is typically more flexible than conventional, FHA, or VA underwriting on $0 IDR or deferred loans.

**Q: Will my deferred student loans count against me?** They may or may not, depending on the program. Some medical professional programs exclude deferred loans entirely from DTI when the deferment is documented to extend at least 12 months past closing. Conventional and FHA underwriting apply placeholder calculations (1 percent or 0.5 percent of balance) regardless of deferred status.

**Q: Will the SAVE plan ending affect my mortgage application?** It can. Borrowers transitioning out of SAVE in 2026 should ensure their current servicer has confirmed their new repayment plan and document the most recent payment status before applying. Mortgage underwriters use the documented current payment, not the prior SAVE payment.

**Q: Should I refinance my federal student loans before applying for a mortgage?** Maybe, but not without considering trade-offs. Refinancing into private loans loses access to Income-Driven Repayment plans, Public Service Loan Forgiveness, and federal forbearance protections. Mortgage qualification is one input among several. A financial advisor or specialized student loan counselor can help evaluate the trade-offs.

### 13.6 Reserves and Documentation

**Q: How much do I need in reserves?** Typically 2 to 12 months of PITIA, depending on the program, the loan amount, the borrower's stage (residents and fellows commonly require more), and whether the borrower is closing pre-start-date. 3 to 6 months is the median; 9 to 12 months is common for pre-start-date scenarios.

**Q: Can I count my retirement accounts as reserves?** Yes, with a discount. Most programs count 60 to 70 percent of vested 401(k), 403(b), and IRA balances as reserves.

**Q: Can I use family gift funds for the down payment?** Yes, universally allowed in this segment. A gift letter signed by the donor, stating the funds are a gift and not a loan and documenting the donor's ability to give, is required.

### 13.7 Loan Type Comparison

**Q: Should I use a physician loan or a conventional loan?** It depends. With 20 percent or more down and well-documented student loan payments, conventional is often cheaper net of all-in cost. With less than 10 percent down, no PMI, and constraint from student loans, the medical professional path is often more affordable. Compare both at pre-approval.

**Q: Should I use a physician loan or a VA loan?** If you're VA-eligible, the VA path is structurally favorable for zero-down financing at any loan amount, with no PMI and (for service-connected disability rated borrowers) no funding fee. Compare at pre-approval.

**Q: How does a physician loan compare to a jumbo loan?** Jumbo is best for higher down payment borrowers at high loan amounts. Medical professional is best for low-down borrowers in the same loan amount range. The borrower's down payment and time horizon usually determine which wins.

### 13.8 Process and Timing

**Q: How long does it take to close a physician loan?** 30 to 45 days from contract acceptance is typical. Pre-start-date scenarios can extend the timeline by a week or two due to additional documentation.

**Q: What can derail my pre-approval or closing?** Changes in employment, new credit applications, large purchases, undocumented deposits to bank accounts, late payments on any debt, and start-date changes. The recommended pre-closing posture is "do nothing financially" until the loan funds.

**Q: What credit score do I need?** 680 to 740 minimum, depending on the program. Most major programs are 700 to 720.

## Section 15. Common Misconceptions and Mistakes

These are statements that recur in borrower-facing content and in physician finance forums that are either wrong, oversimplified, or misleading. Each is documented with the corrected framing.

- "All physician loans cover all medical professionals." Wrong. Eligibility varies dramatically by program. The marketing label does not establish eligibility. The lender's written program guidelines do.

- "Physician loans always have zero down payment." Oversimplified. True at lower loan tiers in many programs; not true at higher loan amount tiers in any program. Down payment is structurally tiered by loan amount within each program.

- "Physician loans always have lower rates than conventional." Wrong. Often higher than conventional, sometimes by a meaningful margin, to compensate for the absence of MI. The all-in cost (rate plus MI plus closing costs) is the relevant comparison, not rate alone.

- "I can close the day before I start my new job." Wrong for most programs. Most programs have a minimum window (typically 30 days; some allow same-week, but the program-by-program documentation is rarely arranged that quickly).

- "My deferred student loans don't count." Wrong as a general statement. Conventional and FHA programs apply placeholder calculations on deferred loans. Some medical professional programs exclude deferred loans entirely; others apply a placeholder. Verify with the specific program.

- "I can use any income on a physician loan." Wrong. Bonus, RVU, moonlighting, and 1099 income all have specific seasoning and continuance rules. Some programs have stricter rules than agency programs on these income types.

- "The physician loan is always best for residents." Wrong. Conventional, FHA, or VA may compete depending on the scenario, especially for residents with strong documented IDR payments and clean credit.

- "I can apply with one lender's program and get the same terms at another." Wrong. Programs are individual products; terms vary materially. A pre-approval at one lender does not transfer to another.

- "Physician loans are non-QM." Wrong as a general statement. Most medical professional programs are QM under 12 CFR 1026.43(e). Some specific products (interest-only ARMs, some non-traditional structures) are non-QM. Verify with the specific lender.

- "Physician loans don't require reserves." Wrong. Most programs require 2 to 12 months of PITIA in reserves, with higher reserves for pre-start-date scenarios.

- "I can keep my old home and buy a new one easily." Oversimplified. Second home and rental conversion qualification questions apply. The prior-residence treatment varies by program.

- "Refinancing my federal student loans before the mortgage will help me qualify." Sometimes true, sometimes harmful. Refinancing federal loans loses IDR, PSLF, and federal protections. The mortgage qualification benefit is one input. Route to a financial advisor or specialized student loan counselor before acting.

- "My signing bonus counts as income for qualification." Wrong typically. The bonus is a one-time payment. The contract's guaranteed compensation is what qualifies.

- "My 401(k) loan doesn't count in DTI." Sometimes wrong on a medical professional loan. Many medical professional programs do count the 401(k) loan payment in DTI, which differs from agency rules that often exclude it. Verify with the specific lender.

- "I can switch lender programs at the closing table." Wrong. Programs are locked at application. Switching means restarting the underwriting clock.

- "All medical professional programs accept H-1B visas." Wrong. Some accept H-1B; some require permanent residency. Verify.

- "PMI on a low-down conventional loan is forever." Wrong. Conventional PMI is cancellable at 80 percent LTV (request) or automatically at 78 percent LTV under the Homeowner's Protection Act. (FHA MIP is generally for the life of the loan in most cases; that's a different rule.)

- "I can use my physician loan for an investment property." Wrong typically. Most programs are primary residence only.

- "My credit will get the same treatment at every lender." Wrong. Credit floors vary (670 to 740). Tradeline depth requirements vary. Authorized-user tradeline treatment varies.

## Section 16. Do / Don't Guidance

### 16.1 Do

- Engage a loan officer 6 to 12 months before your start date if you're a resident, fellow, or pre-start-date attending
- Pull and review your own credit report 12 months out, especially if you have a thin file
- Document your IDR payment plan with your federal student loan servicer; keep the most recent statement available
- Get your offer letter or employment contract fully executed (signed by both parties) before applying for the mortgage
- Confirm departmental and HR sign-off on the start date in writing
- Identify two or three lender programs that fit your profile and pre-qualify against each
- Model cash-to-close including reserves and the gap to first paycheck, conservatively (60 to 90 days from start date to first paycheck)
- Verify the homestead exemption filing process for your county after closing
- Verify homeowner's insurance availability and approximate cost before writing the offer
- Keep employment, credit, and assets stable throughout the underwriting and closing window
- Verify wire instructions in person or by verified phone before sending closing funds

### 16.2 Don't

- Don't refinance federal student loans without consulting a financial advisor or specialized student loan counselor about PSLF and IDR implications
- Don't accept a lender program's eligibility claim without verifying it covers your specific profession, degree, training status, and visa status
- Don't change jobs, sign new contracts, or accept role changes during the application or closing window
- Don't open new credit in the 90 days before pre-approval or between pre-approval and closing
- Don't move money between accounts in the 60 days before application without paper-trailing
- Don't assume the relocation package will arrive in time for closing without verifying the timing
- Don't waive contingencies in a competitive offer without understanding the cash-gap exposure
- Don't accept a single lender's terms as the market rate without comparing against two to three other programs
- Don't close more than 90 days before your start date without confirming the program allows it
- Don't underestimate the cash gap between closing and first paycheck
- Don't trust wire instructions received only by email; wire fraud is a real and increasing risk

## Section 17. Compliance and Phrasing Caveats

This section identifies language requirements for medical-professional content. Borrower-facing pages built from this research must adhere to these standards.

### 17.1 Hedging Vocabulary

Use throughout the borrower-facing content:

- "Many programs," "most programs," "some programs"
- "Common across the segment"
- "Varies by lender"
- "Subject to program-specific overlay"
- "May be available depending on the lender's program"
- "Verify with the specific lender"
- "Programs may differ in their treatment of"

Avoid as absolute statements:

- "The physician loan rule"
- "Physician loans require"
- "All physician loans"
- "Doctors get"
- "You'll qualify"
- "Your student loans won't count"
- "You'll close before your start date"
- "You don't need a down payment"

### 17.2 Statements That Should Never Appear as Guarantees

- "You'll qualify for a physician loan"
- "Your IBR payment will be excluded"
- "Your deferred loans won't count"
- "You'll close in X days"
- "You'll get zero down"
- "You'll pay no PMI"
- "Your rate will be lower than conventional"

Each of these statements is conditional on the lender's program, the borrower's profile, and the specific transaction. State them with hedging language or as program-specific examples.

### 17.3 Eligibility-Overclaim Risk (Repeat from Section 4.4)

The single highest-risk content pattern in this segment is asserting that a profession qualifies, that a tier is available, or that a feature applies, without verifying the specific lender program covers the borrower's specific profile. Borrower-facing content must accurately describe the variability and must avoid describing programs in absolute terms.

### 17.4 Required Disclosures

- Equal Housing Lender disclosure
- NMLS unique identifier for the originator and the lender
- Alabama state mortgage license disclosure
- ATR/QM disclosures where applicable
- Truth in Lending Act (Regulation Z) advertising standards (12 CFR 1026.24 in particular for advertising)
- RESPA disclosures where applicable

### 17.5 Routing Language for Adjacent Questions

- Tax questions (signing bonus, relocation gross-up, 1099 vs. W-2, RSU vesting, ISO vs. NSO, K-1 income for practice owners): "Consult a CPA."
- Student loan strategy questions (refinance decisions, PSLF strategy, plan switching, buyback decisions, married-filing-separately decisions): "Consult a financial advisor or specialized student loan counselor."
- Financial planning questions (asset allocation, insurance needs analysis, retirement plan choice, college funding for children, estate planning): "Consult a financial advisor."
- Entity structure questions (private practice ownership, S-corp vs. LLC, professional association formation, corporate practice of medicine compliance): "Consult an attorney."
- PSLF and loan forgiveness strategy questions: "Contact your federal student loan servicer or a specialized student loan counselor."

### 17.6 The Line Between Mortgage Advice and Adjacent Advice

The loan officer's lane is mortgage qualification, mortgage product selection, mortgage documentation, and mortgage closing. Adjacent topics (tax, financial planning, student loan strategy, entity structure) are not the loan officer's lane and must be routed to the appropriate professional. Borrower-facing content must respect this line and route appropriately.

## Section 18. Risks, Caveats, and Overclaiming Language to Avoid

This is a flagged checklist for the writer building borrower-facing content from this research.

### 18.1 Primary Concern: Eligibility-Overclaim Risk

Statements to avoid without explicit lender-program-specific verification:

- "Veterinarians can use a physician loan" (verify the lender program covers DVM)
- "Pharmacists qualify for doctor mortgages" (verify the lender program covers PharmD)
- "CRNAs are eligible" (verify the lender program covers CRNA)
- "Nurse practitioners can use medical professional loans" (verify; many programs do not)
- "Physician assistants qualify" (verify; many programs do not)
- "All clinical doctorates qualify" (false; varies by program)
- "DPT or DC are eligible" (verify; only select programs cover these)

### 18.2 "No Money Down" Claims

Avoid: "Doctors don't need a down payment"
Better: "Many programs offer zero-down financing at the lower loan amount tier (commonly up to $750,000 to $1.5 million depending on the program); higher loan amounts require 5 to 10 percent or more down. The specific tier varies by lender."

### 18.3 "No PMI" Claims

Avoid: "Physician loans never have PMI"
Better: "Programs in this segment typically waive private mortgage insurance even at high LTV. The rate may be modestly higher than a conventional loan with PMI to compensate for the absence of mortgage insurance."

### 18.4 Student Loan Treatment Claims

Avoid: "Your student loans don't count on a physician loan"
Better: "Student loan treatment varies by lender. Some programs accept the documented IDR payment, some exclude deferred loans entirely under specific documentation, and some apply a fixed percentage of balance. The medical professional segment is typically more flexible than conventional, FHA, or VA underwriting on $0 IDR or active deferment, but the specific treatment must be verified with each program."

### 18.5 Pre-Start-Date Claims

Avoid: "You can close 90 days before you start your new job"
Better: "Most major programs allow closing 30 to 90 days before the borrower's employment start date with a fully executed contract. Some programs allow up to 6 months. The window varies; the contract must be fully executed; and reserves typically must cover the gap to first paycheck."

### 18.6 Rate Claims

Avoid quoting any specific rate. Avoid: "Physician loan rates are around X percent." Better: "Rates vary by lender, market, and borrower profile. The all-in cost comparison (rate plus MI plus closing costs plus reserve impact) is more relevant than rate alone."

### 18.7 Loan Amount Claims

Avoid: "You can borrow up to $3 million on a doctor loan"
Better: "Loan amount ceilings vary by program and range from $1 million to $3.5 million across major programs, with select programs reaching higher. The achievable loan amount depends on the borrower's profession, training stage, credit profile, income, and down payment."

### 18.8 Comparison Claims

Avoid: "Physician loans are always better than conventional"
Better: "Whether a medical professional loan or a conventional loan is the better fit depends on the borrower's down payment, credit, student loan profile, target loan amount, and time horizon. A loan officer who can run both paths at pre-approval is more useful than one who only offers one path."

### 18.9 Universal Disclaimers

Borrower-facing content built from this research should carry, at minimum:

- Equal Housing Lender
- NMLS unique identifier
- "All loans subject to credit approval, underwriting, and property eligibility"
- "Programs subject to change without notice"
- "Loan terms vary by lender; verify with the specific lender"
- A note that the page is for educational purposes and is not a commitment to lend
