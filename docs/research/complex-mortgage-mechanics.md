## 2. Plain-English Summary

A "complex" mortgage file is not a different kind of loan. It is a borrower file that does not fit the simplest pattern of W-2 base salary plus two pay stubs plus two months of bank statements plus an automated approval. The actual loan still has to satisfy the underwriting rules of Fannie Mae, Freddie Mac, FHA, VA, USDA, a jumbo investor, or a non-QM program. Where those rules differ, this packet surfaces the difference rather than picking one rulebook and treating it as universal.

What pushes a file out of "standard" into "complex" is usually one or more of: self-employment of any kind, variable income that is not 100% W-2 base salary (bonus, commission, overtime, RSU, K-1, 1099), a recent employment change or relocation, a current home being kept as a rental rather than sold, multiple already-financed properties, gift funds or family-lender involvement, large recent deposits in bank accounts, retirement or brokerage liquidations to fund cash-to-close, trust assets, foreign income or foreign assets, or recent credit events still inside seasoning windows.

The single most important strategic point: complex files take longer, require more documentation, and benefit substantially from earlier engagement with the loan officer than a simple file does. A clean W-2 borrower can often go from first call to pre-approval in days; a complex borrower commonly needs three to six months of upfront planning to get to a pre-approval that actually holds up under contract. The cost of writing an offer before the file is fully scrubbed is the cost of an underwriting condition that surfaces at week three of escrow and either delays closing or kills the contract entirely.

The right loan type depends on the specific complexity. A self-employed borrower with clean returns often fits conventional fine; a self-employed borrower whose tax returns aggressively reduce taxable income may fit a non-QM bank statement program better than conventional, because the non-QM program qualifies on deposits rather than tax-return net income. A buyer relocating before a new job starts may have a path through the conventional employment-offer rule, an FHA equivalent, or a medical professional program with more pre-start-date flexibility. A borrower retaining a current home that will become a rental needs to know whether the new lease, security deposit, and rent schedule will let them count rental income, whether they will have to qualify with both housing payments, and how the loan type they choose treats those scenarios. A buyer using a 401(k) loan needs to know how that liability counts against DTI under each loan type. A borrower receiving a large family gift needs to know what donor documentation is required, what timing rules apply, and how the donor's bank statement and transfer paper trail will be handled.

What complex borrowers should do, in order, is roughly: engage a loan officer six to twelve months before shopping if anything in the file is non-standard; file tax returns on time every year because extensions are an underwriting headache; pull a full credit report and remediate errors before applying; document variable income components with employer letters; identify the departing-residence strategy and confirm whether rental income will count; identify the cash-to-close target and the reserves target separately; do a verified-income, verified-asset (VIVA) pre-approval before writing an offer; tell the loan officer about every job change, large deposit, and new tradeline in real time; and resist the Realtor pressure to write a 21-day-close offer when the file genuinely needs 35 to 45.

What complex borrowers should never do is treat the lender like a checkbox after a property is found. The lender is the timeline, and the timeline is the offer.

This packet does not provide tax, legal, or financial-planning advice. Tax questions on add-backs, depreciation, K-1 distributions, gift tax, and retirement liquidation route to a CPA. Entity-structure and trust questions route to an attorney. Investment, retirement, and asset-allocation questions route to a financial advisor. Concurrent-closing logistics across two states route to closing attorneys in both states.

## 3. What Makes a Mortgage File "Complex": The Planning Frame

### 3.1 Defining Complexity

The simplest residential mortgage file in the United States is a W-2 borrower with at least two years at the same employer, a fixed base salary, no variable compensation, two pay stubs covering the most recent 30 days, the most recent W-2 (under the 03-04-2026 Fannie Mae Selling Guide restructure, fixed and variable base income now require only the most recent W-2 and pay stub rather than two prior W-2s, per the Income Assessment chapter update at https://singlefamily.fanniemae.com/originating-underwriting/income-assessment-simplified), two months of bank statements with no large deposits, no large recent purchases on credit, and an automated underwriting approval through Fannie Mae's Desktop Underwriter (DU) or Freddie Mac's Loan Product Advisor (LPA).

Any of the following pushes a file out of "standard" into "complex":

- Income that is not 100% W-2 base salary (bonus, commission, overtime, RVU compensation, RSU or other restricted stock, ESPP, 1099, K-1 distributions, owner draws, royalties, board fees)
- Self-employment of any kind, including a side business reported on Schedule C alongside primary W-2 wages, anyone with 25% or more ownership in a business per Fannie Mae B3-3.5-01 (https://selling-guide.fanniemae.com/sel/b3-3.5-01/underwriting-factors-and-documentation-self-employed-borrower)
- Less than two years at the current employer, recent role changes, recent compensation-structure changes, or a job change in progress
- Pre-start-date employment with the future employer's offer letter as the income basis
- A current primary residence that will be retained (kept as a second home, kept vacant, kept as a rental) rather than sold
- Multiple already-financed properties (particularly investment properties)
- Significant gift funds, gifts of equity, or family-lender loans
- Large recent deposits inside the asset documentation window that require sourcing
- Retirement account distributions, 401(k) loans, or brokerage liquidations to fund cash-to-close
- Trust assets, trust beneficiary distributions, or trustee or executor involvement in funding
- Foreign income, foreign assets, or non-U.S.-citizen borrower status (which has its own Fannie Mae topic at B2-2-02)
- Recent credit events (bankruptcy, foreclosure, short sale, deed-in-lieu) still within seasoning windows
- Co-borrowers with materially different financial profiles (one self-employed, one W-2; one with strong credit, one with thin file; one with significant assets, one without)
- Cryptocurrency or digital asset holdings to be liquidated for cash-to-close

A file with one complex element is usually manageable inside a normal closing timeline. A file with three or four compounds; the closing timeline stretches, the documentation set grows, and the probability that an underwriter raises a new condition late in the file increases.

### 3.2 Why Complexity Demands Earlier Planning

The standard W-2 pre-approval timeline is typically days. The complex pre-approval timeline is commonly weeks, sometimes months, and the documentation list is materially longer. A self-employed borrower with K-1 income from two entities, a partial year of YTD profit and loss documentation, a recent partnership change, and a target purchase price that requires both K-1 distributions and W-2 wages to count is not a file that can be moved from initial call to "issue a pre-approval letter today" in any honest way.

Complex files also generate more underwriting conditions than simple files: more letters of explanation, more updated bank statements, more updated YTD income documentation, more verbal verifications of employment, more re-runs through DU or LPA when a number changes. The standard "automatic" underwriting approval is just the start of the work, not the end. The risk is concentrated late in the closing timeline, when an updated paystub reveals a compensation change, an updated bank statement reveals a deposit that has to be sourced, or a verbal VOE reveals that the borrower's role at work has changed.

The most expensive mistake complex buyers make is treating the loan officer as the last call after the property is found. By that point, the timeline pressure has already been set by the seller, the Realtor, and the contract. If the file genuinely needs 35 to 45 days but the contract requires 21, the buyer is exposed to either a closing delay (which can result in earnest money loss or contract termination) or a request for extension (which strengthens the seller's negotiating position). The simplest way to avoid all of this is to engage the loan officer first and write the offer within the timeline the loan officer can actually meet.

### 3.3 The Right-Loan-Type Decision

Complex borrowers often have multiple loan-type options, and the best choice depends on the specific complexity:

- **Self-employed with clean returns (low add-back dependency, two full years of stable returns, owner draws or distributions documented):** conventional through Fannie Mae or Freddie Mac is often the best path. Both agencies use the same general framework of analyzing personal and business returns, applying add-backs, and arriving at a stable monthly qualifying income via Form 1084 (Fannie) or Form 91 (Freddie). The Fannie Mae Income Calculator and Freddie Mac Income Calculator (the latter launched 05-11-2025 per Bulletin 2025-4) automate parts of this analysis.
- **Self-employed with heavy deductions, where tax-return net income is substantially lower than actual cash flow:** a non-QM bank statement loan may produce a much higher qualifying income than conventional. Industry illustrations consistently show 12 to 24 months of business deposits with a 50% expense factor (or a CPA-supported lower factor, often 10% to 20% in some programs) producing two to three times the qualifying income that Form 1084 would produce on the same business. Trade-off: rate premium, larger down payment requirement, often 10% to 20% down minimum, and stronger reserve requirements.
- **Recent relocation with a fully executed offer letter and a start date within roughly 90 days of closing:** Fannie Mae B3-3.3-03 (Employment Offers or Contracts) provides two paths, one where the borrower obtains a paystub before delivery, and one where the loan can be delivered without a paystub when the contract is fully executed and non-contingent and the loan is a one-unit principal-residence purchase. Freddie Mac has parallel rules. FHA, VA, and USDA have analogous but not identical pathways.
- **Medical professional with a future start date and student-loan complexity:** dedicated medical-professional programs (sometimes called "physician loans" or "professional loans") often extend pre-start-date allowance and treat student loan IBR / PAYE / SAVE payments with more flexibility than agency rules. Cross-reference the medical professional mechanics file.
- **Multiple financed properties:** Fannie Mae caps borrowers at 10 financed properties in B2-2-03 (https://selling-guide.fanniemae.com/sel/b2-2-03/multiple-financed-properties-same-borrower), with reserve requirements that scale (2% of aggregate UPB on other financed properties for one to four properties, 4% for five to six, and 6% for seven to ten in DU only, per B3-4.1-01). Borrowers above the cap must look at jumbo investor portfolios or DSCR programs.
- **Asset-rich, current-W-2-light retiree:** asset depletion non-QM programs let the borrower convert liquid asset balances into monthly qualifying income via a divisor (commonly assets divided by remaining loan term in months, with documented adjustments). This can beat agency programs for borrowers with significant brokerage and retirement assets but limited current pay.
- **Large gift funds:** all loan types allow gift funds with documentation. Conventional, FHA, VA, and USDA each have specific rules; non-QM programs vary widely.

The right answer is not "always conventional" or "always non-QM." It is the loan type that produces the highest defensible qualifying income and the cleanest underwriting path for that specific borrower's complexity, at a rate, reserve, and down payment combination the borrower can actually accept.

### 3.4 The Pre-Pre-Approval Conversation

Three to six months before a complex borrower wants to shop, the most useful single action is a 30-to-60-minute planning conversation with the loan officer that produces four outputs:

1. A realistic loan-amount range, anchored to a defensible income calculation rather than a self-reported income number
2. A realistic loan-type recommendation, with the second-best alternative noted
3. A specific list of documentation gaps the borrower needs to close before formal application (missing tax returns on extension, missing K-1 from a 2024 partnership return, an offer letter that doesn't yet specify a start date, a 401(k) loan whose payment isn't yet on the most recent statement)
4. A realistic timeline from formal application to clear-to-close

The planning conversation is not a pre-approval. The borrower should not expect a letter and the loan officer should not produce one. The output is a roadmap, not a commitment. The cost is one conversation; the benefit is avoiding a pre-approval issued on incomplete information that fails to hold up under contract.

## 4. Self-Employed Borrower Documentation

### 4.1 The Core Self-Employed Documentation Set (Conventional Baseline)

Per Fannie Mae B3-3.5-01 (https://selling-guide.fanniemae.com/sel/b3-3.5-01/underwriting-factors-and-documentation-self-employed-borrower) and the parallel Freddie Mac Section 5304.1, a self-employed borrower (defined by Fannie Mae as anyone with 25% or more ownership in a business) typically provides:

- **Two years of personal federal tax returns (Form 1040)** with all schedules and statements
- **Two years of business federal tax returns** when the entity files separately:
  - Schedule C (sole proprietorship, attached to the personal 1040)
  - Form 1065 plus K-1 (partnership)
  - Form 1120-S plus K-1 (S-corporation)
  - Form 1120 (C-corporation)
- **Year-to-date Profit and Loss statement** prepared by the borrower or CPA, typically required when the most recent tax year is more than a few months old or when YTD income is being relied on
- **Year-to-date balance sheet,** more commonly required for partnerships and corporations than sole proprietorships
- **Business bank statements,** typically two months for full-doc files; 12 or 24 months for non-QM bank statement loans
- **CPA letter** confirming continued operation of the business (a "business is still operating" letter is commonly requested as the verbal-VOE substitute for self-employed borrowers under B3-3.1-04). When business funds are used for the down payment, an additional CPA statement that withdrawal will not adversely affect the business is commonly required.
- **Business license and operating documentation:** business license, articles of incorporation or organization, operating agreement, partnership agreement
- **K-1 income:** two years of K-1s plus the underlying entity tax return showing distributions and ownership percentage. Note: per Freddie Mac Bulletin 2024-10 (effective 11-08-2024 per https://corr.pennymac.com/announcements/announcement-24-101) and Fannie Mae Bulletin SEL-2025-08 (https://singlefamily.fanniemae.com/media/43851/display, effective 02-01-2026 with immediate adoption permitted), when a borrower's only self-employment income comes from K-1 with less than 25% ownership and the income reported is rental income only, the documentation set has been streamlined to one year of business returns rather than two in some cases.

Fannie Mae explicitly allows Form 1084 (Cash Flow Analysis), the Income Calculator, or "another type of cash flow analysis" or an automated tool that applies the same principles, with the written analysis retained in the loan file (B3-3.5-01).

### 4.2 Self-Employment Length-of-History Requirements

The standard rule across conventional, FHA, VA, and USDA is two years of self-employment history. Each authority has nuanced exceptions:

- **Fannie Mae (B3-3.5-01):** A borrower with less than two years of self-employment may be considered if the most recent signed personal and business returns reflect a full 12 months of self-employment income from the current business AND the loan file documents a history of receipt of prior income at the same or greater level in the same line of work or in an occupation with similar responsibilities. The lender must give "careful consideration to the nature of the borrower's level of experience, and the amount of debt the business has acquired."
- **Freddie Mac (Section 5304.1):** Generally parallel two-year rule, with some flexibility for borrowers who have been self-employed for less than two years if the income source is otherwise stable, supported by tax returns or transcripts, and represents the same line of work.
- **FHA (Handbook 4000.1, Section II.A.4 / II.A.5):** Two years of self-employment is the standard. If the borrower has been self-employed between one and two years, FHA may allow consideration only if the borrower was previously employed in the same line of work in which the borrower is self-employed, or in a related occupation, for at least two years. Per https://www.fhanewsblog.com/hud-4000-1-on-self-employment-income-for-fha-home-loans/ summarizing HUD 4000.1.
- **VA (Pamphlet 26-7, Chapter 4):** Generally two-year self-employment history; a recently discharged veteran who is self-employed has additional analysis requirements per Section 4.04.j.
- **USDA (HB-1-3555 Chapter 9):** Generally two-year self-employment history with documentation through tax returns or transcripts.
- **Non-QM:** Many bank statement and 1099-only programs accept one year of self-employment if the borrower has prior W-2 history in the same line of work. Some require two years. This is program-specific and shifts.

A recent transition from W-2 to self-employed without a clean two-year self-employment record is typically a strong signal to consider a non-QM bank statement program if the documentation supports it. A recent transition from self-employed to W-2 (within the last 30 days, or with the W-2 income being the basis of qualification) typically allows the W-2 income to be used once the borrower has a paystub and the verbal VOE confirms employment, although a recent shift can trigger underwriter scrutiny on stability and likelihood of continuance.

### 4.3 Cash Flow Analysis: How Underwriters Calculate Self-Employed Income

The single largest gap in self-employed borrower understanding is the difference between what they think their income is and what the underwriter calculates as qualifying income.

Fannie Mae's Cash Flow Analysis Form 1084 and Freddie Mac's Form 91 are the standard worksheets. Both work in the same general way: start with the personal Form 1040, examine each Schedule C or business entity return, identify line items that the lender can add back to taxable income (because they are non-cash expenses or one-time items), and arrive at a stable monthly qualifying income.

Common add-backs (each subject to the relevant authority and its current rules):

- **Depreciation (Schedule C, 1120, 1120-S, 1065 K-1):** typically added back across all loan types, because depreciation is a non-cash expense
- **Depletion:** typically added back, similar logic
- **Amortization:** generally added back. Per FHA Info 2025-04 (effective 04-10-2025), FHA explicitly added amortization as an allowable add-back on 1120, 1120-S, and 1065 K-1 returns, aligning with conventional treatment
- **Business use of home (Form 8829, reported on Schedule C):** add-back rules vary by authority. FHA Info 2025-04 effective 04-10-2025 explicitly added Schedule C business use of home as an add-back for FHA, aligning with conventional practice
- **Mileage / vehicle depreciation portion:** the depreciation component of standard mileage is commonly added back; the total mileage deduction is not. Calculation involves multiplying business miles by the IRS depreciation portion of the mileage rate (which varies by tax year)
- **Non-recurring casualty losses (1120, 1120-S, 1065 K-1):** added back per FHA Info 2025-04 effective 04-10-2025; conventional treatment is similar but item-specific
- **Meals and entertainment:** typically NOT added back, because the IRS already disallows a portion of these expenses

For sole proprietors (Schedule C), the qualifying income is essentially net Schedule C income plus add-backs, divided by 12 (or 24 for two-year averaging).

For partnerships (Form 1065 plus K-1) and S-corps (Form 1120-S plus K-1), the analysis is more complex. The lender looks at:

- The borrower's W-2 from the S-corp (if applicable) plus
- The borrower's K-1 ordinary business income (subject to ownership-percentage and continuance analysis) plus
- Documented distributions to the borrower
- Adjusted by depreciation, amortization, depletion, mortgage or notes payable in less than one year, and other authority-specific items

The two-year averaging rule applies broadly: when both years are stable or increasing, lenders typically average the two years to arrive at monthly qualifying income. When the most recent year is materially lower than the prior year (a declining trend), the conservative practice across conventional, FHA, VA, and USDA is to use the most recent year's lower figure rather than the two-year average. Some authorities formalize this; others leave it to underwriter judgment with a stability and continuance test. Per Fannie Mae B3-3.5-01: "the lender must give careful consideration to the trend" and may use Form 1088 (Comparative Income Analysis) or similar to document the trend.

The five-years-in-existence rule on Fannie Mae B3-3.5-01 lets the lender use one year of returns instead of two if the business has been in existence for at least five years and the borrower has owned 25% or more for at least five years, provided documentation supports the timeline.

### 4.4 K-1 Income and S-Corp / Partnership Owners

K-1 distributions can swing year to year independent of business profitability. A profitable S-corp may retain earnings to fund operations or capital expenditures rather than distribute them; a less profitable year may still produce distributions because of cash on hand. This makes K-1 income the single trickiest income type to qualify on.

Required documentation for K-1 income, per Fannie Mae B3-3.5-01 and B3-3.5-02 (Income or Loss Reported on Schedule K-1) and the parallel Freddie Mac sections:

- K-1 forms for two years
- Underlying entity return (Form 1120-S for S-corps, Form 1065 for partnerships) for two years
- Schedule of ownership percentage
- Documentation of distribution history and likelihood of continuance

The "ownership percentage" rule matters for whether business income on the entity return can be relied on or whether only documented distributions count. Fannie Mae B3-3.5-01 (https://homebuyer.com/guidelines/fannie-mae/underwriting-factors-and-documentation-for-a-self-employed-borrower-b3-3-2-01 paraphrasing the rule) treats anyone with 25% or more ownership as self-employed for income analysis purposes. Below 25% ownership, K-1 income may be treated as non-self-employed income with reduced documentation in some cases.

The S-corp owner who takes minimal W-2 plus large K-1 distributions creates a qualification headache because the underwriter must analyze the S-corp's ability to continue distributing at the historical rate, the borrower's W-2 wage stability, and the relationship between the two. Many S-corp owners are surprised that the underwriter does not simply add their W-2 to their K-1 distributions; the analysis is more nuanced.

### 4.5 1099 Income

1099 contractors (independent contractors, gig workers, locum providers, fee-for-service consultants) typically file Schedule C as sole proprietors unless they operate through an entity. The two-year tax return requirement applies. The Schedule C analysis described in 4.3 controls.

Non-QM 1099-only programs accept 1099 forms (typically two years, sometimes one) as the income basis with reduced documentation, applying an expense factor to gross 1099 receipts to arrive at qualifying income. These programs are common for borrowers with significant Schedule C deductions or recently shifted-to-1099 borrowers.

Per the Department of Labor guidance on independent contractor classification under the Fair Labor Standards Act (https://www.dol.gov/agencies/whd/flsa/misclassification), the W-2 versus 1099 classification is fact-specific. Mortgage underwriters do not adjudicate this question but will treat the file based on how the borrower has actually been paid and reported.

### 4.6 Bank Statement Loans (Non-QM Alternative)

Non-QM bank statement loans use 12 or 24 months of personal or business bank statements as the income basis. The product exists outside QM under 12 CFR 1026.43; the lender still has an Ability-to-Repay obligation but uses bank statements rather than tax returns to verify income.

Common deposit-analysis methods (illustrative, not normative; varies by program):

- **Fixed expense factor on business statements:** commonly 50% applied to total eligible deposits, with the remainder counted as qualifying income. Example: $20,000 monthly business deposits times 50% expense factor equals $10,000 qualifying monthly income. Some programs allow lower expense factors (10% to 20%) for low-overhead businesses with documentation.
- **CPA-supported expense ratio:** a CPA, enrolled agent, or licensed tax preparer provides a customized expense ratio based on the actual business. Programs typically require a minimum 10% to 20% expense factor floor.
- **Personal bank statements:** lenders typically count 100% of deposits attributable to the business as qualifying income, with restrictions on transfers, retransfers, and non-business deposits.

When bank statement loans make sense:

- Heavy-deduction Schedule C where qualifying income on returns is low but actual cash flow is strong
- Recent business owners without two full years of returns
- 1099 contractors whose returns under-represent actual cash flow

Trade-offs across illustrative non-QM programs (varies widely):

- Rate premium of roughly 0.5 to 2 percentage points above conventional (varies by program, credit profile, LTV)
- Down payment minimums commonly 10% to 20% (some programs go to 90% LTV with mortgage insurance)
- Reserve requirements often 3 to 12 months of PITI
- Credit score floors typically 620 to 700 depending on program

Clay's note on non-QM specifically: Optimal Blue's December 2025 Market Advantage data showed non-QM at 9% of total mortgage lock volume, with bank statement loans accounting for roughly one third of that segment per https://nationalmortgageprofessional.com/news/non-qm-mortgage-production-climbs-new-heights-2025-ends-strong. Polygon Research's HMDA-based estimate puts 2025 non-QM at roughly $239 billion across 697,605 loans, or 10.2% of all originations by count (https://www.polygonresearch.com/non-qm-market). The segment is mainstream, not subprime; it is a real and rapidly growing alternative for self-employed borrowers.

### 4.7 P&L-Only Loans (Non-QM Alternative)

P&L-only loans use a CPA-prepared profit and loss statement as the income basis, sometimes supplemented by bank statements to validate gross revenue. Fewer lenders offer this product than offer bank statement loans. Use cases overlap with bank statement loans but require stronger CPA involvement and tend to carry tighter LTV and credit overlays. Programs vary too widely for normative description here.

### 4.8 Common Self-Employed Documentation Pitfalls

- **Filing tax returns late or on extension at the moment of mortgage application.** Lenders need the most recent year's filed return; an extension typically forces the lender to use the prior year's return as the most recent filed, plus a year-to-date P&L, plus close attention to whether the YTD numbers match the trend the prior year suggests. The simplest fix is to file on time every year.
- **Cash-basis accounting that does not translate cleanly to underwriter analysis.** Cash-basis returns can show large income swings driven by client payment timing rather than actual business performance. The underwriter still uses the return; the borrower may need a CPA letter explaining the swings.
- **Heavy 4562 depreciation that is not cleanly added back.** Depreciation on business equipment, vehicles, and rental properties is a major add-back; if the return does not clearly identify the depreciation amount, the underwriter cannot apply the add-back.
- **Owner draws documentation that does not match what the borrower described.** Owner draws on a sole proprietorship or partnership are not the same as W-2 wages or S-corp distributions; the underwriter looks at the underlying entity return rather than the draw amount.
- **Business bank account commingling with personal funds.** Mixed personal and business activity in a single bank account makes large-deposit sourcing harder and complicates a future bank statement loan if needed.
- **Recent business pivots:** new entity, change in entity type (sole prop converted to S-corp), recent partnership dissolution. Each of these starts a new entity-history clock for the underwriter.
- **Year-to-date P&L that diverges sharply from prior years.** A YTD P&L showing income materially higher or lower than the prior year creates underwriter scrutiny. The borrower should be ready with documentation of why.
- **Revenue concentration with a single client.** Some lenders flag heavy single-client concentration as a stability risk.
- **Sole proprietor whose actual structure is more complex.** Some borrowers describe themselves as "I just file Schedule C" when the actual business has employees, contractors, and significant fixed costs. The underwriter analyzes what the returns show, not the borrower's casual description.

Tax questions on add-backs, depreciation strategy, K-1 timing, and entity-structure tax consequences route to a CPA. Entity-structure questions on S-corp election timing or LLC versus partnership election route to an attorney.

## 5. Variable Income: Bonus, Commission, Overtime, Seasonal, Declining

### 5.1 The Two-Year History Standard

The standard rule across conventional, FHA, VA, and USDA is a two-year history of receipt for variable income to be used in qualification. The conventional rule sits in Fannie Mae B3-3.3 (Variable income now lives in B3-3.3-01 Base Income, B3-3.3-02 Bonus, Commission, Overtime, and Tip Income, and B3-3.3-08 Seasonal Income post 03-04-2026 restructure). The Freddie Mac parallel sits in Section 5303.

The 12-month exception applies in some cases when variable income is paid by the same employer with documented continuance and stability. Per Fannie Mae's restructured Income Assessment chapter, "income continuance requirement is established by the respective income source," meaning the rule lives inside each specific income type rather than as a single global rule. Lenders may apply the changes immediately and must apply them to applications dated 06-01-2026 or later (https://singlefamily.fanniemae.com/originating-underwriting/income-assessment-simplified).

### 5.2 Bonus Income

Documentation set:

- Most recent W-2 (under the 03-04-2026 Fannie Mae update, only the most recent W-2 is required for fixed and variable base income, replacing the prior two-W-2 standard, but bonus income is generally analyzed alongside base income and may require additional historical documentation depending on amount and stability)
- Employer letter on bonus structure and likelihood of continuance
- Year-to-date pay stubs showing year-to-date bonus receipt
- Two years of W-2s when needed to establish history of bonus receipt

Calculation method: typically two-year averaging when the trend is stable or increasing. Per Fannie Mae's Top Trending Selling FAQ on bonus income (https://singlefamily.fanniemae.com/faq-top-trending-selling-faqs): "When a bonus is paid annually or at another time during the year, the lender may use historical bonus income documented on prior year's pay stubs, W-2s, and/or the borrower's federal income tax returns to determine the qualifying amount and to accurately annualize the bonus, as applicable."

The declining-trend rule typically forces the most recent year's lower number to be used rather than the average.

Discretionary versus guaranteed bonus distinction:

- Guaranteed bonus (e.g., a contractually defined performance bonus tied to documented metrics) is more easily counted
- Discretionary bonus (employer's discretion year over year) requires more support for continuance

Special bonus types:

- **Sign-on bonus:** typically not used as qualifying income; treated as a one-time payment. Per Fannie Mae's restricted-stock guidance specifically and the broader bonus framework, sign-on bonuses received in the form of restricted stock that vest over any length of time also cannot be used as qualifying income (https://corr.pennymac.com/non-delegated-announcements/announcement-24-16)
- **Retention bonus:** treatment varies by structure, with multi-year retention bonuses sometimes qualifying as continuing income

### 5.3 Commission Income

Documentation set:

- Two years of W-2s or 1099s showing commission breakdown
- Commission statements
- Employer letter identifying the commission structure (commission-only, base plus commission)
- Year-to-date pay stubs

The 25% threshold matters: when commission income represents more than 25% of total compensation (often described as the "high-commission" threshold), conventional underwriting typically requires the borrower's tax returns and analyzes any unreimbursed business expenses on Schedule A or Form 2106 as a reduction to qualifying income. Per Fannie Mae's prior treatment (preserved in the restructured Income Assessment chapter), unreimbursed employee business expenses are not required to be deducted for commission income less than 25% of total compensation. For commission income of 25% or more, the analysis is more complex.

Commission-only versus commission-plus-base:

- Commission-only employment with two years of W-2s is treated like other variable income with two-year averaging
- Commission-plus-base requires both components to be analyzed: base is typically counted on current rate; commission is averaged

Draws against commission: a draw is typically treated as an advance against future commission, not as separate income. The underwriter looks at total compensation rather than the draw component alone.

### 5.4 Overtime Income

Two-year history is the standard. Documentation:

- Pay stubs and W-2s showing overtime separately
- Employer letter on overtime expectation and continuance
- Industry context (some industries have predictable overtime, others do not)

Per Fannie Mae's Top Trending Selling FAQ (https://singlefamily.fanniemae.com/faq-top-trending-selling-faqs), overtime income "must be evaluated separately from base hourly pay and calculated using an earnings trend / average approach (using year-to-date and prior earnings, as applicable), not by applying an 'average hours x rate' method." This is a frequent underwriter-borrower confusion point.

The continuance question is whether overtime will continue at the same rate. A recent slowdown in overtime hours, a recent role change to a non-overtime position, or an industry trend toward reduced overtime can each cause underwriter caution.

### 5.5 Seasonal Income

Per Fannie Mae B3-3.3-08 (https://selling-guide.fanniemae.com/sel/b3-3.3-08/seasonal-income), the standard rule is a two-year history of seasonal employment plus documentation that the seasonal pattern is expected to continue and that the borrower has been re-hired or expects to be re-hired.

Documentation:

- Pay stubs and W-2s
- Employer letter confirming seasonal pattern and re-hire intent
- For self-employed seasonal businesses, two years of returns showing the seasonal pattern

Income is calculated on an annual basis (not monthly), since the borrower works only part of the year and receives no income in off-seasons.

Common seasonal scenarios: construction, agriculture, education (teachers and school district employees with summer breaks), hospitality, ski-industry roles, tax-preparation seasonal staff.

### 5.6 Declining Income

When the trend is downward year over year, the conventional rule under Fannie Mae's framework (and the parallel Freddie Mac, FHA, VA, USDA frameworks) is to use the lower (most recent) year's figure rather than averaging. This applies to base income, bonus, commission, overtime, and self-employment income.

Documentation requirements when income has declined:

- Letter of explanation identifying the cause
- If the cause is a one-time anomaly (a documented medical leave, a specific client loss, a known industry event), supporting documentation
- If the trend is continuing, the lower number controls

The interpretation challenge is whether the decline is a one-time anomaly or a continuing trend. The borrower's case is strongest when the cause is documented externally (medical records confirming a leave, the client's bankruptcy filing, public industry data on a specific event) rather than self-reported. Per Freddie Mac Bulletin 2025-6 (effective 05-07-2025 per https://corr.pennymac.com/announcements/announcement-25-63), Freddie Mac clarified that "a shorter income calculation period (minimum 12 months combined) in certain instances for fluctuating earnings (base, hourly, bonus, overtime, commission, tips) when a specific event caused reduced income and is unlikely to recur. The 12 months combined income does not need to be consecutive (e.g., the period impacted by the non-recurring event can be excluded)." This is a meaningful improvement for borrowers with a documented one-time disruption.

### 5.7 RSU, RSA, PSU, ESPP, and Stock Compensation

Restricted stock units (RSUs), restricted stock awards (RSAs), and similar equity compensation are increasingly common in tech, defense contractor, and large-corporate employment. Treatment varies more across loan types than any other income category.

Conventional treatment per Fannie Mae B3-3.3-07 (Restricted Stock Units and Restricted Stock Employment Income) (https://selling-guide.fanniemae.com/sel/b3-3.3-07/restricted-stock-units-and-restricted-stock-employment-income), as updated by SEL-2023-11 effective for applications dated 03-01-2024 or later (https://corr.pennymac.com/non-delegated-announcements/announcement-24-16):

- **Time-based restricted stock:** minimum 12 months of restricted stock income from the current employer; continuance must be documented
- **Performance-based restricted stock:** minimum 24 months of restricted stock income; 12 to 24 months may be acceptable with positive offsetting factors
- **Continuance requirement:** the restricted stock must be publicly traded company stock; the vesting schedule must show at least 36 months of forward distributions remaining
- **Documentation:** vesting schedule from employer, two years of vested-and-distributed history (or one year for time-based), brokerage statements showing receipt, employer offer letter or compensation summary, recent paystub showing year-to-date receipt
- **Stock price calculation (Fannie Mae):** 200-day moving average of company stock price as of application date, multiplied by total shares distributed in the prior 24 months, divided by 24 months

Freddie Mac follows a similar framework but uses the 52-week average stock price rather than the 200-day moving average (per https://bpfund.com/how-fannie-mae-and-freddie-mac-treat-rsu-income-in-2025/ and the underlying Freddie Mac Section 5303.4).

Important caveats:

- **FHA, VA, USDA generally do not allow RSU income** for qualification. This pushes RSU-heavy borrowers toward conventional or jumbo programs.
- **Sign-on bonuses paid in restricted stock that vest over any length of time cannot be used as qualifying income** (Fannie Mae explicit rule, SEL-2023-11)
- **Non-publicly-traded company stock is generally not eligible** under conventional rules; some non-QM and jumbo programs may consider it with substantial documentation
- **Unvested or restricted shares cannot be counted;** only vested and distributed RSUs count

Jumbo investor portfolios sometimes use a current-stock-price-discounted-to-75% method or follow agency rules. Some non-QM programs treat RSU income more flexibly than agency. RSU income treatment varies more than any other income category across lender programs; do not assume one lender's rule applies broadly.

ESPP (Employee Stock Purchase Plan) is treated similarly to other variable income with 12 to 24 month history requirements and continuance analysis, depending on plan structure. Performance Stock Units (PSUs) generally follow performance-based RSU rules (24 months).

### 5.8 Tip Income, Gratuity, and Cash Income

Reported tip income on W-2 (line 7) is typically counted as qualifying income with a two-year history of receipt and documentation similar to base wages. Unreported tip income is not counted (and is generally a tax compliance issue separate from mortgage qualification; route to a CPA). This is a structural limitation in industries with high cash-tip income (food service, hospitality, personal services) and no clean alternative absent a non-QM bank statement program that captures the deposits.

### 5.9 Other Variable Income Types

- **Royalty income** (book, music, IP licensing): typically requires two years of receipt documented on Schedule E plus continuance documentation. Royalty contracts must show forward continuance.
- **Note income** (seller-financed notes held by the borrower): typically counted with two years of receipt history and the note documentation showing remaining term.
- **Mineral rights and oil / gas royalty:** typically two years of Schedule E history plus documentation of remaining reserves where applicable.
- **Side-business income that has not been fully documented on tax returns:** generally cannot be used; the documentation gap is the qualification gap.
- **Annuity, pension, or retirement income** (Fannie Mae B3-3.4-03 as restructured 03-04-2026, https://selling-guide.fanniemae.com/sel/b3-3.4-03/...) is treated as continuing income with documentation showing the source and amount.

### 5.10 Variable Income Pitfalls

- Assuming all bonus income counts when only documented and continuing bonus does
- Failing to document the bonus, commission, or overtime structure with an employer letter, then having the underwriter request it under timeline pressure
- Trying to use 12 months of variable income when the program requires 24
- Recent receipt of variable income with no prior history (a bonus newly introduced in the current year cannot typically be counted)
- Variable income from a recent job change (the prior employer's variable income does not transfer; the new employer's variable income needs its own history)

## 6. Relocation and Job-Change Scenarios

### 6.1 The Standard Two-Year Employment History Rule

Conventional, FHA, VA, and USDA all require a two-year employment history (or two-year history of training plus current employment for recent graduates). The rules are similar in shape, different in detail.

- **Fannie Mae B3-3.2-02 Standards for Employment-Related Income (03/04/2026)** added a new section on Job Changes, replacing prior guidance on individuals who change jobs frequently (https://singlefamily.fanniemae.com/originating-underwriting/income-assessment-simplified)
- **FHA Handbook 4000.1 Section II.A.4** requires "the most recent two years of employment and income"; the employment does not have to be with the same employer; gaps are addressed separately
- **VA Pamphlet 26-7 Chapter 4** requires verification of "a minimum of 2 years employment"; if the applicant has been with the present employer less than 2 years, prior employment plus present employment must cover a total of 2 years
- **USDA HB-1-3555 Chapter 9** requires similar two-year analysis

"Same line of work" considerations: A job change within the same industry or with similar responsibilities is treated more favorably than a major career change. Education that bridged a transition typically counts toward continuity rather than as a gap. A "major career change" can effectively restart the two-year clock.

### 6.2 New Job: Already Started

Documentation:

- Current pay stub(s) covering the most recent 30 days at the new employer
- Employment offer letter
- Prior-employer documentation if total employment with current employer is less than two years
- Written or third-party verification of employment

Probationary period: some lenders require completion of probationary period before closing; others accept the offer letter and current paystub. Per Fannie Mae's Top Trending Selling FAQ: "If the probationary period is described only as a standard post-hire employment term (and does not delay the start date or change the stated pay terms needed for qualifying), it is not considered a contingency for employment."

The "I just started two weeks ago" scenario: typically requires the borrower's initial paystub plus an employer letter, plus documentation that the prior employment can be paired with the new to establish continuity. Some lenders are more comfortable here than others.

### 6.3 Pre-Start-Date Employment (Offer Letter Path)

This is the highest-leverage subsection for relocation scenarios. The conventional path is documented in Fannie Mae B3-3.3-03 Employment Offers or Contracts (03/04/2026) (https://selling-guide.fanniemae.com/sel/b3-3.3-03/employment-offers-or-contracts) and the parallel Freddie Mac framework.

Per the Enact summary of Fannie Mae's two options (https://blog.enactmi.com/post/how-to-verify-future-income-for-borrowers-mid-job-move):

**Option 1:** A fully executed employment contract or offer letter outlining position, salary, and start date is in the file. Prior to delivery of the loan to Fannie Mae, the lender must obtain a paystub from the borrower that provides sufficient information supporting the income used to qualify based on the offer or contract.

**Option 2:** When a paystub cannot be produced before closing, a fully executed contract for future employment is required. The contract must:

- Clearly identify the employer and borrower and be signed by both
- Clearly identify the terms of employment including position, type and rate of pay, and start date
- Be non-contingent (if conditions of employment exist, the lender must confirm all conditions are satisfied prior to closing the loan)

Option 2 only applies to one-unit, principal-residence purchase transactions.

Documentation requirements typically include:

- Fully executed offer letter (signed by both employer and borrower)
- Departmental confirmation of the position and start date
- Paystub before closing if possible (Option 1)
- For Option 2, additional reserve requirements may apply (some lenders require six months of PITIA in reserves to cover the gap between closing and first paycheck)

Cross-references:

- **VA active-duty PCS:** an active-duty service member with PCS orders has a parallel framework using the orders, the LES, and a Statement of Service. The PCS orders document the move, the LES documents current military pay, and the Statement of Service documents the rank, time in service, and likelihood of continuance.
- **Medical professional programs:** physician loans and other professional programs typically extend pre-start-date allowance further than conventional rules, sometimes allowing closing 60 to 90 days before the start date with no first paystub required, in exchange for stricter LTV, reserve, and credit requirements. Cross-reference the medical professional mechanics file.
- **VA Lender's Handbook Chapter 4** requires that for service members within 12 months of release from active duty, additional documentation is needed: a verifiable extension of active duty service, a written civilian job offer with earnings information, significant compensating underwriting factors (such as a 10% down payment or substantial cash reserves), or a written statement of intent to re-enlist with confirmation from the commanding officer.

### 6.4 Employment Gaps

- **FHA Handbook 4000.1 Section II.A.4:** For borrowers with gaps in employment of six months or more (an "extended absence"), FHA may consider the borrower's current income as Effective Income if the lender can verify the borrower has been employed in the current job for at least six months at the time of case number assignment AND there is a two-year work history prior to the absence using standard or alternative employment verification (per https://www.fha.com/fha_article?id=620 paraphrasing HUD 4000.1).
- **FHA on frequent job changes:** if the borrower has changed jobs more than three times in the previous 12-month period, or has changed lines of work, additional verification is required.
- **Conventional, VA, USDA:** apply gap analysis but do not have the FHA's specific six-month threshold codified the same way; underwriter judgment governs, with letters of explanation typically required.

Gaps for documented reasons (education, medical leave, family leave, military service, parental leave) are treated more favorably than voluntary unemployment. Documentation of the reason is essential.

### 6.5 Remote Work and Cross-State Employment

Remote work has become common since 2020. Lender comfort with remote employment varies. Some lenders require:

- Documentation of the employer's remote-work policy
- Documentation that the borrower's specific role is remote
- Documentation of the borrower's intended state of residence

Federal civilian remote work has its own framework under the Telework Enhancement Act of 2010. A federal civilian working remotely for a federal employer in another state is generally treated like any other federal civilian employee.

Alabama-resident, employer-elsewhere scenarios are common in Huntsville given the federal civilian and contractor workforce; cross-reference the local context file for specifics.

### 6.6 Job Changes Within the Same Line of Work

Lateral moves at higher pay, promotions within the same employer, and industry-standard career trajectory moves are generally treated favorably. The underwriter focuses on:

- Whether the new role is in the same line of work or a related occupation
- Whether the income is stable or increasing
- Whether the borrower's experience supports the new role

### 6.7 Major Career Changes

A move from one industry to another generally restarts the two-year clock unless education or training bridged the change. The borrower's case is strongest when:

- Education (degree, certification, training program) directly prepared the borrower for the new role
- The new role is at the same or higher compensation than the prior role
- The borrower has been in the new role long enough to demonstrate stability

Non-QM bank statement alternatives can fill the gap for borrowers who cannot satisfy the two-year rule.

### 6.8 Federal and Defense Contractor Employment Context

Federal civilian career trajectory:

- General Schedule (GS) scale with periodic step increases
- Locality pay supplementing base
- Performance awards (typically not used as qualifying income unless documented as continuing)

Defense contractor career trajectory:

- Often includes RSU, performance bonus, and contract-based employment
- Security clearance dynamics matter: clearance changes, contract gaps between primes, contract pivots

The contractor-to-government transition (or vice versa) is an income-source change and a potentially income-amount change. The underwriter analyzes the new income on its own merits, with prior employment supporting the two-year history.

These dynamics matter heavily for North Alabama complex-borrower content. Cross-reference the local context file.

### 6.9 Self-Employed Pivoting to W-2 (or Vice Versa)

The two-year clock implications:

- Recent W-2 to self-employed: typically requires waiting until two years of self-employment returns are filed, unless the prior W-2 work was in the same line of work and the Fannie Mae one-year exception applies
- Recent self-employed to W-2: typically allows the W-2 income to be used once a paystub and verbal VOE confirm employment, although the underwriter may still scrutinize the recent transition

The "same line of work" question matters when a business owner takes a W-2 role in their industry: a former S-corp owner of a CPA firm taking a W-2 role at a larger CPA firm is generally a smoother transition than a former retail business owner taking a W-2 role in software development.

### 6.10 Common Relocation Pitfalls

- Closing too far in advance of start date (pushing into territory where the offer letter pathway no longer fits)
- Closing with an unsigned or contingent offer letter
- A start-date change between contract signing and closing (re-disclosure may be required, lender re-verification of income source typically required)
- A compensation change between offer and closing (re-verification, possibly re-running through DU or LPA)
- Failing to budget for the cash gap to first paycheck (the gap between closing and first paycheck can be 30 to 60 days; the borrower's reserves must cover housing, moving, and living expenses through that window)
- Spouse-not-relocating scenarios where one spouse stays at the old job (occupancy intent must be clear; the relocating spouse must intend to occupy the new home as a primary residence)

## 7. Multiple Properties and the Retained-Home Scenario

### 7.1 The Conventional 10-Financed-Properties Rule

Fannie Mae B2-2-03 Multiple Financed Properties for the Same Borrower (last updated 11-05-2025, https://selling-guide.fanniemae.com/sel/b2-2-03/multiple-financed-properties-same-borrower) caps the borrower at 10 financed properties. The number-of-financed-properties calculation includes:

- The total number of properties financed (not the number of mortgages on a property, nor the number of mortgages sold to Fannie Mae). Multi-unit properties (such as a two-unit) count as one property
- The borrower's principal residence if it is financed
- The cumulative total for all borrowers (jointly financed properties are only counted once)

For HomeReady loans, financed properties owned by a non-occupant co-borrower that are owned separately from the borrower are excluded from the financed-properties count.

Property types not subject to the 10-property limit include certain commercial properties, farms, multifamily 5+ unit properties, and certain ownership structures where the borrower is not personally obligated on the mortgage.

Borrowers above the 10-property cap typically need to look at jumbo investor portfolios, DSCR programs, or commercial financing for additional properties.

### 7.2 Departing Residence: Sell, Rent, or Hold Vacant

When a borrower buys a new primary residence and currently owns a home, three options apply to the departing residence:

1. Sell before or concurrent with closing on the new home
2. Retain as a rental
3. Retain vacant or as a second home

Each has distinct underwriting implications.

### 7.3 Departing Residence Sold Before Closing

The cleanest scenario underwriting-wise. Documentation:

- HUD-1 / Closing Disclosure of the sale showing distribution of proceeds
- Cash-from-sale as a documented source of funds for the new purchase

Timing risk when the sale and purchase are not concurrent: the borrower may be carrying both PITIA payments for some period, which has cash flow and DTI implications. Some closing strategies use a sale contingency or simultaneous closings to manage this.

### 7.4 Departing Residence Converting to Rental: Conventional Rules

This is the highest-leverage subsection. Per Fannie Mae B3-3.8-01 Rental Income (last updated 10-08-2025, https://selling-guide.fanniemae.com/sel/b3-3.8-01/rental-income) and the related Fannie Mae policy update:

**Important update on the 30% equity rule:** Fannie Mae has eliminated the requirement specifically associated with 30% equity for the conversion of a principal residence to a rental property. Per the lender summary in https://homeloanartist.com/move-up-buyers-converting-primary-to-rental-income/ (which describes the Fannie Mae change) and corroborated by the current B3-3.8-01 text and broader policy commentary at https://trussfinancialgroup.com/blog/fannie-mae-rental-income-rules: standard rental income and financial reserve requirements now apply when the borrower converts a current principal residence to an investment property. Some lender overlays still impose a 30% equity floor; the agency rule no longer requires it. Confirm with your specific lender's overlays.

The current Fannie Mae documentation set for a departing residence converting to rental:

- Fully executed lease agreement of at least 12 months
- Evidence the lease has gone into effect: two consecutive months of bank statements or electronic transfers showing rental payments for existing leases, OR copies of the security deposit and first full month's rent check with proof of deposit for newly executed agreements
- Form 1007 (Single-Family Comparable Rent Schedule) provided in conjunction with the appraisal of the departing property, or Form 1025 for two-to-four-unit properties

The 75% vacancy / expense factor: Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac apply 75% to the gross monthly rent (lease amount or market rent, whichever is more conservative) when the borrower does not have a history of receipt on the property. The remaining 25% is treated as the expected vacancy and operating expense factor.

When rental income cannot be counted (insufficient documentation, lease not in effect, equity threshold not met under lender overlay, recently signed lease without supporting paper trail), both PITIA payments must be qualified by the borrower's income alone. This is the "qualifying with both housing payments" scenario.

### 7.5 Departing Residence Converting to Rental: FHA Rules

FHA retains a 25% equity requirement and a 100-mile relocation rule for rental income from a departing residence. Per Handbook 4000.1 Section II.A.4 / II.A.5 (paraphrased at https://innovativemtgbrokers.com/understanding-fhas-100-mile-rule/ and https://www.fha.com/fha_article?id=806):

To use rental income from a property the borrower is vacating, FHA requires:

- The borrower must be relocating to an area more than 100 miles from the borrower's current principal residence
- A lease agreement of at least one year's duration after the mortgage is closed
- Evidence of the payment of the security deposit or first month's rent
- Where the borrower does not have a history of rental income for the property since the previous tax filing, including property being vacated by the borrower, the lender must obtain an appraisal evidencing market rent and that the borrower has at least 25% equity in the property. The appraisal is not required to be completed by an FHA Roster Appraiser.

Calculation of effective net rental income from other real estate holdings where the borrower does not have a history of rental income: deduct PITI from 75% of the lesser of fair market rent reported by the appraiser or the lease amount.

This is where FHA and conventional diverge significantly. A borrower retaining a current home as a rental and buying within 100 miles of that home generally cannot use FHA's rental income rule for that departing property, even with substantial equity, unless the equity test path applies under the alternative reading of the rule.

### 7.6 Departing Residence Converting to Rental: VA Rules

VA Lender's Handbook Chapter 4 Section 4.04 addresses prospective rental income from a departing property. Generally, VA allows prospective rental income only to offset the mortgage payment on the rental property, not to be included in effective income. The lender obtains a working knowledge of the local rental market; if there is a strong rental market, prospective rental income may be considered for offset purposes even without a lease.

### 7.7 Departing Residence Converting to Rental: USDA Rules

USDA HB-1-3555 Chapter 9 generally requires rental income to be documented on Schedule E for prior history; departing residence treatment is more conservative than Fannie Mae's current rule and generally requires a lease and security deposit / first month's rent paper trail.

### 7.8 Departing Residence Held Vacant or as a Second Home

Both PITIA payments must be qualified by the borrower's income alone. The cash flow stress test is real; the DTI analysis must include both housing payments plus all other monthly debt service. This is viable when the borrower has substantial income and strong reserves; it is not viable when the borrower's qualifying income just barely supports the new property's PITIA on its own.

For an existing principal residence converting to a second home (rather than rental), Fannie Mae per B3-6-06 Qualifying Impact of Other Real Estate Owned (https://selling-guide.fanniemae.com/sel/b3-6-06/qualifying-impact-other-real-estate-owned) requires the PITIA of the second home to be counted as part of the borrower's recurring monthly debt obligations.

### 7.9 Existing Rental Properties

Schedule E history requirements: typically two years of rental receipts and expenses on Schedule E. Rental income is calculated from Schedule E by:

- Starting with gross rents
- Subtracting actual expenses (or applying 75% factor in lieu of detailed expense documentation in some cases)
- Adding back depreciation, mortgage interest (which is already counted as the PITIA), insurance, and taxes
- Arriving at net rental income for qualification purposes

Fannie Mae provides Form 1037 (Principal Residence, 2-4 unit), Form 1038 (Individual Rental Income from Investment Properties up to 4), Form 1038A (up to 10), and Form 1039 (Business Rental Income from Investment Properties).

### 7.10 Reserve Requirements for Multiple Properties

Per Fannie Mae B3-4.1-01 Minimum Reserve Requirements (https://selling-guide.fanniemae.com/sel/b3-4.1-01/minimum-reserve-requirements):

When the borrower owns other financed properties, additional reserves are required as a percentage of the aggregate UPB on those other financed properties:

- 2% of aggregate UPB if the borrower has 1 to 4 financed properties
- 4% of aggregate UPB if the borrower has 5 to 6 financed properties
- 6% of aggregate UPB if the borrower has 7 to 10 financed properties (DU only)

These are layered on top of the standard reserve requirement on the subject transaction. VA and FHA reserve requirements differ; jumbo investor portfolios typically scale reserves more aggressively than agency.

### 7.11 Bridge Financing

Bridge financing options for borrowers retaining a departing property:

- HELOC on the departing residence: a home equity line of credit drawn on the existing primary residence, used to provide cash for the new home's down payment, with the HELOC repaid from sale proceeds or refinanced later. Underwriting question: does the HELOC payment count in DTI? Most loan types include the required minimum payment in DTI.
- Bridge loan from a portfolio lender: a short-term loan secured by the existing home, repaid at sale. Bridge loan payments are typically included in DTI.
- Cross-collateralized loans: rare in current market but available from some portfolio lenders.

When bridge financing makes sense: when the borrower needs the equity in the existing home for the new home's down payment, the existing home is reasonably likely to sell within the bridge term, and the borrower can carry both housing payments plus the bridge loan payment for the bridge term.

When bridge financing creates more problems than it solves: when the existing home does not sell quickly, when the bridge loan payment pushes DTI past the new loan's limits, when the bridge loan rate is materially higher than alternatives, or when the borrower cannot carry both housing payments plus the bridge.

### 7.12 Sale Contingency vs Non-Contingent Offer Planning

Sale contingency: protects the buyer if the existing home does not sell, but materially weakens the offer in a competitive market. Many sellers reject sale-contingent offers categorically.

Non-contingent offer: strengthens the offer but requires the buyer to qualify for both PITIA payments simultaneously, or to use bridge financing, HELOC, retained-home rental qualification, or gift funds to bridge the gap.

The financing alternatives that make non-contingent offers viable:

- Bridge loan or HELOC on departing residence
- Retained-home rental qualification (under whichever rule applies)
- Gift funds to cover the down payment without the existing home's equity
- Substantial cash reserves that allow both PITIA payments on the borrower's income alone

### 7.13 Common Multiple-Property Pitfalls

- Assuming retained-home rental income will count without lease documentation, security deposit, and Form 1007
- Failing to budget reserves for multiple properties
- Underestimating the cash flow stress of dual PITIA during the gap between closings
- Sale contingency offers in a competitive market that lose to non-contingent buyers
- Bridge loan timing miscalculation (the existing home does not sell within the bridge term)
- Property management arrangements that do not satisfy underwriter documentation (informal management by a family member without lease, deposit, or paper trail)

## 8. Asset Documentation: Gift Funds, Large Deposits, Retirement and Brokerage

### 8.1 The Standard Asset Documentation Window

The standard asset documentation window across conventional, FHA, VA, and USDA is approximately the most recent two months of full-page bank statements (every page, including blank pages and account-summary pages), supplemented by year-to-date documentation for accounts where the most recent statement is older. Fannie Mae's specific framework lives in B3-4.2-02 Depository Accounts and the broader B3-4 Asset Assessment chapter.

Verification of Deposit (VOD) is an alternative documentation method in some cases, but most lenders use bank statements as the primary documentation. The DU validation service can validate assets from a third-party verification report, in which case the lender is not required to obtain bank statements directly.

### 8.2 Large Deposit Sourcing

Per Fannie Mae B3-4.2-02 Depository Accounts (https://selling-guide.fanniemae.com/sel/b3-4.2-02/depository-accounts):

> A large deposit is defined as a single deposit that exceeds 50% of the total monthly qualifying income for the loan. When bank statements (typically covering the most recent two months) are used, the lender must evaluate large deposits.

The 50% threshold is the conventional rule. Worth noting:

- If the source of a large deposit is readily identifiable on the account statement (direct deposit from employer, Social Security, IRS or state tax refund, transfer between verified accounts), no further documentation is required
- The DU validation service automates the assessment of large deposits
- Compliance with DU messages satisfies the requirement

FHA's rule was updated to align: per the summary at https://homesteadfinancial.com/purchase/fha-guideline-updates-2024/, FHA changed from a 1%-of-purchase-price threshold to "anything larger than 50% of the total gross monthly income." Example: at $8,000 monthly gross income, deposits up to $4,000 do not require sourcing.

VA and USDA generally apply lender practice in line with the 50% threshold, with underwriter judgment on what constitutes a "large" deposit relative to the borrower's normal account activity.

Common sources and documentation:

- **Paycheck deposits:** matched to pay stub showing the same amount and date
- **Tax refund:** IRS notice, filed return, or direct deposit reference
- **Sale of personal property:** bill of sale, photos of the item, cleared check
- **Internal transfer between borrower's own accounts:** statements from both accounts showing the matching debit and credit
- **Sale of investments:** brokerage transaction confirmation and trade confirmation
- **Insurance settlement:** settlement documentation from the insurer
- **Loan proceeds against a secured asset (HELOC, 401(k) loan, etc.):** loan documentation
- **Cash deposits:** generally cannot be sourced and may be excluded from the qualifying balance entirely

The "let it season" strategy: depositing funds 60+ days before the most recent statement removes the deposit from the documentation window. This is the cleanest path for borrowers who anticipate a complex deposit (a private sale, a family transfer, etc.) and have time to plan.

### 8.3 Gift Funds

Per Fannie Mae B3-4.3-04 Personal Gifts (last updated 02-04-2026, https://selling-guide.fanniemae.com/sel/b3-4.3-04/personal-gifts):

> A borrower of a mortgage loan secured by a principal residence or second home may use funds received as a personal gift from an acceptable donor. Gift funds may fund all or part of the down payment, closing costs, or financial reserves subject to the minimum borrower contribution requirements below. Gifts are not allowed on an investment property.

Acceptable donors per Fannie Mae:

- A relative defined as the borrower's spouse, child, or other dependent, or any other individual related to the borrower by blood, marriage, adoption, or legal guardianship
- A non-relative who shares a familial relationship with the borrower defined as a domestic partner (or relative of the domestic partner), individual engaged to marry the borrower, former relative, or an individual with a long-standing familial-like or mentorship relationship

The donor may not be, or have any affiliation with, the builder, developer, real estate agent, or any other interested party to the transaction. Gift funds from a seller who is also an acceptable donor and not affiliated with any other interested party are allowed.

Minimum borrower contribution per Fannie Mae:

- For one-unit principal residence at 80% LTV or below: gift funds may cover the entire transaction; no minimum borrower contribution required
- For one-unit principal residence at LTV greater than 80%: gift funds may cover the entire transaction; no minimum borrower contribution required (this changed from a prior 5% rule for higher-LTV loans on 2-4 unit properties)
- For 2-4 unit principal residence: a 5% minimum borrower contribution from the borrower's own funds is generally required

Gift letter requirements per Fannie Mae:

- Signed by the donor and borrower
- Must specify the dollar amount
- Must state the funds are a gift with no expectation of repayment
- Must include donor's name, address, telephone, and relationship to the borrower

Documentation of donor's ability and transfer:

- A copy of the donor's check and the borrower's deposit slip, OR
- A copy of the donor's withdrawal slip and the borrower's deposit slip, OR
- Evidence of the electronic transfer of funds from the donor's account to the borrower's account, OR
- A copy of the donor's bank statement showing sufficient funds for the amount of the gift, paired with evidence of receipt

Cohabitating donor exception: when a gift comes from an acceptable donor who has lived with the borrower for the past 12 months, the gift is considered the borrower's own funds and may be used to satisfy the minimum borrower contribution requirement, provided both individuals will use the home being purchased as their principal residence. Documentation includes a certification letter from the donor and supporting evidence of shared residency.

Wedding gift exception: Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac permit wedding gifts from related and unrelated persons as an eligible source of funds for a principal residence mortgage if documented as on deposit in the borrower's account within 90 days of the documented marriage license or certificate. Freddie Mac additionally permits graduation gift funds.

**FHA gift fund rules** (Handbook 4000.1 Section II.A.4 / II.A.5):

- Gifts are defined as contributions of cash or equity with no expectation of repayment
- Acceptable donors: family member, employer or labor union, close friend with a clearly defined and documented interest, charitable organization, governmental agency or public entity providing homeownership assistance to low or moderate income families or first-time homebuyers
- Documentation per FHA Info 2024-30 (https://corr.pennymac.com/announcements/announcement-24-66): donor's bank statement showing the withdrawal and evidence of deposit into the borrower's account, OR donor's canceled check (front and back) and evidence of deposit, OR donor's withdrawal receipt and evidence of deposit, OR evidence of electronic transfer of funds from donor to borrower
- Gift letter requirements parallel to conventional but with FHA-specific elements

**VA gift fund rules** (Pamphlet 26-7 Chapter 4): gifts are allowed with documentation of source and transfer; donor restrictions are similar to FHA in spirit.

**USDA gift fund rules** (HB-1-3555 Chapter 10): gifts from acceptable donors are allowed with documentation similar to other agency programs.

**Jumbo and non-QM gift fund rules:** vary widely by program. Many require a minimum borrower contribution (5% to 10%) before gift funds can be used.

Tax implications for the donor (route to a CPA):

- Annual gift tax exclusion (IRS publishes the current annual exclusion amount; the figure is adjusted periodically)
- Lifetime exemption
- Gift tax return filing requirements on Form 709 for gifts above the annual exclusion

### 8.4 Family Lender Loans (Not Gifts)

A family lender loan is structurally different from a gift: there is an expectation of repayment. The required documentation:

- Signed promissory note specifying the loan amount, interest rate, repayment schedule, and security
- The monthly payment included in the borrower's DTI calculation
- The funds documented in the bank statement paper trail similar to any other source

Trade-off: cleaner family record-keeping versus added DTI burden. When a family member would otherwise want to be repaid at all, structuring the funds as a loan rather than a gift can prevent later disputes; when the family member is genuinely making a gift, structuring the funds as a gift is cleaner because the gift does not create a DTI liability.

Family-lender loans cannot replace the borrower's minimum contribution or be used to circumvent gift fund rules; the loan must be a real loan with documented repayment.

### 8.5 Retirement Account Distributions

401(k), 403(b), 457, IRA, Roth IRA, SEP-IRA, and SIMPLE-IRA accounts can be sources of cash-to-close. Each is treated separately.

Using retirement assets as a documented source of cash-to-close:

- Documentation of the distribution (account statement showing the withdrawal)
- Documentation of receipt (deposit shown on the borrower's account)
- For IRA and Roth IRA distributions, documentation that the borrower has actually accessed the funds
- For 401(k) distributions, documentation of the distribution and of the borrower's continued employment if the distribution was a hardship distribution or in-service withdrawal

Tax implications (route to a CPA):

- Pre-59½ distributions from traditional 401(k), 403(b), 457, traditional IRA, and SEP-IRA typically incur a 10% early withdrawal penalty plus ordinary income tax
- Roth IRA contributions can typically be withdrawn tax- and penalty-free; earnings have separate rules
- The tax cost of liquidating retirement assets to fund cash-to-close can be substantial; the borrower should model the after-tax math before liquidating

401(k) loans as cash-to-close alternative:

- Allowed up to 50% of vested balance or $50,000, whichever is less, per IRS rules
- The 401(k) loan does not trigger the early withdrawal penalty as long as the loan is repaid on schedule
- Treatment of the 401(k) loan payment in DTI varies by loan type:
  - **Conventional (Fannie Mae):** typically excluded from DTI per Fannie Mae's general approach to obligations secured by the borrower's own retirement assets (verify current rule)
  - **VA:** commonly excluded
  - **FHA:** verify current Handbook 4000.1 rule; treatment has shifted historically
  - **Non-QM and jumbo:** commonly included in DTI as a recurring debt obligation

The common pitfall: liquidating retirement assets without modeling the tax consequence. A $50,000 traditional 401(k) distribution at age 45 in a 24% marginal tax bracket can mean roughly $12,000 in federal income tax plus $5,000 in early withdrawal penalty plus state tax, leaving roughly $30,000 of usable cash. Route to a CPA before pulling the trigger.

### 8.6 Brokerage and Non-Retirement Investment Accounts

Treatment as cash-to-close: requires documentation of liquidation and transfer to the borrower's account.

Treatment as reserves (rather than as cash-to-close):

- Stocks, bonds, mutual funds: per Fannie Mae's prior rule (preserved in current practice), 100% of the vested value of the asset is allowed when determining available reserves, with documentation that the value of the asset is at least 20% more than the funds needed for the borrower's down payment and closing costs in some cases. Per the lender summary at http://www.billprovost.com/fannie-mae-update/.
- The lender typically applies a haircut to reflect market risk; conventional commonly counts 100%, jumbo and non-QM may count 60% to 80% of the value

Tax implications of capital gains realization (route to a CPA): selling appreciated securities triggers capital gains tax at long-term or short-term rates; the after-tax cash from a sale is less than the sale proceeds.

Concentrated single-stock positions: lenders may apply additional haircuts or require documentation of risk management when a borrower's brokerage holdings are heavily concentrated in a single stock.

### 8.7 Asset Depletion (Non-QM)

Asset depletion programs (sometimes called asset utilization or asset-based qualifying) convert the borrower's liquid assets into a synthetic monthly income for qualification purposes. Typical structure:

- Eligible assets: liquid retirement (counted at 60% to 70% of value typically, given taxes and penalties), brokerage and investment accounts (counted at 70% to 100%), cash (counted at 100%)
- Divisor: assets divided by remaining loan term in months (360 for a 30-year loan, sometimes 240 or 180 depending on program), or assets divided by a shorter period like 5 to 10 years
- Result: a monthly synthetic income added to or replacing the borrower's actual monthly income

Asset depletion is the standard non-QM solution for asset-rich, current-income-light borrowers (retirees, founders post-exit, individuals with significant inherited wealth). Programs vary widely; structure and divisor are program-specific.

### 8.8 Business Funds for Personal Cash-to-Close

When a self-employed borrower uses funds from the business to fund a personal cash-to-close, conventional rules typically require:

- A CPA letter confirming withdrawal of the funds will not adversely affect the business
- Documentation of the borrower's ownership percentage
- Documentation of the source of funds within the business (consistent with normal business operations)

The risk: withdrawing significant business funds to fund a personal home purchase can affect the underwriter's analysis of business stability and income continuance. The lender's analysis of qualifying income depends on the business's ongoing ability to generate income; a large withdrawal that materially impairs the business undermines the income calculation.

### 8.9 Trust Assets and Trust Beneficiary Distributions

Documentation of trust structure:

- Trust documents (legally filed or recognized)
- Documentation of the borrower's status as beneficiary
- Documentation of distribution history and likelihood of continuance

Distributions counted as income (with continuance) or as assets:

- When distributions are documented as recurring (e.g., quarterly distributions from a family trust), they may be counted as continuing income with appropriate documentation
- When distributions are one-time or irregular, they typically count as assets only

The complexity of irrevocable versus revocable trusts: irrevocable trusts have stronger separation between trustor and beneficiary; revocable trusts (like a typical living trust) are more flexible but may complicate the analysis of who actually owns the assets.

Route trust-structure questions to an attorney.

### 8.10 Crypto, Digital Assets, and Alternative Holdings

Treatment varies wildly by loan type and lender:

- **Most agency programs (Fannie Mae, Freddie Mac, FHA, VA, USDA):** require liquidation to USD and seasoning before the funds count. The seasoning is typically 60+ days for the funds to fully exit the asset documentation window. Per Fannie Mae B3-3.8-01 (rental income context): "The rental payment on the lease must be reflected in U.S. dollars (cannot be in virtual currency)." Crypto holdings themselves are generally not counted as reserves under agency programs.
- **Some non-QM programs:** accept crypto holdings as qualifying assets with documentation, sometimes applying a haircut for volatility
- **Volatility and valuation challenge:** crypto values fluctuate; the lender's documentation of the asset value at a single point in time may not reflect the value at closing

Documentation requirements when crypto is liquidated for cash-to-close:

- Statements from the exchange showing the holdings before liquidation
- Documentation of the liquidation transaction
- Documentation of the transfer of USD funds to a verified bank account
- Bank statement showing receipt of the funds

The pitfall: liquidating crypto too late in the process, leaving the funds in transit at closing, or failing to document the chain of custody.

### 8.11 Foreign Assets

Documentation of foreign-held assets:

- Account statements from the foreign institution
- Currency conversion documentation
- Documentation that the funds have been transferred to a U.S. account before closing (most lenders require this)

Country-specific scrutiny: some lenders will not accept assets from certain jurisdictions (countries on sanctions lists, countries with significant money-laundering risk). OFAC and FinCEN compliance applies.

Route foreign-asset and international-tax questions to a tax advisor experienced with international clients.

### 8.12 Common Asset-Documentation Pitfalls

- Cash deposits that cannot be sourced and are excluded from the qualifying balance
- Family transfers documented as gifts when they are really loans (or vice versa), creating documentation inconsistency
- Liquidating retirement positions without modeling the tax consequence
- Moving money between accounts in the 60-day window without paper trail
- Brokerage funds that look available but are tied up in unsettled trades
- Business funds used personally without CPA documentation
- Crypto liquidation timing relative to closing (funds in transit at closing, or liquidation that fails the seasoning test)
- Foreign assets that have not been transferred to a U.S. account by closing

## 9. Offer Strategy for Complex Borrowers

### 9.1 Why Complex Buyers Should Plan Earlier

Complex pre-approval is not a same-day exercise. The typical timeline:

- Initial conversation and document request: day 1
- Borrower gathers and uploads documents: 1 to 2 weeks for a clean borrower; longer if items need to be obtained from a CPA, prior employer, or financial institution
- Lender's full review of documents, credit pull, AUS run: several days
- Conditional underwriting review: 5 to 10 business days
- Conditions cleared and pre-approval letter issued: variable

Total commonly 2 to 4 weeks for a complex pre-approval, sometimes longer when self-employed or K-1 income requires CPA involvement.

The risk of incomplete pre-approval at the moment a property hits the market: the borrower writes an offer based on a "we can probably get you to X" conversation rather than a verified pre-approval. The offer is weaker, the pre-approval may not hold under contract, and the closing timeline is at risk.

The cost of writing offers that fall through during underwriting: lost earnest money in some contracts, lost momentum, market re-entry costs as inventory shifts.

### 9.2 Verified-Income, Verified-Asset (VIVA) Pre-Approval

The hierarchy of pre-approval depth:

- **Pre-qualification:** based on self-reported information; the weakest form
- **Pre-approval:** based on a credit pull and an AUS run, but income and assets may not be fully verified
- **Verified-income, verified-asset (VIVA) pre-approval:** based on full documentation review, credit pull, AUS run, and conditional underwriting; the strongest form before a specific property is identified

Why complex buyers benefit most from VIVA pre-approval: the complex elements (self-employment income calculation, K-1 distributions, retained-home rental income, gift fund documentation) are exactly the elements that surface unexpected issues during underwriting. A VIVA pre-approval that has already worked through those issues produces an offer the buyer can stand behind in a competitive market.

What VIVA pre-approval requires:

- Full documentation review (tax returns, business returns, bank statements, asset statements, employer letters, gift letters, etc.)
- Credit pull and credit analysis
- AUS run (DU or LPA)
- Conditional underwriting review where the underwriter has reviewed the file and identified any conditions before the property is identified

The pre-approval expiration timeline is typically 60 to 90 days, with re-verification triggered by:

- New credit pull becoming stale (typically 120 days)
- Updated income documentation needed (a new pay stub, an updated bank statement)
- Material changes in the borrower's financial position (new tradeline, large deposit, job change)

### 9.3 Documentation Risk During the Contract Period

The change-in-circumstances triggers that derail complex files:

- **New credit pulls between pre-approval and closing:** every credit inquiry in the closing window is a flag; the borrower should avoid all unnecessary credit applications
- **New tradelines (auto loan, credit card, BNPL):** any new debt obligation between pre-approval and closing changes DTI and may require re-running through DU or LPA
- **Job change or compensation change:** triggers re-verification of employment and income; may require new offer letter or pay stubs
- **Large deposit during the contract period:** triggers sourcing analysis under the same rules as the initial application
- **Movement of assets between accounts:** must be paper-trailed
- **Tax filing during the contract period (especially for self-employed):** the new tax return may produce a different qualifying income than the prior year's; the underwriter typically re-analyzes
- **Business changes (new entity, partnership change, large client loss):** can affect the income analysis

Why the LO needs to know about every change in real time: the alternative is the LO discovering the change during pre-closing verification (verbal VOE, soft credit pull, updated bank statement), at which point the change is harder to manage under timeline pressure.

### 9.4 Appraisal Risk for Complex Files

Lower-than-expected appraisal in a complex file is a bigger problem than in a standard file because:

- The file may already be tight on DTI; reducing the loan amount changes the math
- The borrower may have committed to a specific cash-to-close based on the offer price; an appraisal gap requires more cash
- The complex elements (rental income from departing residence, jumbo investor reserve requirements) may have been calibrated to a specific loan amount

The appraisal contingency strategy: protect the buyer's earnest money with an appraisal contingency clause that allows the buyer to walk if the appraisal comes in significantly below contract.

The appraisal gap clause: a clause in the offer specifying that the buyer will cover up to a stated dollar amount of any appraisal gap. This strengthens the offer in a competitive market but requires the buyer to model cash-gap exposure.

Reconsideration of Value (ROV): the appraisal can be challenged through an ROV process; the lender submits comparable sales the appraiser may have missed. ROVs sometimes succeed, sometimes do not.

Comp scarcity in unique submarkets: cross-reference jumbo mechanics and the local-context file for Huntsville-specific dynamics.

### 9.5 Financing Contingency

The standard financing contingency in Alabama purchase contracts gives the buyer a defined window (commonly 21 days, varies by contract) to complete underwriting and confirm financing. The complex buyer's heightened need for the contingency: complex files take longer, and the contingency window is the buyer's protection if the file does not clear.

When to extend the contingency: when the file genuinely needs more time and the seller will agree. The seller's willingness depends on market dynamics and the buyer's standing (earnest money level, pre-approval depth).

The risk of waiving the contingency to win a bidding war: if the file does not clear, the buyer's earnest money is at risk and the buyer may face contract-default consequences.

### 9.6 Closing Timeline Realism

Standard closing timeline: 30 days from contract to close.

Complex closing timeline: 35 to 45 days, sometimes longer for files with unusual income structure, multiple properties, or unusual asset documentation. Specific complications add specific time:

- K-1 income with a recently filed return: typically adds 1 to 2 weeks
- Gift funds with donor in another country or with complex documentation: adds 1 to 2 weeks
- Departing residence rental qualification: adds 1 to 2 weeks while the lease, security deposit, and Form 1007 are gathered

The cost of agreeing to a tight closing the file cannot actually meet: lost earnest money on contract default, contract extension fees, seller leverage in extension negotiations.

Setting seller and Realtor expectations upfront: the buyer's agent communicates the realistic timeline to the listing agent before the offer is accepted, with the LO available to confirm.

### 9.7 Agent and Lender Communication Before the Offer

The pre-offer phone call between buyer's agent and lender typically confirms:

- Pre-approval status (VIVA versus standard pre-approval)
- Maximum purchase price and the specific loan amount the file supports
- Realistic closing timeline given the file's complexity
- Contingency duration recommendation
- Documentation in hand versus still needed

The agent confirms:

- Target property and listing agent
- Expected competition
- Seller's contingency expectations and timeline preferences

The collaboration that protects the buyer in a competitive market: when the listing agent calls the LO directly to verify the buyer's standing, the LO's preparation determines whether the offer is taken seriously.

### 9.8 The Listing Agent's Concerns About Complex Buyers

"Will this loan close" is the underlying anxiety. Listing agents have seen complex deals fall apart and naturally favor buyers whose loans look easiest.

How to address it:

- VIVA pre-approval letter from a named LO with track record
- LO available by phone to address listing agent questions
- Earnest money level appropriate to the price point (higher earnest money signals buyer commitment)
- Realistic closing timeline that the LO can defend

Why complex buyers benefit from a strong LO who is willing to talk to listing agents directly: the listing agent's comfort with the loan officer is a real factor in offer selection.

### 9.9 Multi-Offer Scenarios

Strengthening a complex offer without sacrificing buyer protections:

- Earnest money level adjustments (within reason for the buyer's risk tolerance)
- Closing timeline adjustments (offering a longer close in exchange for better price terms, when the file genuinely needs it)
- Personal letters: in many markets these are now discouraged or prohibited due to fair housing risk; verify current Realtor association guidance for North Alabama before including. The Alabama Association of Realtors and the National Association of Realtors have raised concerns about love letters; some brokerages prohibit them.
- Escalation clauses: useful in competitive bidding but should be modeled against the buyer's actual qualifying ceiling
- Appraisal gap coverage: useful but requires modeling cash-gap exposure

### 9.10 Common Offer-Strategy Pitfalls

- Writing the offer before the lender has reviewed full documentation
- Agreeing to a 21-day close on a complex file that needs 35 to 45 days
- Waiving financing contingency without VIVA pre-approval
- Waiving appraisal contingency without modeling cash-gap exposure
- Not introducing the lender to the listing agent
- Letting Realtor pressure override LO timeline guidance

## 10. The Pre-Offer Planning Checklist (Synthesized)

### 10.1 Six to Twelve Months Before Shopping

- Pull a full credit report (all three bureaus) and review for accuracy. Dispute and remediate errors before applying. The Fair Credit Reporting Act gives consumers the right to one free credit report per bureau per year through annualcreditreport.com.
- Review credit utilization, recent inquiries, and tradeline depth. Aim for utilization below 30% on revolving accounts and below 10% if pursuing the strongest credit profile.
- Begin organizing two years of personal tax returns with all schedules and statements.
- Begin organizing two years of business tax returns if self-employed (Schedule C, 1065 plus K-1, 1120-S plus K-1, 1120 as applicable).
- Begin organizing two years of W-2s, 1099s, and K-1s.
- Begin tracking variable income components (bonus, commission, RSU) with documentation that supports continuance.
- Review retirement and brokerage account statements. Identify any planned liquidations and model the tax consequence with a CPA.
- Review trust documentation if applicable.
- Identify the departing residence strategy if relocating: sell, retain as rental, retain vacant.
- File current-year tax returns on time. Extensions complicate underwriting and force the lender to use the prior year's return as the most recent filed.

### 10.2 Three to Six Months Before Shopping

- Engage a loan officer for a planning conversation. Output: realistic loan amount range, realistic loan type recommendation, documentation gap list, realistic timeline.
- Identify the realistic loan amount range and the right loan type. If a non-QM bank statement program is being considered, identify the specific programs the LO has access to and their parameters.
- Identify documentation gaps and start closing them. Get the offer letter signed if pending. Get the K-1 if outstanding from a partnership return. Get the CPA letter if business funds are being used.
- Identify the cash-to-close target and the reserve target separately. Confirm the borrower has both amounts available without needing to liquidate retirement assets at the last moment.
- Identify the income documentation path: full-doc agency, full-doc jumbo, non-QM bank statement, asset depletion, etc.
- Avoid new credit, large purchases, or job changes. Each is a potential underwriter flag.
- Begin the asset seasoning clock. Funds need a 60-day paper trail to clear the documentation window cleanly.
- Stabilize self-employment business operations if a sale, pivot, or restructure was contemplated. Significant business changes inside the application window are an underwriter risk.

### 10.3 One to Three Months Before Shopping

- Complete a verified-income, verified-asset (VIVA) pre-approval. The output is a pre-approval letter from a named LO that has been through full documentation review and conditional underwriting.
- Confirm reserve target is met after cash-to-close is set aside.
- Confirm DTI math at the target purchase price, including all current debt obligations and the new PITIA.
- Identify a buyer's agent experienced with complex-buyer scenarios.
- Verify property tax assumptions for the target submarket. Property tax can vary materially by jurisdiction and by escrowed amount versus actual amount.
- Verify homeowner's insurance availability and approximate cost. Some properties have insurance challenges (older roofs, historical claims, specific geographic exposure); identifying these early prevents surprises.

### 10.4 At Offer

- Pre-approval letter ready and current
- Cash-to-close confirmed and seasoned
- Earnest money source seasoned (typically 60+ days in the borrower's account)
- Realtor briefed on contingency strategy and realistic closing timeline
- LO briefed on the specific property and target offer; LO available to talk to listing agent if needed
- Closing timeline negotiated to allow full underwriting (35 to 45 days for complex files)

### 10.5 Under Contract

- Order inspection promptly within the inspection contingency window
- Order appraisal through the lender's AMC (Appraisal Management Company)
- Submit final documentation to underwriting promptly
- Keep employment, credit, and assets stable through closing. No new credit applications, no new tradelines, no large deposits, no job changes, no large purchases.
- Notify the LO immediately of any change in employment, compensation, credit, or financial position
- Confirm wire instructions in person or by verified phone before sending funds at closing. Wire fraud is a real and ongoing threat; verify wire instructions through a known phone number, not through email links.

## 12. Suggested FAQ Questions and Answers

These are written in answer-first AEO format suitable for FAQPage schema. Organized by topic area for retrieval. Every answer hedges where rules differ across loan types or where lender overlays apply.

### 12.1 Self-Employed FAQ

**Q: How do lenders calculate self-employed income for a mortgage?**
A: Lenders use a cash flow analysis worksheet, most commonly Fannie Mae Form 1084 or Freddie Mac Form 91, to start with the borrower's tax return net income and add back non-cash expenses like depreciation, depletion, and amortization. The result is a stable monthly qualifying income that often differs from what the borrower thinks of as "income." For most loan types, two years of personal and business federal tax returns are required, with a year-to-date profit and loss statement added when needed. Bank statement non-QM programs work differently: they qualify based on 12 to 24 months of business or personal bank deposits, with an expense factor (commonly 50% on business statements, lower in some programs) applied to gross deposits. The right path depends on whether the borrower's tax-return income or actual cash flow is the better representation of stable income.

**Q: Do my business tax write-offs hurt my mortgage qualification?**
A: Often yes, when qualifying through a conventional, FHA, VA, or USDA loan. Write-offs reduce taxable income, which is the starting point for the lender's qualifying income calculation. Common add-backs (depreciation, depletion, amortization, business use of home on Schedule C, non-recurring casualty losses) recover some of the reduction, but aggressive deductions for things like meals, entertainment, vehicle expenses, and contractor payments cannot be added back and reduce qualifying income directly. For borrowers whose tax return materially understates actual cash flow, a non-QM bank statement program can produce a higher qualifying income because it works from deposits rather than tax-return net income. The trade-off is a higher rate, larger down payment, and stronger reserve requirements. Tax strategy should be discussed with a CPA, who can help balance tax minimization against future mortgage qualification needs.

**Q: How many years of self-employment do I need?**
A: The standard rule across conventional, FHA, VA, and USDA is two years of self-employment. Fannie Mae's exception lets a borrower with 12 months of self-employment in the current business qualify if they have prior employment in the same line of work or related occupation, with documentation. FHA has a similar two-year rule with a 1-to-2-year exception when prior employment was in the same line of work for at least two years. Non-QM bank statement programs frequently accept one year of self-employment with prior W-2 history in the same field. A borrower less than one year into self-employment typically does not have a clean conventional path; non-QM is the more likely route.

**Q: Can I use K-1 income to qualify?**
A: Yes, with documentation. The standard set is two years of K-1s plus the underlying entity tax returns (Form 1065 for partnerships, Form 1120-S for S-corps), schedule of ownership percentage, and documentation of distribution history. The lender analyzes whether the entity has been generating income, whether distributions have been made consistently, and whether the borrower's ownership percentage and distribution history support continuance. K-1 income is among the trickiest to qualify on because distributions can swing year to year based on business decisions rather than profitability. A K-1 borrower who has both W-2 wages from the entity and K-1 distributions usually has a stronger profile than one with K-1 distributions alone.

### 12.2 Variable Income FAQ

**Q: Will my bonus count for mortgage qualification?**
A: Generally yes if you have a two-year history of receipt and the bonus is expected to continue. Documentation typically includes the most recent W-2, year-to-date pay stubs, and an employer letter confirming the bonus structure. The lender averages the two years of bonus income to arrive at a qualifying monthly figure. If the bonus has declined year over year, the lender typically uses the lower (more recent) figure rather than the average. Sign-on bonuses are generally not counted as qualifying income because they are one-time. A bonus that is new in the current year (no history) generally cannot be used. The 12-month exception applies in some cases when the bonus is structured as guaranteed, but the two-year history is the standard.

**Q: Will my RSUs count for mortgage qualification?**
A: Sometimes, with significant variation across loan types. Conventional loans through Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac allow RSU income with strict requirements: the RSUs must be from publicly traded company stock, the borrower needs at least 12 months of vested-and-distributed history for time-based RSUs (or 24 months for performance-based), and the vesting schedule must show at least 36 months of forward distributions remaining. Calculation methods differ: Fannie Mae uses the 200-day moving average of stock price; Freddie Mac uses the 52-week average. FHA, VA, and USDA generally do not allow RSU income for qualification, which pushes RSU-heavy borrowers toward conventional or jumbo programs. Sign-on bonuses paid in restricted stock that vest over time cannot be used as qualifying income under conventional rules.

**Q: What if my income went down last year?**
A: Lenders typically use the lower (most recent) year's income figure rather than the two-year average when income is declining. The borrower's case for using the higher historical figure is strongest when the decline is documented as a one-time event (medical leave, specific client loss, documented industry disruption) and when the current income trend has stabilized or recovered. Freddie Mac's Bulletin 2025-6 (effective 05-07-2025) added flexibility to use a shorter income calculation period (minimum 12 months combined) when a specific event caused reduced income and is unlikely to recur. The 12 months of combined income does not need to be consecutive, so the period impacted by the non-recurring event can be excluded. This is a meaningful improvement for borrowers with documented one-time disruptions.

### 12.3 Job Change / Relocation FAQ

**Q: Can I buy a house before I start my new job?**
A: Often yes, through Fannie Mae's employment offer or contract pathway, with parallel rules in Freddie Mac, FHA, VA, and USDA programs. Fannie Mae provides two options: (1) close with a fully executed offer letter and obtain the borrower's first paystub before delivery to Fannie Mae; or (2) close on a one-unit principal residence with a fully executed, non-contingent contract for future employment without obtaining a paystub first. The contract must clearly identify the employer, borrower, position, type and rate of pay, and start date, and must be signed by both parties. Documentation typically also includes departmental confirmation and reserves to cover the gap between closing and first paycheck. Medical professional programs often extend the pre-start-date allowance further. Active-duty PCS scenarios use the Statement of Service plus orders rather than a civilian offer letter framework.

**Q: I just changed jobs; can I still qualify?**
A: Usually yes, especially if the new job is in the same line of work or represents a career advancement. The lender will document the new employer, current paystub, and offer letter. A two-year employment history is required, but the history can include prior employers; you do not need two years at the same employer. A lateral move at higher pay or a promotion is generally treated favorably; a major career change can effectively restart the two-year clock. The most important practical advice is to notify the loan officer immediately if a job change happens during pre-approval or under contract; the alternative is the LO discovering it during pre-closing verification, which is harder to manage under timeline pressure.

**Q: What if I have a gap in employment?**
A: Gaps are addressed differently across loan types. FHA Handbook 4000.1 allows gaps of six months or more if the borrower has been employed in the current job for at least six months at case number assignment AND has a two-year work history prior to the absence. Conventional, VA, and USDA apply gap analysis with letter-of-explanation requirements rather than a specific six-month threshold. Gaps for documented reasons (education, medical leave, family leave, military service, parental leave) are treated more favorably than voluntary unemployment. Documentation of the reason is essential; the borrower's letter of explanation should pair with supporting documentation where possible.

### 12.4 Multiple Properties / Retained Home FAQ

**Q: Can I keep my current house and buy a new one?**
A: Yes, with planning. The three options for a departing residence are sell, retain as a rental, or retain vacant. Selling before or concurrent with closing is the cleanest path. Retaining as a rental requires documenting the lease, security deposit or first month's rent, and Form 1007 rent schedule; rental income at 75% of gross rent counts toward qualification. Retaining vacant means both housing payments must be qualified by the borrower's income alone. Fannie Mae has eliminated the prior 30% equity requirement for departing-residence rental income; FHA still requires 25% equity AND a relocation of more than 100 miles to use rental income from a property the borrower is vacating. Lender overlays may be more conservative than agency rules; verify with the specific lender.

**Q: How many properties can I finance under conventional rules?**
A: Fannie Mae caps borrowers at 10 financed properties under B2-2-03. The count includes the borrower's principal residence if financed, all financed investment properties, and second homes. Multi-unit properties (such as a two-unit) count as one property. Reserve requirements scale with the number of financed properties: 2% of aggregate UPB on other financed properties for 1 to 4 properties, 4% for 5 to 6, and 6% for 7 to 10 (DU only). Borrowers above the 10-property cap typically use jumbo investor portfolios or DSCR (Debt Service Coverage Ratio) programs for additional properties. DSCR programs qualify based on the property's rental cash flow rather than the borrower's personal income.

### 12.5 Asset Documentation FAQ

**Q: What's a "large deposit" and why does it matter?**
A: Per Fannie Mae's depository accounts rule, a large deposit is a single deposit that exceeds 50% of the borrower's total monthly qualifying income. When the lender reviews bank statements (typically the most recent two months), large deposits must be sourced. Deposits with readily identifiable sources on the statement (payroll direct deposit, IRS or state tax refund, Social Security, transfer between verified accounts) do not need additional documentation. Other large deposits require a paper trail showing where the funds came from. Cash deposits generally cannot be sourced and may be excluded from the qualifying balance entirely. The cleanest strategy when a large deposit is anticipated is to "let it season" by depositing 60+ days before application to clear the documentation window.

**Q: Can I use gift funds for the down payment?**
A: Yes for principal residence and second-home transactions across all loan types. Conventional loans (Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac) allow gifts from relatives by blood, marriage, adoption, or legal guardianship, plus domestic partners, fiancés, former relatives, and individuals with long-standing familial-like or mentorship relationships. The donor cannot be affiliated with the builder, developer, real estate agent, or other interested party. Documentation requires a signed gift letter, the donor's name and address, the gift amount, and a paper trail showing donor's ability to fund the gift and transfer to the borrower. FHA, VA, and USDA each have their own donor and documentation rules. Gifts are generally not allowed on investment properties under agency rules. Tax implications for the donor (annual exclusion, lifetime exemption, Form 709 filing) should be discussed with a CPA.

**Q: Can I use my 401(k) for the down payment?**
A: Yes, with several routes. A 401(k) loan (up to 50% of vested balance or $50,000, whichever is less) avoids the early withdrawal penalty if the loan is repaid on schedule. Treatment of the 401(k) loan payment in DTI varies by loan type: conventional and VA typically exclude it from DTI; non-QM and jumbo commonly include it. A direct distribution from the 401(k) avoids the loan-payment DTI issue but triggers ordinary income tax plus a 10% early withdrawal penalty if pre-59½. The tax cost of liquidating retirement assets to fund cash-to-close can be substantial; modeling the after-tax math with a CPA before pulling the trigger is essential. The lender requires documentation of the distribution (account statement showing withdrawal) and receipt (deposit on the borrower's account).

### 12.6 Offer Strategy FAQ

**Q: How long does a complex mortgage take to close?**
A: Standard closings run about 30 days from contract to close. Complex closings commonly run 35 to 45 days, sometimes longer for files with self-employment income calculation, K-1 distributions, gift funds with complex documentation, retained-home rental income qualification, or other complications. Specific complications add specific time: K-1 income with a recently filed return adds 1 to 2 weeks; gift funds with complex donor documentation add 1 to 2 weeks; departing-residence rental qualification adds 1 to 2 weeks. The cost of agreeing to a tight closing the file cannot meet is real: lost earnest money on default, contract extension fees, and seller leverage in extension negotiations. The right strategy is to set the timeline based on the file's actual complexity rather than the seller's preference.

**Q: Should I waive the financing contingency?**
A: Generally not on a complex file. The financing contingency is the buyer's protection if the loan does not close. Waiving it puts the buyer's earnest money and contract performance at risk if any condition surfaces during underwriting that delays or kills the loan. In a competitive multi-offer scenario, some buyers waive the financing contingency to strengthen the offer; this only makes sense when the buyer has a verified-income, verified-asset (VIVA) pre-approval, the file is complete with no remaining conditions, and the buyer has substantial cash reserves that would let the deal close even if the loan were delayed. For most complex buyers, the contingency should stay in place; the offer can be strengthened through earnest money level, closing timeline, escalation clauses, or appraisal gap coverage instead.

## 14. Risks, Caveats, and Overclaiming Language to Avoid

### 14.1 Statements That Should Never Appear as Guarantees

Each of these is a frequent overclaim risk that should be hedged or rewritten:

- **"You'll qualify."** Replace with "You may qualify, depending on the loan type, your specific documentation, and the lender's underwriting decision."
- **"Your income will count."** Replace with "Your income may count, subject to the loan type's documentation and continuance requirements. Variable income (bonus, commission, RSU) typically requires a two-year history of receipt and documentation of likely continuance."
- **"Your offer letter is sufficient."** Replace with "An offer letter may support qualification under specific loan-type rules. Fannie Mae's employment offer or contract pathway has specific requirements; FHA, VA, and USDA pathways differ. The right pathway depends on the specific borrower and transaction."
- **"Your retained-home rental will count."** Replace with "Rental income from a retained departing residence may count, subject to documentation requirements that vary by loan type. Fannie Mae has eliminated the prior 30% equity rule but still requires a fully executed lease and evidence of receipt; FHA still requires 25% equity AND relocation of more than 100 miles. Lender overlays may apply."

### 14.2 Marketing Language to Avoid

- **"We get self-employed borrowers approved when others can't."** This is an overclaim that requires substantiation; substantiation in mortgage advertising has specific compliance requirements. Replace with factual statements about the lender's experience with self-employed scenarios and the specific products available.
- **"Any income works."** Never appropriate; not true. Some income types (unreported tips, undocumented gig work, projected pay raises, sign-on bonuses paid in restricted stock with vesting) cannot be used as qualifying income.
- **"Always approved with the right loan officer."** Approval depends on the borrower's qualifying file, the property, and the loan type, not on the loan officer's salesmanship. The LO's role is to help the borrower assemble the strongest defensible file and choose the right loan type.
- **Timeline guarantees that don't survive the full-doc reality of complex files.** "Close in 21 days" promises on complex files commonly fail to deliver.

### 14.3 Hedging Language for Complex-Borrower Content

Where rules differ across loan types, recent agency restructures (the 03-04-2026 Fannie Mae Income Assessment chapter restructure with mandatory compliance 06-01-2026, FHA Info 2025-04 effective 04-10-2025, Freddie Mac Bulletin 2025-6 effective 05-07-2025), or where lender overlays are common, content should hedge with:

- "may," "can," "depending on loan type"
- "subject to lender overlay"
- "varies by program"
- "verify with your specific lender"

Where Fannie Mae has updated a rule (such as the elimination of the 30% equity requirement for departing residences) but lender overlays may still apply the older standard, content should explicitly note that lender overlays may diverge from current agency rules.
