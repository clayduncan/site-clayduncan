## 2. What Makes a Mortgage File "Complex": The Planning Frame

### 2.1 Defining Complexity

- The standard W-2 base-salary file: two pay stubs, two years W-2s, two months bank statements, automated underwriting approval
- What pushes a file out of "standard" into "complex":
  - Income that is not 100% W-2 base salary (bonus, commission, overtime, RVU, RSU, 1099, K-1, distributions)
  - Self-employment of any kind, including side businesses
  - Recent employment changes (less than two years)
  - Pre-start-date employment or relocation in progress
  - Retained primary residence converting to rental
  - Multiple financed properties
  - Significant gift funds or family-lender involvement
  - Large recent deposits requiring sourcing
  - Retirement or brokerage account distributions funding cash-to-close
  - Trust assets or trust beneficiary distributions
  - Foreign income, foreign assets, foreign nationality
  - Recent credit events (bankruptcy, foreclosure, short sale within seasoning windows)
  - Co-borrowers with materially different financial profiles

### 2.2 Why Complexity Demands Earlier Planning

- Complex files require more documentation, more time, and more underwriter scrutiny
- The standard pre-approval timeline (days) versus complex pre-approval timeline (weeks)
- The risk of last-minute conditions in complex files
- The compounding effect: one complex element is manageable; three or four create real timeline risk
- Why "I'll talk to a lender after I find a house" is the single most expensive mistake complex buyers make

### 2.3 The Right-Loan-Type Decision

- Complex borrowers often have multiple loan-type options: conventional (Fannie/Freddie), FHA, VA, USDA, jumbo, non-QM
- The right loan type depends on the specific complexity:
  - Self-employed with heavy deductions: bank statement non-QM may beat conventional
  - Self-employed with clean returns: conventional often best
  - Recent relocation with offer letter: conventional may work; medical professional or lender-specific programs may extend pre-start-date allowance
  - Multiple financed properties: conventional has 10-property cap; jumbo or DSCR may be the path
  - Large gift fund: conventional, FHA, VA, USDA all allow with documentation; non-QM allows with overlays
  - Asset-rich, current-W-2-light retiree: asset depletion non-QM may beat agency
- The packet should compile the decision logic for each common complexity, with cross-references to existing or planned content (FHA, USDA, VA, jumbo, DSCR, medical professional)

### 2.4 The Pre-Pre-Approval Conversation

- The 30-minute planning conversation 3–6 months before shopping
- What the loan officer needs to see
- What the borrower needs to gather to make the conversation productive
- The output of this conversation: a realistic loan amount range, a realistic loan type, a list of documentation gaps to close, and a realistic timeline

## 3. Self-Employed Borrower Documentation

### 3.1 The Core Self-Employed Documentation Set (Conventional Baseline)

Document, with citations to Fannie Mae B3-3.2 and Freddie Mac 5300.4:

- **Two years of personal federal tax returns** (Form 1040) with all schedules and statements
- **Two years of business federal tax returns** when the entity files separately:
  - Schedule C (sole proprietorship)
  - Form 1065 + K-1 (partnership)
  - Form 1120-S + K-1 (S-corp)
  - Form 1120 (C-corp)
- **Year-to-date Profit and Loss statement** prepared by the borrower or CPA
- **Year-to-date balance sheet** (often required for partnerships and corporations)
- **Business bank statements** — typically two months for full-doc; 12 or 24 months for bank statement non-QM
- **CPA letter** confirming continued operation of the business (commonly required) and confirming withdrawal of funds for cash-to-close would not adversely affect the business (when business funds are used)
- **Business license and operating documentation** — business license, articles of incorporation/organization, operating agreement, partnership agreement
- **K-1 income** — two years of K-1s plus the underlying entity tax return showing distributions and ownership percentage

### 3.2 Self-Employment Length-of-History Requirements

- The standard two-year history requirement under conventional, FHA, VA, USDA
- The Fannie Mae 12-month history exception (with prior employment in the same line of work)
- How a recent transition from W-2 to self-employed is treated
- How a recent transition from self-employed to W-2 is treated
- The "less than two years" non-QM alternatives

### 3.3 Cash Flow Analysis: How Underwriters Calculate Self-Employed Income

This is the most-misunderstood area in self-employed lending. Document with citations:

- **Fannie Mae Form 1084 (Cash Flow Analysis)** — the standard worksheet underwriters use
- The "income" the underwriter calculates is rarely the same as the income the borrower thinks they make
- The line-by-line treatment of:
  - Gross receipts / gross income
  - Cost of goods sold
  - Expenses (each line of Schedule C, Form 1120/1120-S/1065)
  - Depreciation (commonly added back)
  - Depletion (commonly added back)
  - Amortization (commonly added back)
  - Business use of home (commonly added back)
  - Mileage / vehicle depreciation portion (commonly added back)
  - Meals and entertainment (typically not added back)
  - Owner's draws / member distributions (treated as the income for sole proprietors and partners; for S-corps, only documented W-2 + K-1 distributions)
- The two-year averaging rule
- The declining-trend rule (when the most recent year is lower than the prior year, underwriters typically use the lower number)

### 3.4 K-1 Income and S-Corp / Partnership Owners

- The challenge of K-1 income: distributions can swing year to year independent of business profitability
- The required documentation:
  - K-1 for two years
  - Underlying entity return (1120-S or 1065)
  - Schedule of ownership percentage
  - Documentation of distribution history and continuance
- The "ownership percentage" rule for using business income vs distributions
- The S-corp owner taking minimal W-2 plus large K-1 distributions — and how that creates qualification headaches

### 3.5 1099 Income

- 1099 contractors as a category (independent contractors, gig workers, locum providers, fee-for-service consultants)
- Two-year tax return requirement
- The Schedule C analysis (1099 income flows to Schedule C unless the contractor operates through an entity)
- The non-QM 1099-only loan products that qualify off gross 1099 income with reduced documentation

### 3.6 Bank Statement Loans (Non-QM Alternative)

- The product structure: 12-month or 24-month personal or business bank statements as the income basis
- The deposit-analysis method: lender averages monthly deposits, applies an expense factor (commonly 50% for service businesses, lower for high-overhead businesses)
- The CPA letter alternative method: CPA-provided expense ratio replaces the standard expense factor
- When bank statement loans make sense:
  - Heavy-deduction Schedule C where qualifying income on returns is low but actual cash flow is strong
  - Recent business owners without two full years of returns
- The trade-offs: rate premium, down payment premium, reserve premium

### 3.7 P&L-Only Loans (Non-QM Alternative)

- The product structure: CPA-prepared P&L is the income basis
- More restrictive than bank statement; fewer lenders offer it
- Use cases similar to bank statement but with stronger CPA involvement

### 3.8 Common Self-Employed Documentation Pitfalls

- Filing tax returns late or on extension at the moment of mortgage application
- Cash-basis accounting that doesn't translate cleanly to underwriter analysis
- Heavy 4562 depreciation that isn't cleanly added back
- Owner's-draw documentation that doesn't match what the borrower described
- Business bank account commingling with personal funds
- Recent business pivots (new entity, change in entity type, recent partnership dissolution)
- Year-to-date P&L that diverges sharply from prior years
- Revenue concentration with a single client (some lenders flag)
- The "I just file Schedule C" sole proprietor whose actual structure is more complex

## 7. Asset Documentation: Gift Funds, Large Deposits, Retirement and Brokerage

### 7.1 The 60-Day Asset Documentation Window

- The standard window for asset documentation across loan types

## 11. Common Misconceptions and Mistakes

- "I can get pre-approved over the phone in five minutes" — true for simple files; not true for complex
- "All my income counts for mortgage qualification" — variable income has seasoning and continuance rules
- "My business shows $X profit, so my income is $X" — Form 1084 cash flow analysis often produces different numbers
- "I can close before my new job starts as long as I have an offer letter" — depends on loan type and lender
- "My retained home rental income will count immediately" — typically requires documentation, equity threshold, or signed lease
- "Gift funds are easy — just transfer the money" — documentation is non-trivial
- "I can pay down debt at the closing table to fix DTI" — sometimes works, sometimes creates new problems
- "I can move my own money between accounts without explanation" — internal transfers still need paper trail
- "My CPA letter solves my self-employed income problem" — supplements but rarely replaces tax-return analysis
- "I can apply with multiple lenders and pick later" — credit pulls within 14–45 days are typically scored as one inquiry, but file consistency matters
- "Non-QM is just a higher rate" — also higher down, higher reserves, higher scrutiny
- "I can change jobs after pre-approval" — can derail the file
- "I can switch from W-2 to 1099 mid-application" — can derail the file
- "The lender will figure it out" — complex files require the borrower to understand the file too

## 12. Do / Don't Guidance

### Do

- Engage a loan officer 6–12 months before shopping if the file is complex
- File tax returns on time, every year, even when an extension is tempting
- Document every variable income component prospectively
- Reconcile business and personal finances cleanly
- Verify employment offer letter contents before relying on them
- Identify the departing-residence strategy before shopping
- Model cash-to-close including reserves
- Verify gift fund timing and documentation upfront
- Choose a buyer's agent and LO experienced with complex files

### Don't

- Don't change jobs, sign new contracts, or accept compensation changes during the application or closing window
- Don't open new credit in the 90 days before pre-approval or between pre-approval and closing
- Don't move money between accounts in the 60 days before application without paper trail
- Don't liquidate retirement or brokerage positions for cash-to-close without modeling tax (route to CPA)
- Don't pay down collections without LO guidance
- Don't waive contingencies in a competitive offer without modeling cash-gap exposure
- Don't accept the first lender's terms as the market — complex files benefit from comparison
- Don't assume rental income from departing residence will count — verify rules upfront
- Don't write an offer before VIVA pre-approval on a complex file
