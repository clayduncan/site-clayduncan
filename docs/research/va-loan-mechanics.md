## 2. Plain-English Summary (470 words)

A VA home loan is not a loan made by the VA. It is a loan made by a private lender (a bank, credit union, or non-bank mortgage lender) that the VA partially guarantees against borrower default. That guaranty is the entire mechanism. Because the VA backs roughly the first 25 percent of any loss above $144,000, lenders are willing to make loans with no down payment, no monthly mortgage insurance, and pricing that is generally competitive with conventional financing. The benefit is funded primarily by a one-time funding fee that most borrowers pay at closing, with significant exemptions for veterans receiving service-connected disability compensation, certain surviving spouses, and active-duty Purple Heart recipients.

Eligibility starts with a Certificate of Eligibility (COE). The COE is the document that proves to a lender that the borrower has earned the benefit and shows how much "entitlement" is available. Active-duty service members generally qualify after 90 continuous days. Veterans qualify based on minimum service requirements that vary by era. Members of the National Guard and Reserve qualify after either 90 days of full-time federal active duty (with at least 30 consecutive days), 90 days of qualifying Title 32 duty under specific Code sections (added by the Johnny Isakson Act of 2020), or six creditable years in the Selected Reserve or National Guard. Surviving spouses can qualify in narrowly defined circumstances. Discharge characterization matters: honorable and general discharges are eligible; OTH and bad conduct discharges typically require a separate VA Character-of-Discharge review before the COE will issue.

Since 01-01-2020, when the Blue Water Navy Vietnam Veterans Act took effect, borrowers with full entitlement have no VA-imposed loan limit. The lender will still cap the loan based on income, credit, residual income, and the property appraisal, but the federal cap is gone. Borrowers with partial entitlement (those with an active VA loan, a prior loss, or a still-encumbered property) compute remaining entitlement using the county conforming loan limit and the previously charged entitlement.

The VA appraisal serves two purposes: establishing reasonable value and confirming that the property meets Minimum Property Requirements (MPRs). MPRs cover safety, structural soundness, and sanitation. They are not a perfection standard. If the appraisal comes in below the contract price, the appraiser may invoke the Tidewater Initiative, giving the lender 48 hours to submit additional sales data before the Notice of Value is final. After the NOV, the borrower (only the borrower) may request a formal Reconsideration of Value. Throughout this process, the VA escape clause, a federally required provision in every VA purchase contract, protects the borrower's earnest money if the appraisal comes in low.

Refinances are governed by 38 USC § 3709. IRRRLs require 210 days of seasoning, six consecutive payments, a 36-month recoupment of all costs, and a defined net tangible benefit. Cash-out refinances are bifurcated into Type I and Type II, with the same seasoning rules and an LTV cap of 100 percent (most lenders cap at 90 percent).

## 3. VA Loan Eligibility

### 3.1 Eligibility categories

The federal eligibility rule lives in 38 USC § 3702 and 38 CFR § 36.4302. VA's consumer-facing eligibility page at https://www.va.gov/housing-assistance/home-loans/eligibility/ (last updated 06-12-2025) is the authoritative summary for COE issuance.

**Active-duty service members.** A current active-duty member meets the minimum service requirement after 90 continuous days of active duty. The COE for an active-duty borrower is supported by a current Statement of Service signed by the unit commander, personnel officer, adjutant, or similar authorized official. The Statement of Service must contain the borrower's full name, Social Security number, date of birth, entry-on-duty date, time lost, and the name of the issuing command.

**Veterans by era.** The minimum active-duty service requirement varies by service period. Per VA's eligibility page, the bands are:

- Gulf War to present (08-02-1990 forward): 24 continuous months, or the full period for which called or ordered (at least 90 days), or at least 90 days under a qualifying exception, or less than 90 days if discharged for a service-connected disability.
- Peacetime 09-08-1980 to 08-01-1990 (officers 10-17-1981 to 08-01-1990): 24 continuous months, or the full period for which called (at least 181 days), or 181 days under a qualifying exception, or less than 181 days for service-connected disability.
- Post-Vietnam peacetime 05-08-1975 to 09-07-1980 (officers to 10-16-1981): 181 continuous days, or less than 181 days for service-connected disability.
- Vietnam era 08-05-1964 to 05-07-1975 (or 11-01-1955 to 05-07-1975 for service in the Republic of Vietnam): 90 total days, or less than 90 for service-connected disability.
- Post-Korean 02-01-1955 to 08-04-1964: 181 total days, or less than 181 for service-connected disability.
- Korean War 06-27-1950 to 01-31-1955: 90 total days, or less than 90 for service-connected disability.
- Post-WWII 07-26-1947 to 06-26-1950: 181 continuous days, or less than 181 for service-connected disability.
- WWII 09-16-1940 to 07-25-1947: 90 total days, or less than 90 for service-connected disability.

The qualifying exceptions referenced in each band are: hardship; convenience of the government (with at least 20 months of a 2-year enlistment served); early-out (with 21 months of a 2-year enlistment served); involuntary reduction in force; certain medical conditions; and service-connected disability.

**National Guard members.** Per VA's current eligibility rule and the Johnny Isakson and David P. Roe, M.D. Veterans Health Care and Benefits Improvement Act of 2020 (Pub. L. 116-315, enacted 01-05-2021), a Guard member meets the minimum length-of-service requirement if they served:

- At least 90 days of non-training active-duty Title 10 service, _or_
- At least 90 days of active-duty service including at least 30 consecutive days, where the DD-214 reflects activation under 32 USC § 316, § 502, § 503, § 504, or § 505 (the Title 32 expansion added by the Isakson-Roe Act), _or_
- 6 creditable years in the National Guard and continuing to serve, _or_
- 6 creditable years in the National Guard and discharged honorably or placed on the retired list.

The 2020 Act changed Guard eligibility materially. Before the change, Title 32 service generally did not count toward eligibility. Documentation typically includes the NGB-22 for separated members and a current Statement of Service or activation orders for currently serving Guard members.

**Reserve members.** A Reserve member meets the minimum length-of-service requirement if they served:

- At least 90 days of non-training active-duty service, _or_
- 6 creditable years in the Selected Reserve and continuing to serve, _or_
- 6 creditable years in the Selected Reserve and discharged honorably or placed on the retired list.

Note that the Title 32 90-day path is a Guard-only addition; it does not apply to Reservists, whose paths are limited to active duty or 6 years in the Selected Reserve. Documentation for separated Reservists is similar to Guard separations.

**Surviving spouses.** A surviving spouse may obtain a COE if any of the following apply:

- Eligible for or currently receiving certain types of VA Dependency and Indemnity Compensation (DIC); or
- Spouse of an active-duty service member who is missing in action (MIA) or held as a prisoner of war (POW); or
- Unmarried surviving spouse of a veteran who died in service or from a service-connected disability; or
- Spouse of a veteran who was rated totally disabled at the time of death (subject to additional conditions).

The surviving-spouse COE process generally uses VA Form 26-1817 (revised per Circular 26-23-2 dated 01-05-2023) for unmarried surviving spouses. DIC-receiving spouses can typically request the COE through VA Form 26-1880 or via a participating lender. Documentation includes the marriage certificate, the death certificate, and verification of DIC eligibility.

Remarriage rules for surviving-spouse VA loan eligibility have been liberalized over the years. Statutory changes have, in most cases, allowed remarried surviving spouses to retain eligibility under specified conditions; route any remarriage-status question to the St. Petersburg RLC or the Atlanta NEC for a case-specific determination.

**Other eligible borrowers** (per VA's eligibility page):

- U.S. citizens who served in the armed forces of a government allied with the U.S. in WWII;
- Public Health Service officers;
- Cadets at the U.S. Military, Air Force, or Coast Guard Academies and midshipmen at the U.S. Naval Academy;
- Officers of the National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration;
- Merchant seamen during WWII.

**Joint loans.** VA Pamphlet 26-7, Chapter 7, Section 1, governs joint loans. The most common joint-loan scenarios are:

- Veteran with non-veteran joint borrower (typically a spouse): VA's guaranty applies only to the veteran's portion. If the joint borrower is the veteran's spouse, the loan can be treated as if the entire loan were for the veteran for guaranty purposes; for non-spouse joint borrowers, VA's guaranty is limited to the veteran's portion of the loan.
- Two veterans, both using entitlement: Each veteran's entitlement is charged for half the loan.
- Two veterans, one using entitlement: Only the using veteran's entitlement is charged.

### 3.2 Certificate of Eligibility (COE)

The COE is the document that proves to a lender that the borrower has earned the VA home loan benefit and confirms the entitlement amount, prior loan use, and funding fee exemption status.

**Three methods to obtain a COE.**

1. **Through a VA-participating lender via WebLGY.** Fastest. Lenders submit through VA's Web-based Loan Guaranty system and frequently receive an instant determination for borrowers with clean records.
2. **Through VA.gov directly (borrower-initiated).** The borrower can request the COE online through https://www.va.gov/housing-assistance/home-loans/request-coe-form-26-1880.
3. **By mail using VA Form 26-1880.** Slowest. Mail to the Atlanta National Eligibility Center at the address listed in section 1.8. Use this when WebLGY cannot resolve the determination, when records are incomplete, or when a discharge upgrade or character-of-discharge review is required.

**Documentation by borrower type.**

- **Veterans:** DD-214 (Member 4 copy preferred because it shows the narrative reason for separation and the character of service). Member 1 copies omit the separation narrative and may slow the determination.
- **Active duty:** Current Statement of Service signed by unit commander, personnel officer, adjutant, or other authorized official; must include name, SSN, DOB, entry date, lost time, and command name.
- **National Guard (separated):** NGB-22, supplemented by retirement points statements and any Title 10 orders or DD-214s if applicable. For Title 32 90-day eligibility, ensure the activation Code section (316, 502, 503, 504, or 505) is documented.
- **Reserve (separated):** Equivalent separation documentation; retirement points statements; Title 10 active-duty orders or DD-214s if the member served on active duty.
- **Surviving spouses:** VA Form 26-1817 (or VA Form 26-1880 if DIC-receiving), marriage certificate, death certificate, and DIC verification.

**Common COE issues.**

- "Insufficient service" responses generally indicate that the time band requirement was not met. The COE may still issue if a qualifying exception applies (hardship, RIF, medical, service-connected disability).
- Recently discharged veterans whose DD-214s have not yet been ingested into VA databases. The fix is generally to mail or upload the DD-214 with the COE request.
- Records lost in the 1973 NPRC fire at the National Personnel Records Center, particularly affecting Army personnel records from 11-01-1912 to 01-01-1960 and Air Force records from 09-25-1947 to 01-01-1964 (roughly). Reconstruction can take additional time and requires alternate documentation.
- Discrepancies between the DD-214 and VA records (rank, dates, service-connected disability rating). These typically require correction through the appropriate Discharge Review Board or Board for Correction of Military Records before VA can issue a COE consistent with the corrected record.
- COE shows used entitlement that the borrower thought was restored. This typically means a prior VA loan paid off in full but never had the entitlement formally restored; the cure is to submit a restoration request through the lender or VA Form 26-1880, Section II.

### 3.3 Service status and discharge edge cases

**Honorable discharge:** Standard eligibility once the time-band requirement is met.

**General under honorable conditions:** Generally eligible. The administrative reason for the general discharge can occasionally trigger additional inquiry but does not bar eligibility on its face.

**Other Than Honorable (OTH):** Generally not eligible without a VA Character-of-Discharge (COD) review. The COD review is conducted by the VA, separately from any discharge upgrade pursued through DoD. VA evaluates whether the conduct that led to the OTH discharge bars benefits under 38 CFR § 3.12. The 04-26-2024 Final Rule (89 FR 32372, effective 06-25-2024) materially liberalized the regulatory bars by limiting the conduct that can be considered to that which formed the basis of discharge, removing one regulatory bar entirely, refining another, and instituting a "compelling circumstances" exception that requires VA to weigh mental and cognitive impairment, physical health, combat exposure, racial bias, and similar factors. Source: https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2024/04/26/2024-09012/update-and-clarify-regulatory-bars-to-benefits-based-on-character-of-discharge

**Bad Conduct Discharge (BCD):** A BCD issued by general court-martial is a statutory bar to benefits under 38 USC § 5303(a). A BCD issued by special court-martial is treated under the regulatory bars in 38 CFR § 3.12(d) and is reviewable.

**Dishonorable Discharge:** A statutory bar under 38 USC § 5303(a). Not reviewable by VA absent an upgrade through the DoD process.

**Uncharacterized / Entry-Level Separation (ELS):** Per 38 CFR § 3.12(l)(1), an ELS is treated as "under conditions other than dishonorable" and is generally compatible with VA loan eligibility once the underlying time-in-service rules are otherwise satisfied. "Void enlistment" and "dropped from the rolls" uncharacterized separations require fact-by-fact review.

**Two parallel review tracks for adverse discharges.**

1. **VA Character-of-Discharge review:** Determines whether VA, for VA benefits purposes, will treat the service period as honorable. This does not change the underlying DoD discharge characterization. A favorable VA COD outcome makes the borrower eligible for VA benefits, including the home loan.
2. **DoD discharge upgrade:** Pursued through the service branch's Discharge Review Board (DRB) or Board for Correction of Military Records (BCMR/BCNR). A DRB or BCMR upgrade to honorable or general is binding on VA per 38 CFR § 3.12(f), unless a statutory bar in 38 CFR § 3.12(c) still applies.

**Special considerations.** Veterans whose adverse discharges are connected to PTSD, traumatic brain injury, military sexual trauma, or historical "Don't Ask, Don't Tell" or pre-DADT sexual orientation policies have additional review pathways. The 2014 Hagel Memorandum and subsequent DoD guidance directed liberal consideration of mental-health conditions in discharge upgrade requests. A second 2017 Kurta Memorandum extended this to MST cases.

**Medical retirement vs medical separation:** Both are eligibility-permitting characterizations, but they have different effects on disability rating and funding fee exemption status. A medical retirement (with a permanent disability rating) commonly produces funding fee exemption. A medical separation may not, depending on the rating and timing.

**Service component transitions.** Active to Guard/Reserve (or vice versa), multiple enlistments, and breaks in service all require careful documentation. Each period of service is evaluated under its own time-band rule, and credit can sometimes be combined across periods to satisfy the minimum.

**Routing rule for any discharge or COD question:** Direct the borrower to a VA-accredited representative, an accredited Veterans Service Organization (VSO), or a county Veterans Service Officer. This is not the lender's lane.

### 3.4 Restoration and multiple-use eligibility

Per 38 CFR § 36.4303 and VA's eligibility page, an entitlement that has been used in the past can be restored to enable a new VA loan if:

- The home bought with the prior loan has been sold and the loan paid in full; or
- A qualified veteran-transferee assumed the loan and substituted their entitlement for the same amount the original veteran used; or
- The prior loan was repaid in full but the home was not sold (this option can be used only once in a lifetime, the "one-time restoration without sale" rule).

**One-time restoration without sale** is a narrow but critical option. It is most often used when a borrower refinances a VA loan into conventional financing (paying off the VA loan in full) but keeps the property, intending to free entitlement for a new VA loan on a different property. The veteran must affirmatively request restoration, typically using VA Form 26-1880 Section II.

**Restoration after refinance to non-VA financing and subsequent payoff.** Once the VA loan is paid off, the borrower can request restoration even if they still own the property, subject to the once-per-lifetime limit if the property has not been sold.

**Restoration after short sale or foreclosure.** Restoration can be available after a foreclosure or short sale, but the borrower's used entitlement is not automatically restored, VA may absorb a loss on the prior loan and reduce the available entitlement going forward. Waiting periods (lender overlays) of 2 to 3 years post-foreclosure or short sale are common, separate from VA's own re-eligibility rule. Direct seasoning questions to a VA-experienced lender.

**Bonus / second-tier entitlement.** Covered in Section 4 below.

## 4. VA Entitlement and Loan Limits

### 4.1 Basic entitlement concepts

VA's "entitlement" is the dollar amount that VA pledges to repay the lender in the event of a borrower default. It is not a loan amount. It functions as a guaranty that lets the lender extend credit at no-down-payment terms.

**Two-tier entitlement structure.** Per VA's loan limits page (https://www.va.gov/housing-assistance/home-loans/loan-limits/, last updated 08-12-2025):

- **Basic (Tier 1) entitlement:** $36,000. Shown on the COE in the basic entitlement field.
- **Bonus (Tier 2 / secondary) entitlement:** Calculated, not displayed on the COE. Equals the amount VA will guarantee for any loan amount above $144,000.

Why $36,000 and $144,000? The original 1944 G.I. Bill set the basic guaranty at the lower of 50 percent of the loan or $2,000. Through subsequent statutes, basic entitlement has been raised to $36,000, which represents 25 percent of $144,000. For loans over $144,000, the bonus entitlement layer kicks in to provide additional guaranty up to the cap calculated below.

**The 25 percent guaranty math.** For loans over $144,000, VA's guaranty equals 25 percent of the loan amount, capped (for borrowers with partial entitlement) at 25 percent of the conforming loan limit. For full-entitlement borrowers, the cap is removed (see Section 4.2).

**How entitlement is "charged."** When a veteran takes a VA loan, the entitlement charged on the COE equals the larger of (a) the basic $36,000 (for loans up to $144,000) or (b) 25 percent of the loan amount up to 25 percent of the applicable loan limit (for loans above $144,000). Once the loan is paid in full and the property is sold, the entitlement charge is removed and the entitlement is restored to full.

### 4.2 The Blue Water Navy Vietnam Veterans Act of 2019 changes

This is the highest-leverage section because most pre-2020 VA loan content on the public internet still references the prior loan limit framework.

Pub. L. 116-23, the Blue Water Navy Vietnam Veterans Act of 2019, was enacted on 06-25-2019 with VA loan provisions effective for loans closed on or after 01-01-2020. Source: https://www.congress.gov/bill/116th-congress/house-bill/299; VA program summary at https://benefits.va.gov/benefits/blue-water-navy.asp.

**Removal of VA loan limits for full-entitlement borrowers.** The Act removed the cap on the dollar amount that VA will guarantee for veterans with full entitlement. Per VA Circular 26-19-23 and the consolidated guidance in Circular 26-25-10, "for loans above $144,000, the maximum amount of the guaranty will be 25 percent of the loan amount, regardless of the Freddie Mac conforming loan limit." This means a full-entitlement borrower can purchase a home at any price with no down payment, subject to the lender approving the loan based on the borrower's income, credit, and the property appraisal.

**What "full entitlement" means in current VA terminology.** A borrower has full entitlement if they have never used their VA loan benefit, or if they previously used it and have since had the entitlement fully restored (loan paid in full and property sold, or the once-per-lifetime restoration without sale exercised, or substitution of entitlement by a transferee). The COE will reflect $36,000 in basic entitlement and zero used entitlement.

**Interaction with conforming loan limits.** Conforming loan limits remain relevant to two groups: borrowers with partial entitlement (the CLL is part of the bonus-entitlement formula) and lenders' secondary-market pricing decisions. The 2026 baseline CLL is $832,750 per FHFA's 11-25-2025 announcement; high-cost areas can go up to $1,249,125. None of Alabama's 67 counties exceeded the baseline as of FHFA's 2026 release. Source: https://www.fhfa.gov/news/news-release/fhfa-announces-conforming-loan-limit-values-for-2026

**Jumbo VA loans.** A loan above the CLL is a jumbo for secondary-market purposes, but for a full-entitlement VA borrower, no down payment is required at the federal level. Many lenders apply overlays (additional down payment requirements, higher credit minimums) for jumbo VA loans because of investor preferences in the Ginnie Mae securitization market.

**Funding fee changes from the same Act.** Covered in Section 5. The Act adjusted the funding fee schedule and equalized fees between regular military and Guard/Reserve borrowers. The adjustment was originally scheduled to expire on 01-01-2022, but the Johnny Isakson Act of 2020 extended the rates through 10-01-2030 (per Pub. L. 116-315 § 2103).

**What did NOT change:** Borrowers with partial entitlement (those with an active VA loan, or a still-encumbered property whose entitlement is tied up, or a prior loss to VA) still face the entitlement-calculation rules. The Act did not eliminate the math for partial-entitlement borrowers; it only removed the loan limit for full-entitlement borrowers.

### 4.3 Loan limit mechanics for borrowers without full entitlement

For a borrower with partial entitlement seeking a loan over $144,000, VA's guaranty calculation, per Circular 26-25-10 (12-01-2025):

> Remaining entitlement = (25% × applicable conforming loan limit) − previously used entitlement that has not been restored

**Maximum no-down-payment loan amount.** VA-guaranteed loans are typically pooled in Ginnie Mae securities that require a 25 percent total guaranty (VA's guaranty plus borrower equity). If the borrower has less than 25 percent of the loan amount in remaining entitlement, the borrower must either bring a down payment or accept a smaller loan. The practical formula lenders use:

> Maximum no-down-payment loan = remaining entitlement × 4

**Worked example structure for partial-entitlement borrowers in Madison County, Alabama (2026 CLL = $832,750):**

Scenario A: Veteran with $50,000 of previously used entitlement, no other VA loan still encumbering it, wants to buy a home in Madison County.

- 25% of CLL: $832,750 × 0.25 = $208,187.50, rounded to $208,187
- Less used entitlement: $208,187 − $50,000 = $158,187
- Remaining entitlement: $158,187
- Maximum no-down-payment loan: $158,187 × 4 = $632,750
- For a property priced above $632,750, the borrower would need to bring a down payment equal to 25 percent of the amount above $632,750.

Scenario B: Same veteran, same county, wants to buy a home priced at $700,000.

- Loan needed at zero down: $700,000
- 25% of $700,000: $175,000 of guaranty needed
- Remaining entitlement: $158,187
- Shortfall: $175,000 − $158,187 = $16,813 down payment required to satisfy the 25 percent total guaranty target.

**Madison, Limestone, and Morgan County, Alabama 2026 CLLs:** All three counties are at the 2026 baseline of $832,750 per the FHFA 2026 conforming loan limit map (https://www.fhfa.gov/data/dashboard/conforming-loan-limit-values-map). No Alabama county is in a high-cost designation for 2026. (Verify current values annually each November/December when FHFA announces.)

**Jumbo VA loans with partial entitlement.** When the loan amount exceeds the county CLL and the borrower has partial entitlement, the down payment math gets more involved. VA's guaranty caps at 25 percent of the CLL minus used entitlement; the borrower must cover the rest up to a 25 percent total. Direct partial-entitlement jumbo questions to a VA-experienced lender for borrower-specific math.

### 4.4 Second-tier (bonus) entitlement in practice

Bonus entitlement is most commonly used in active-duty PCS scenarios where a service member wants to retain a previously VA-financed home as a rental and use the remaining bonus entitlement to purchase a new primary residence at the new duty station.

**Common scenarios at military installations.**

- PCS to Redstone Arsenal in Huntsville from a prior duty station, where the veteran retains the prior home as a rental.
- Inverse PCS away from Huntsville while keeping a Madison County home as a rental.
- Spouse-assigned PCS where one military spouse uses entitlement at the new duty station while the other manages the rental at the old duty station.

**Documentation requirements.** Generally include:

- Evidence of a PCS order or other change in duty station (when invoking the occupancy exception);
- Lease agreements or rental documentation if the prior home is being retained as a rental;
- Proof that the borrower has sufficient remaining entitlement under the calculation in 4.3.

**Restoration of entitlement after refinance, sale, or short sale.** Restoration is automatic when the prior VA loan is paid in full and the property is sold. Otherwise, the borrower must affirmatively request restoration, generally on VA Form 26-1880 Section II.

### 4.5 One-time restoration without sale

Statutory carve-out at 38 USC § 3702 allowing one-time restoration of entitlement on a property the borrower still owns. Most commonly used when:

- The borrower refinances a VA loan into conventional financing (paying off the VA loan), wishes to keep the property as a rental or second home, and wants to free entitlement to use a VA loan on a new primary residence.
- This option is once-per-lifetime. Document the request on VA Form 26-1880 Section II and submit through a participating lender via WebLGY for fastest turnaround.

## 5. VA Funding Fee and Exemptions

### 5.1 Funding fee mechanics

The VA funding fee is a one-time fee paid on most VA-guaranteed loans. Per 38 USC § 3729, the fee is the primary funding mechanism for the VA Loan Guaranty Program, replacing the cost that would otherwise be borne by taxpayers. Source: https://www.va.gov/housing-assistance/home-loans/funding-fee-and-closing-costs/ (last updated 01-15-2026); rate authority at 38 USC § 3729(b)(2); Circular 26-23-6 (02-14-2023) implemented the current rate schedule effective for loans closed on or after 04-07-2023.

**Plain-English explanation.** The fee is a percentage of the loan amount. It pays for the program. Most borrowers finance the fee into the loan; some pay it in cash; some have the seller cover it as a concession; many are exempt entirely.

### Current funding fee rates (effective 04-07-2023; in force as of 05-09-2026)

| Purchase / construction                                         | 5% to less than 10% | 1.5%      | 1.5%            |
| Purchase / construction                                         | 10% or more         | 1.25%     | 1.25%           |

The Blue Water Navy Vietnam Veterans Act of 2019 originally raised the 2020-2021 fees and equalized rates between regular military and Guard/Reserve. The Johnny Isakson Act of 2020 (Pub. L. 116-315 § 2103) extended these rates through 10-01-2030, after which the rates revert per the underlying statute unless further extended.

### 5.2 How the funding fee is paid

**Three methods.**

1. **Financed into the loan amount (most common).** The funding fee is added to the loan principal. The borrower pays no out-of-pocket fee at closing for the funding fee specifically, but the monthly P&I is slightly higher because the loan balance is larger. The fee plus the loan amount cannot exceed VA's reasonable value for the property; the financed fee does not count toward LTV for the appraised-value test, but it does increase the actual loan balance.
2. **Paid in cash at closing.** The borrower writes a check or wires the fee at closing. This results in a lower loan amount and lower lifetime interest cost, but a higher cash-to-close.
3. **Paid by the seller as part of seller concessions.** The funding fee, when paid by the seller, counts toward the 4 percent seller concession cap discussed in Section 7.2.

**Effect on LTV calculations.** When financed, the fee is added to the loan balance. For LTV calculations on cash-out refinances, the financed fee is included in the total loan amount; the resulting LTV cannot exceed 100 percent of reasonable value (per Circular 26-19-5).

### 5.3 Funding fee exemptions

This is one of the most-overlooked areas of VA loan content. The exemption categories per VA's funding fee page:

A borrower is exempt from the funding fee if any of these are true:

- **Receiving VA compensation for a service-connected disability** (any rating that yields compensation);
- **Eligible to receive VA compensation but receiving retirement or active-duty pay instead** (the "would be entitled" rule). This applies to retirees who waived disability compensation in favor of retirement pay, and to active-duty members with a pre-discharge claim awaiting effective date.
- **Receiving Dependency and Indemnity Compensation (DIC) as a surviving spouse;**
- **A service member who has received a proposed or memorandum rating before the loan closing date stating eligibility for compensation due to a pre-discharge claim;**
- **An active-duty member of the Armed Forces who, on or before the loan closing date, provides evidence of having received a Purple Heart** (per Circular 26-19-23 implementing the Blue Water Navy Act).

**Documentation requirements.**

- The COE typically reflects exempt status when VA has the disability rating or DIC information in its database.
- VA Form 26-8937 (Verification of VA Benefits) confirms exempt status when the COE alone is insufficient or the rating decision is pending.
- For Purple Heart exemption: VA accepts a Purple Heart Certificate, a DD-214 clearly showing the Purple Heart award, or military orders. Evidence must be received by VA or the lender on or before the closing date (per Circular 26-19-23 / 6-19-30).

**What happens if a borrower closes paying the fee and is later determined exempt.** The borrower may be eligible for a refund. The refund process is initiated through the St. Petersburg RLC (for Alabama borrowers). The retroactive disability rating must have an effective date that predates the loan closing for the funding fee refund to apply.

**Disability rating decisions pending at the time of closing.** VA's current rule is that if a proposed or memorandum rating is issued before closing, the borrower may be exempt. If the rating decision is issued after closing, even if retroactive to before closing, a refund is potentially available, but the borrower must still pay the fee (or finance it) at closing. Direct case-specific timing questions to the St. Petersburg RLC at 1-877-827-3702.

### 5.4 Funding fee refunds

The refund process applies when:

- The borrower paid the funding fee at closing or financed it; and
- The borrower is later determined to have been exempt as of a date that predates the closing.

**Process.** The St. Petersburg RLC processes refund requests for Alabama borrowers. Documentation typically includes the rating decision letter, the closing disclosure showing the fee paid, and a refund request through the lender or directly through the RLC.

**Retroactive disability rating decisions.** If VA issues a rating with an effective date before the loan closing date, the borrower can request a refund. If the effective date is after closing, the funding fee is not refundable on that basis.

**Important compliance note.** Marketing copy must never promise a specific refund timeline or guarantee that any borrower will be exempt or eligible for refund. These are case-by-case determinations made by VA, not by the lender.

## 6. VA Appraisal and Minimum Property Requirements

### 6.1 The VA appraisal process

The VA appraisal serves two distinct purposes that are sometimes conflated in marketing copy:

1. **Establish reasonable value.** The appraiser determines the fair market value supporting the loan.
2. **Confirm Minimum Property Requirements (MPRs).** The appraiser verifies that the property meets VA's safety, structural soundness, and sanitation requirements per VA Pamphlet 26-7, Chapter 12.

**Notice of Value (NOV).** The output of the VA appraisal is the Notice of Value, issued by the lender's Staff Appraisal Reviewer (SAR) under VA's Lender Appraisal Processing Program (LAPP). The NOV is the document that establishes the official VA reasonable value for the transaction. The NOV may be conditional on repairs being completed before closing.

**The VA-assigned appraiser system.** Unlike conventional appraisals (which the lender or Appraisal Management Company orders), VA appraisals are assigned by VA on a rotational basis from the VA fee panel. The lender requests a VA case number through WebLGY, and VA's system assigns an appraiser. Neither the borrower, the seller, nor the lender chooses the appraiser. This independence is a structural feature of the VA program and is one reason VA appraisals are generally not subject to the same conflict-of-interest concerns that periodically affect conventional appraisals.

**Typical VA appraisal turnaround.** VA publishes statewide and county-level timeliness targets in business days, available at https://www.benefits.va.gov/HOMELOANS/appraiser_fee_schedule.asp. National typical range is 7 to 20 business days. Alabama is generally on the shorter end of the range. Liquidation appraisal timeliness matches typical assignment timelines for the geographic area.

**VA appraisal fee schedule for Alabama.** Per the VA Appraisal Fee Schedule (https://www.benefits.va.gov/HOMELOANS/appraiser_fee_schedule.asp), Alabama single-family fee caps as of 05-09-2026 are typically in the $600 range, with Alabama in the standard cost tier. VA-posted fees are maximum allowable; appraisers may charge less. Add-ons include $50 for proposed/under-construction; $150 for re-inspection; mileage when service is outside the assigned coverage area (subject to GSA mileage rates and prior VA approval). The VA rescinded the prior $50 surcharge authorization in recent years; appraisers are no longer authorized to charge $50 above the schedule fee. Verify current Alabama caps at the VA fee schedule URL above before quoting in reader-facing copy.

**Tidewater Initiative.** When a VA appraiser concludes that the appraisal will likely come in below the contract price, the appraiser must invoke the Tidewater Initiative before issuing the NOV. Per VA Circular 26-17-18 and the Pamphlet 26-7, Chapter 10 procedure:

- The appraiser notifies the requester (typically the lender or buyer's agent) that the value will likely fall short.
- The lender, real estate agent, or veteran has 48 hours (two business days) to submit additional supporting market data (closed comparable sales, special property features, contract concessions data) for the appraiser's consideration.
- The appraiser reviews the submission and finalizes the appraisal report.

Tidewater is an evidence window. It is not a negotiation tool. Submissions should be neutral, fact-based market data, never directives toward a target value, which the appraiser is required to disregard.

**Reconsideration of Value (ROV).** If the NOV is issued at a value below the contract price, the borrower (and only the borrower, per VA rules) may request a formal Reconsideration of Value through the lender. Per VA's congressionally mandated 2023 report, the ROV process is:

- The veteran submits a written ROV request through the lender, supported by:
  - Up to 3 comparable sales not used in the original appraisal (closed sales, with full MLS documentation);
  - Identification of any factual errors (square footage, condition, comparable selection);
  - Optional borrower letter explaining valuation rationale.
- The ROV is reviewed by VA staff (the Staff Appraisal Reviewer for LAPP-approved lenders, or VA's Construction & Valuation team for non-LAPP files).
- VA targets a 5 business-day review for ROVs, but actual timelines vary.
- VA may issue an amended NOV if the additional evidence supports a higher value.

ROV outcomes are not guaranteed. Many ROVs are denied if the additional comparables are not materially superior to those used in the original appraisal. A clear, organized, evidence-driven ROV package has a meaningfully better chance of success than a narrative-only request.

### 6.2 Minimum Property Requirements (MPRs)

VA Pamphlet 26-7 Chapter 12 codifies the MPRs. Source: https://www.benefits.va.gov/WARMS/docs/admin26/m26-07/Ch12_Minimum_Property_Requirement_NEW.pdf

Note: VA published an Advance Notice of Proposed Rulemaking on 12-11-2023 (88 FR 85862) seeking public comment on aligning MPRs with the Uniform Appraisal Dataset condition ratings (C1 to C4). As of 05-09-2026, VA has not issued a final rule, and Chapter 12 MPRs remain in force. Watch for further rulemaking under the Improving Access to the VA Home Loan Benefit Act of 2022.

**Core MPR categories (from Chapter 12 with practical issues):**

**Safe and sanitary water supply.**

- Public water: minimal documentation required.
- Private well: water testing required by a disinterested third party; collection and transport must be by a party acceptable to the local health authority. Common testing standards include EPA private well guidelines.
- Test failure (bacteria, nitrates, lead) typically triggers either treatment system installation or alternative water supply before closing.

**Sewage disposal.**

- Municipal sewer connection: minimal documentation.
- Septic system: inspection, sometimes pump-out documentation. Failed septic typically requires repair or replacement before closing.

**Roof condition and remaining useful life.** The roof must be expected to last a "reasonable future utility" period. Active leaks, missing shingles, sagging, or visible deterioration trigger required repairs.

**Heating adequate for healthful and comfortable living conditions.**

- A functional permanent heat source is required.
- Unvented space heaters as the sole heat source require additional review (Section 11.12 of Chapter 12).
- Wood-burning stoves as the primary heat source must be supplemented by a permanent conventional system that maintains at least 50°F in plumbed areas.

**Electrical safety.** No exposed wiring; functional service. Frayed or visibly damaged wiring must be repaired regardless of property location.

**Lead-based paint (homes built before 1978).** Peeling, chipping, or cracking paint must be repaired (scraped and repainted with non-lead-based paint) before closing. This is one of the most common MPR triggers in older Alabama neighborhoods, particularly in pre-1978 housing stock in Huntsville, Decatur, Athens, and Madison.

**Wood-destroying organisms.** Termite inspection is required on virtually every VA transaction in Alabama under the state's specific WDO inspection requirement. The NPMA-33 form (Wood-Destroying Insect Inspection Report) is the standard. Active infestation requires treatment; visible damage requires repair before closing.

**Crawl space access and ventilation.** Crawl spaces must be accessible (typically 18 inches by 24 inches minimum opening), ventilated, and free of excessive moisture or standing water.

**Drainage and grading away from foundation.** Water must flow away from the foundation. Pooling water, negative grading, or evident water entry to the crawl space or basement triggers required corrections.

**Property access.** The property must have year-round vehicle access on a public or permanent private road with an all-weather surface. Properties accessed only by dirt or gravel roads can sometimes pass MPR; properties with seasonal-only access typically cannot.

**Defective conditions affecting safety, structural soundness, or sanitation.** Catch-all category for any condition that materially affects habitability.

### 6.3 Common MPR issues that derail VA transactions in Alabama

Practical experience and Chapter 12 guidance, combined:

- **Peeling exterior paint on pre-1978 homes** in older Huntsville, Decatur, Athens, and similar neighborhoods. Repair scope can range from spot-priming to full re-paint.
- **Roof condition on older homes.** Roofs near the end of useful life or with visible deterioration are the most common appraisal-triggered repair item in Alabama's older housing stock.
- **Active termite infestation or unrepaired termite damage.** Alabama's climate makes termite issues common. The NPMA-33 inspection is required on virtually every Alabama VA transaction. Active infestation triggers treatment (typically $400 to $1,500 depending on home size); visible damage triggers structural repair.
- **Private well water test failures.** Particularly relevant in Limestone, Morgan, Marshall, Jackson, and DeKalb counties where well water is more common than in central Madison County.
- **Septic system issues** on rural properties.
- **GFCI outlets in wet areas** (kitchen, bathroom, garage, exterior) under newer interpretations of MPR electrical safety.
- **Handrails on stairs with more than three risers.** This includes both interior and exterior stairs.
- **Missing or non-functioning smoke detectors.**

### 6.4 Repair requirements and resolution paths

**Who can perform required repairs.** In most cases, the seller pays for and completes the required repairs before closing. Buyer-paid repairs are sometimes structured (the buyer brings cash to closing or finances repair allowances), but VA generally treats this as the buyer doing work on a property they do not yet own, which creates documentation complexity.

**Escrow holdbacks for completion after closing.** Allowed in limited circumstances under Pamphlet 26-7 Chapter 9, particularly for weather-dependent repairs that cannot be completed before closing (exterior paint in winter, landscaping, roofing during severe weather). Holdback amounts are typically 1.5x the repair estimate.

**Termite treatment requirements when WDO is found.** Treatment by a licensed pest control operator before closing; documentation includes the NPMA-33 reflecting active infestation, the treatment company's invoice, and a follow-up NPMA-33 confirming treatment.

**Seller's posture toward MPR repairs and offer-strategy implications.** This is where buyer's agent skill matters. Strong VA buyer's agents pre-negotiate the seller's commitment to MPR repairs at offer time, often using the standard contract addendum that specifies the seller's obligation to complete VA-required repairs. Listing agents who default to "as-is" terms often reject VA offers without understanding that MPRs are not a perfection standard and that most MPR repairs are minor.

## 7. VA Closing Costs and Allowable Fees

### 7.1 The 1% origination cap and allowable fees

VA Pamphlet 26-7 Chapter 8 codifies allowable and unallowable fees. Source: https://www.benefits.va.gov/WARMS/docs/admin26/m26-07/Chapter_8_Borrower_Fees_and_Charges_and_the_VA_Funding_Fee.pdf. VA Circular 26-10-01 governs the 1% origination cap framework.

**The 1% origination fee cap.** A lender may charge the veteran a flat origination fee of up to 1 percent of the loan amount in lieu of itemized lender fees. The 1 percent fee covers all lender overhead, processing, underwriting, document preparation, courier, photo, lock fees, and similar administrative charges.

**Two structures, with the same outcome.** Per Circular 26-10-01:

- **Flat 1% origination:** If the lender charges the full 1 percent flat fee, the lender may NOT additionally itemize lender overhead charges. Stacking a 1 percent origination with separate processing or underwriting fees is a violation.
- **Itemized lender fees:** If the lender does not charge a flat 1 percent fee, the lender may itemize specific lender charges, but the aggregate total of itemized lender charges may not exceed 1 percent of the loan amount.

**Allowable fees the veteran may pay (in addition to or in lieu of the 1% lender cap).** Per Chapter 8 and Circular 26-10-01:

- VA appraisal fee
- Credit report
- Title insurance (lender's and owner's policies) and title-related charges (including title examination by an attorney, which is allowed even though attorney fees for the lender's settlement work are not)
- Recording fees
- Survey
- Hazard insurance premiums
- Flood zone determination
- Environmental Protection (NFIP) insurance premiums when applicable
- VA funding fee (separate from the 1% origination, as Section 5 details)
- Discount points (separate from the 1% cap; treated as a borrower-paid pricing concession)
- Notary, courier, and similar third-party fees as itemized when not charged separately by the lender
- WDO/termite inspection fee in states (including Alabama) where the inspection is required by the NOV

Per Circular 26-24-19 (09-13-2024), itemized fees and charges paid to third parties must be supported by invoices or documentation of actual cost; lenders cannot mark up third-party fees beyond the actual cost paid.

**Tax service fees.** Historically a non-allowable Veteran-paid fee. Verify current treatment via Chapter 8 and the State Fees & Charges Deviations List under Circular 26-24-19.

**Non-allowable fees the veteran may not pay** (must be paid by the seller, lender, or absorbed):

- Attorney fees for the lender's settlement work (NOT title examination, that is allowed)
- Brokerage fees (other than the limited buyer-broker variance discussed in Section 7.5)
- Loan-finder fees
- Prepayment penalties (largely a non-issue under modern VA rules but listed in the handbook)
- HUD/FHA inspection fees
- Photo fees
- Loan application fees beyond the 1% origination cap
- Lender-only inspection fees
- Postage, courier, document preparation, and similar overhead when the lender has charged the 1% flat fee

**Buyer-broker charges (temporary local variance).** Per Circular 26-24-14 (06-11-2024) and Change 1 (08-05-2024), VA established a temporary local variance allowing veterans to pay reasonable and customary buyer-broker compensation on purchase contracts signed on or after 08-10-2024, subject to specific conditions (see Section 7.5). The variance is "valid until rescinded" as of 05-09-2026. This is a recent and material change and should be tracked closely; VA will likely formalize or modify the rule via formal rulemaking in a future cycle.

### 7.2 Seller concessions under VA rules

VA Pamphlet 26-7 Chapter 8 limits "seller concessions" to 4 percent of the established reasonable value of the property. The critical distinction (and a frequent point of confusion in practice):

**Customary closing costs paid by the seller are NOT seller concessions and are NOT subject to the 4% cap.**

What counts toward the 4% concession cap:

- VA funding fee paid by the seller
- Prepayment of buyer's hazard insurance premiums
- Prepayment of buyer's property taxes
- Payoff of buyer's pre-existing debts (credit cards, auto loans, judgments)
- Temporary buydown funds (1-0, 2-1, 3-2-1 buydowns)
- Gift items conveyed at no additional cost (appliances if not in the original contract, televisions, decor)

What does NOT count toward the 4% cap (customary closing costs the seller can pay):

- Origination fee (the lender's 1 percent)
- Title insurance (lender's policy and owner's policy)
- Recording fees
- Discount points (when normal market discount points, not buydowns)
- Survey
- Other typical closing costs

**The practical math.** A VA buyer can negotiate seller-paid customary closing costs (uncapped) plus up to 4 percent in concessions for things like funding fee, prepaid taxes/insurance, debt payoff, or buydowns. This stacks well: a $500,000 home in Madison County could carry, for example, $15,000 in seller-paid customary closing costs plus $20,000 (4 percent) in seller concessions for funding fee, taxes, insurance, and debt payoff, without violating either VA cap.

**Comparison with FHA and conventional.**

- FHA seller concession cap: 6% of sales price.
- Conventional: tiered cap based on LTV (3% if LTV > 90%, 6% if LTV between 75% and 90%, 9% below 75%) for primary residences.

VA's 4% cap looks restrictive on its face but is functionally generous because it sits on top of uncapped customary closing costs.

### 7.3 Funding fee at closing

Covered in Section 5.2. The funding fee can be financed, paid in cash, or absorbed by seller concessions (counting toward the 4% cap). Disclosure on the Closing Disclosure (CD) follows TILA-RESPA Integrated Disclosure rules; the funding fee appears as a section B fee on the CD.

### 7.4 Earnest money in VA transactions

**Typical earnest money expectations in Alabama VA transactions.** Earnest money in north Alabama VA transactions typically ranges from $500 to $5,000, with the higher end common in Madison County purchases above $400,000. There is no federal VA rule on earnest money amounts; this is purely a contract-negotiation matter governed by Alabama real estate practice and the buyer's contract.

**The VA escape clause (38 CFR § 36.4303(k)(4)).** Federally required in every VA purchase contract. The exact statutory language:

> It is expressly agreed that, notwithstanding any other provisions of this contract, the purchaser shall not incur any penalty by forfeiture of earnest money or otherwise be obligated to complete the purchase of the property described herein, if the contract purchase price or cost exceeds the reasonable value of the property established by the Department of Veterans Affairs. The purchaser shall, however, have the privilege and option of proceeding with the consummation of this contract without regard to the amount of the reasonable value established by the Department of Veterans Affairs.

Authority: 38 U.S.C. §§ 501, 3703(c)(1).

**How the escape clause must appear.** The clause must be in the contract before closing. If the contract was signed before the appraisal/NOV, the clause must be added by amendment (often called the "VA Amendment to Contract" or "VA Amendatory/Escape Clause"). The lender verifies the clause is in the file before processing the loan.

**The buyer's right to walk and recover earnest money if the appraisal is short.** When the NOV comes in below contract price, the buyer has three contract options:

1. Renegotiate the contract price down to the appraised value;
2. Proceed at the contract price by bringing the appraisal-gap difference to closing in cash (the loan amount is capped at the appraised value);
3. Walk away from the contract and recover earnest money pursuant to the escape clause.

The escape clause does not require any particular reason for the buyer to walk if the appraisal is short. The buyer cannot be required to forfeit earnest money or pay penalties when the contract price exceeds the VA's reasonable value.

**Important limit.** The escape clause protects earnest money on the home purchase. It does not protect funds spent on builder upgrades, custom finishes, or change orders in new construction. Those are typically non-refundable once paid to the builder.

### 7.5 Buyer-broker compensation under the temporary local variance

Per Circular 26-24-14 (06-11-2024) and Circular 26-24-15 (07-19-2024), with Change 1 (08-05-2024):

**Effective date.** Applies to purchase contracts signed on or after 08-10-2024.

**Conditions for veteran-paid buyer-broker compensation to be allowable:**

- Listing brokers are prohibited from setting buyer-broker compensation through MLS postings; or
- Buyer-broker compensation cannot be established by, or flow through, the listing broker;
- Buyer-broker charges are not financed into the loan amount;
- Buyer-broker charges are considered when determining whether the borrower has sufficient liquid assets to close;
- The total amount paid by the borrower is recorded in lines 1 through 3 of section H ("Other") of the Closing Disclosure;
- The buyer-broker representation agreement is uploaded as part of the appraisal request package.

**Required components of the buyer-broker representation agreement** (per Circular 26-24-14 and consistent with the post-2024 NAR settlement framework):

- A prominent disclosure of the amount or rate of compensation;
- Compensation must be objective ($0, X flat fee, X percent, X hourly rate), not open-ended;
- A term prohibiting the agent from receiving compensation from any other source over the agreed-upon amount;
- The specific services to be provided.

**Practical implications for north Alabama VA transactions.** The most common pattern is still seller-paid buyer-broker compensation (negotiated via the offer or covered by a pre-existing seller-side cooperation arrangement), but the variance gives veterans the option to pay buyer-broker compensation directly when sellers will not. Because the charges cannot be financed, this is a cash-to-close item.

Track the variance status quarterly. VA will eventually replace the temporary variance with formal rulemaking; the rule may differ from current variance terms.

## 8. VA Offer Strategy and Competitive Markets

### 8.1 The "VA offers don't get accepted" myth

The persistent listing-agent skepticism about VA offers is durable but largely unsupported by current data and current rules. The myth has roots in two real historical issues:

1. **Pre-2020 loan limit constraints.** Before the Blue Water Navy Act took effect on 01-01-2020, VA loans above the county loan limit required a down payment, which made high-priced VA offers genuinely harder to structure.
2. **Older Pamphlet 26-7 MPR rules.** Before the 2019 Chapter 12 revision, MPRs included some language that lenders and listing agents read as more restrictive than current rules.

Neither concern is current. Full-entitlement VA borrowers face no loan limit. Chapter 12 was revised in 2019 to clarify that MPRs are a habitability standard, not a perfection standard. VA appraisals are not categorically more conservative than conventional appraisals. VA closing timelines in 2025 and 2026 are competitive with conventional and FHA timelines, typically 30 to 45 days from contract to close.

**Counter-narrative facts that should appear in any VA offer-strategy content:**

- The VA appraisal and a conventional appraisal use the same comparable sales methodology. The MPR check is in addition to value, not in lieu of value.
- VA appraisers are independently assigned by VA, not by the lender. This independence is actually a structural advantage in valuation accuracy.
- MPR repairs are typically minor (paint, handrails, smoke detectors, GFCI outlets, well water tests). Major MPR-driven deal kills are uncommon.
- VA closing timelines in north Alabama track 30 to 45 days, comparable to conventional and FHA.
- VA offers are routinely accepted in competitive markets when the offer terms (price, contingency timing, earnest money) are competitive.

### 8.2 Strategic offer components for VA buyers

**Earnest money.** $500 to $5,000 is typical in north Alabama. Higher earnest money signals seriousness. The escape clause protects the earnest money in the event of a low appraisal, so buyers can offer competitive earnest money without taking on uncapped risk.

**Inspection contingency timing.** Standard 7- to 10-day inspection contingencies are typical. Aggressive offers sometimes shorten this to 5 days.

**Appraisal contingency interplay with the VA escape clause.** Because the federal VA escape clause is mandatory, the buyer's appraisal protection is automatic regardless of whether the contract includes a separate appraisal contingency. Some VA buyers waive the standard appraisal contingency to make the offer more competitive, knowing they retain the federal escape-clause protection. This is a sophisticated strategy and only works correctly when:

- The escape clause is present in the contract (it must be);
- The buyer is comfortable foregoing other appraisal-related protections that the contractual contingency may carry.

**Closing date.** VA closing timelines in Alabama are typically 30 to 45 days. Sellers focused on speed can be offered 21- to 30-day VA closings if the buyer's lender pre-underwrites the file.

**Seller-paid closing cost requests.** Strong VA offers structure seller-paid closing costs as customary closing costs (which do not count toward the 4% concession cap) rather than as concessions, leaving room within the 4% cap for funding fee or buydowns.

**Repairs vs. concessions.** When MPR repairs are anticipated, the offer can specify either seller-completed repairs (most common) or repair credits structured as concessions within the 4% cap.

### 8.3 Listing agent and seller education

Common misconceptions VA buyers face from listing agents and how a buyer's agent should frame the response:

- **"VA appraisers always come in low."** False. VA appraisers use the same comparable sales methodology as conventional appraisers and are independently assigned. There is no structural reason to expect categorically lower values.
- **"VA will require everything to be perfect."** False. MPRs are a habitability standard. Most VA repairs are minor.
- **"Sellers can't choose the appraiser."** True. Neither buyer nor seller chooses the VA appraiser. This applies equally in conventional appraisals under modern AMC rules.
- **"VA buyers can't pay closing costs."** False. VA buyers can pay all customary closing costs. VA limits which lender-overhead fees they can pay (the 1% origination cap) but does not prohibit veterans from paying typical buyer closing costs.
- **"VA loans take forever to close."** False in 2025-2026. Current Alabama VA closings track 30 to 45 days, comparable to conventional and FHA.
- **"You can't waive the appraisal contingency on a VA loan."** Misleading. The federal escape clause is non-waivable, but a separate contractual appraisal contingency can be waived if the buyer chooses, with the understanding that the escape clause still operates.

### 8.4 Multiple offer scenarios

**Strengthening a VA offer without sacrificing buyer protections.**

- Higher earnest money (the escape clause protects it on appraisal-gap walks).
- Shorter inspection contingency.
- Pre-underwritten loan approval (often called "fully underwritten" or "TBD approval") that demonstrates the loan is contingent only on the property.
- Clear statement of buyer's flexibility on closing date.
- Personal letter to seller (note: NAR's revised guidance has cooled buyer-letter recommendations on fair-housing concerns; check current NAR guidance before recommending).

**Escalation clauses and VA-specific cautions.** Escalation clauses work mechanically the same on VA offers as on conventional. The cap on the escalation should remain at or below the buyer's pre-approval amount. The escape clause still operates if the appraisal comes in below the escalated price.

**Appraisal gap coverage.** A VA buyer can offer to cover an appraisal gap in cash (the "we'll close at the contract price even if the appraisal is short" structure). The escape clause still gives the buyer the option to walk if they choose, but offering appraisal gap coverage signals strong commitment. The cap on appraisal gap coverage should be set based on what cash the buyer is genuinely willing to bring.

**Cash-back-to-buyer offers.** VA does not allow cash to flow back to the buyer at closing as a contractual benefit beyond the buyer's actual paid items being refunded. Any "cash back to buyer" structure must be carefully reviewed for compliance with both VA rules and TRID.

## 9. VA Refinance Mechanics

### 9.1 Interest Rate Reduction Refinance Loan (IRRRL)

The IRRRL (pronounced "Earl") is VA's streamline refinance program, codified at 38 USC § 3710(a)(8) and (a)(11), with seasoning, NTB, and recoupment standards under 38 USC § 3709. Implementing rules at 38 CFR § 36.4307. Current VA policy guidance is consolidated in Circular 26-19-22 and Change 1.

**What makes the IRRRL streamlined.** Per 38 CFR § 36.4307 and Pamphlet 26-7, an IRRRL generally does not require:

- A new appraisal (NOV);
- A new credit underwriting package (income, employment, full asset documentation);
- A new Certificate of Eligibility (the prior COE is reused);
- VA pre-approval (most IRRRLs are processed under automatic authority).

The IRRRL replaces an existing VA-guaranteed loan with a new VA-guaranteed loan on the same property, with the goal of reducing the borrower's interest rate, payment, or overall financial position. The IRRRL cannot be used to refinance a non-VA loan into a VA loan; that requires a cash-out refinance.

**Net Tangible Benefit (NTB) requirements.** Per 38 USC § 3709 and Circular 26-19-22, the IRRRL must satisfy specific NTB tests:

For fixed-rate-to-fixed-rate IRRRLs:

- New interest rate must be at least 0.50 percentage points lower than the existing rate (the "0.5% rule").

For fixed-rate-to-ARM IRRRLs:

- New initial interest rate must be at least 2.00 percentage points lower than the existing fixed rate.

For ARM-to-fixed IRRRLs:

- Generally evaluated as a stability benefit, with no specific rate-reduction threshold but the recoupment and overall benefit tests still apply.

**Seasoning requirements (the 210-day and six-payment rule).** Per Pub. L. 115-174 (the Economic Growth, Regulatory Relief, and Consumer Protection Act of 2018) codified at 38 USC § 3709(c) and clarified by Pub. L. 116-33 (Protecting Affordable Mortgages for Veterans Act of 2019):

The IRRRL note date must be the **later of**:

- 210 days after the first payment due date on the existing loan; AND
- The date the sixth monthly payment is made on the existing loan.

In practice, both conditions must be satisfied. A loan that has had six payments made within 180 days does not qualify; a loan that has had fewer than six payments by day 210 does not qualify.

**36-month recoupment requirement.** Per 38 USC § 3709(a) and Circular 26-19-22, the lender must certify that all fees, closing costs, and expenses incurred in the refinance (other than taxes, escrow, and fees paid under chapter 37, including the funding fee) are recouped through reduced monthly P&I payments within 36 months of the note date.

The recoupment formula:

> Recoupment period (months) = Total recoupable fees and costs ÷ Monthly P&I reduction

If the recoupment period exceeds 36 months, the loan does not qualify for VA guaranty.

Excluded from recoupment math: real estate taxes, hazard insurance escrow, prepaid interest, and the VA funding fee. Included in recoupment math: lender origination, third-party closing costs (title, recording, etc.), and any other charges the borrower would not have paid absent the refinance.

**IRRRL funding fee.** 0.5% of the new loan amount, regardless of first or subsequent use, with the same exemption categories as purchase loans (Section 5).

**Comparison disclosure requirement.** Per 38 USC § 3709(b), the lender must provide the borrower with a Loan Comparison Disclosure showing the existing loan and the new loan side-by-side. Required at the time of the initial Loan Estimate, with any revisions to the prior comparison disclosure, and at the final Closing Disclosure. The borrower must sign the final comparison disclosure to acknowledge receipt.

**Predatory IRRRL solicitation patterns and CFPB enforcement.** The IRRRL's streamlined nature has made it a recurring target for predatory mailers and lender churning. The CFPB's 2020 enforcement sweep produced 9+ consent orders against lenders sending deceptive IRRRL mailers. Common patterns flagged in CFPB orders:

- Use of VA logos, seals, or look-alike government formatting suggesting endorsement (Service 1st, Hypotec, Sovereign Lending, Prime Choice, ClearPath, Low VA Rates, RMK Financial, others);
- Fake form numbers like "VA-1211 Benefit Allotment Notice" or "Form 21-0760 Eligibility Notification";
- Misrepresenting that benefits will expire on a date certain;
- Promising "skip 1 or 2 mortgage payments" (which VA prohibits as an advertised benefit per Circular 26-19-22);
- Promising specific escrow refund amounts;
- Hypothetical loan term comparisons that the lender was not actually prepared to offer.

CFPB civil money penalties from the 2020 sweep totaled over $4.4 million across the orders.

Sources:

- ClearPath Lending consent order: https://www.consumerfinance.gov/enforcement/actions/clearpath-lending-inc/
- Low VA Rates consent order: https://files.consumerfinance.gov/f/documents/cfpb_low-va-rates-llc_consent-order_2020-10.pdf
- RMK Financial consent order: https://files.consumerfinance.gov/f/documents/cfpb_rmk-financial-corp-majestic-home-loan-mhl_consent-order_2023-02.pdf
- CFPB / VA WARNO joint warning: https://www.consumerfinance.gov/about-us/blog/cfpb-and-va-warno-va-refinancing-offers-sound-too-good-be-true/

### 9.2 VA Cash-Out Refinance

Codified at 38 USC § 3710(a)(5) and (a)(9), with rules at 38 CFR § 36.4306. Implementing guidance in Circulars 26-18-30 and 26-19-5.

**Type I vs. Type II distinction.** Per 38 USC § 3709 and Circular 26-19-5:

- **Type I cash-out refinance:** The new loan amount (including the financed funding fee) does not exceed the payoff amount of the loan being refinanced. Effectively a no-cash-out refinance, used to refinance to a better rate or term, or to refinance a non-VA loan into a VA loan, or to convert from a different VA loan type.
- **Type II cash-out refinance:** The new loan amount (including the financed funding fee) exceeds the payoff amount of the loan being refinanced. The classic "cash out equity" scenario.

**Maximum LTV for cash-out refinances.** Per Circular 26-18-30, VA will not guarantee a refinance with LTV above 100 percent of reasonable value. The financed funding fee counts toward the LTV. As a practical matter, most lenders impose investor overlays capping LTV at 90 percent (or in some cases 80 percent) due to Ginnie Mae securitization rules that took effect in late 2019. Loans above 90 percent LTV are typically limited to the conforming loan amount, 30-year term, and a 641 minimum credit score under most lender programs.

**Net Tangible Benefit (NTB) requirements for cash-out.** Per Circular 26-19-5, a cash-out refinance must satisfy at least one of the following NTB tests:

1. The new loan eliminates monthly mortgage insurance;
2. The new loan term is shorter than the loan being refinanced;
3. The new loan interest rate is lower than the loan being refinanced;
4. The new monthly P&I payment is lower than the loan being refinanced;
5. The new loan results in an increase in monthly residual income;
6. The new loan is used to pay off the veteran's interim construction loan;
7. The new loan LTV is at or below 90 percent of reasonable value;
8. The new loan refinances an ARM into a fixed-rate loan.

**Seasoning requirements.** Per 38 USC § 3709(c), the seasoning rule applies when the loan being refinanced is itself a VA-guaranteed loan. The new loan note date must be the later of 210 days after the first payment due date on the existing loan and the date the sixth payment is made.

Note: Seasoning requirements apply only when refinancing an existing VA loan. When refinancing a non-VA loan into a VA cash-out (a Type I or Type II that pays off a conventional or FHA loan), the seasoning rules under 38 USC § 3709 do not apply, though investor overlays may impose their own seasoning.

**Funding fee.** Per Section 5 above: 2.15% first use / 3.3% subsequent use, with the same exemption categories as purchase loans.

**Recoupment requirement.** Per 38 USC § 3709(a), recoupment within 36 months applies to Type I cash-out refinances only. Type II cash-out refinances (where the new loan exceeds the payoff) do not have the 36-month recoupment test, but the NTB tests still apply.

**When VA cash-out makes sense vs. IRRRL vs. conventional cash-out.**

- **IRRRL** when the borrower has an existing VA loan, no need for cash, and the new rate clears the 0.5% threshold. Streamlined process, no appraisal, low funding fee.
- **VA cash-out (Type I)** when the borrower wants to convert a non-VA loan to a VA loan, or wants to refinance a VA loan with a small amount of additional financing (closing costs into the loan).
- **VA cash-out (Type II)** when the borrower wants substantial equity-out cash. Higher LTV cap than conventional cash-out (100% federal cap, 90% lender cap vs. typical conventional 80% cap). No PMI. Higher funding fee than IRRRL.
- **Conventional cash-out** when the veteran has substantial equity, low LTV, high credit, and the conventional rate beats VA pricing (rare scenario but possible).

### 9.3 VA refinance compliance notes

**Recoupment-period disclosure requirements.** Lenders must disclose the recoupment calculation in the IRRRL Worksheet (VA Form 26-8923) and in the borrower-facing Loan Comparison Disclosure required by 38 USC § 3709(b).

**Comparison statement requirements.** Per 38 USC § 3709(b) and the proposed rule under RIN 2900-AR04, the lender must provide:

- Initial Loan Comparison Disclosure on the same date as the initial Loan Estimate;
- Updated comparison disclosure with any revised Loan Estimate that materially changes the comparison or recoupment;
- Final Loan Comparison Disclosure on the date the Closing Disclosure is provided;
- Borrower's signature acknowledging receipt of the initial and final comparison disclosures.

**Predatory IRRRL solicitation language to avoid.** Synthesized from CFPB enforcement actions and the November 2017 CFPB-VA WARNO:

- Do not use VA logos, seals, or look-alike government formatting.
- Do not invent fake form numbers or "VA Benefit Notice" labels.
- Do not represent that benefits expire on a date certain.
- Do not advertise "skip a payment" or "skip 1 or 2 payments" as a refinance benefit. VA prohibits this as an advertised benefit (Circular 26-19-22).
- Do not promise specific escrow refund amounts.
- Do not advertise interest rates without specific terms (loan amount, lock period, points, credit assumption).
- Do not represent that the lender is the VA, is endorsed by the VA, or that the COE will issue, the appraisal will hit, or the borrower will qualify.

## 10. Real Borrower Questions (AEO-Targeted)

These questions are pulled from common patterns on Google "People Also Ask," r/Veterans, r/VeteransBenefits, r/MilitaryFinance, r/VALoans, r/Huntsville, military spouse forums, and BiggerPockets veteran threads. Each question is followed by the underlying intent and the precise factual answer in answer-first AEO format. Organized by topic for retrieval.

### Eligibility / COE

**Q: Who is eligible for a VA loan?**
A: Active-duty service members with at least 90 continuous days of service, veterans who meet era-specific minimum service requirements, National Guard members with 90+ days of qualifying federal active duty (or 90 days of qualifying Title 32 service under specific Code sections, or 6 creditable years), Reservists with 90 days of non-training active duty (or 6 creditable years), and certain surviving spouses, may all be eligible. Eligibility also extends to U.S. citizens who served in WWII allied forces, Public Health Service officers, NOAA commissioned officers, and U.S. service academy cadets/midshipmen. The exact rule depends on the service period, see VA's eligibility page.

**Q: How do I get a Certificate of Eligibility?**
A: Three ways. Fastest is through a VA-participating lender via WebLGY. You can also request it yourself online at VA.gov, or mail VA Form 26-1880 to the Atlanta National Eligibility Center. Most lenders can return a COE in minutes for borrowers with clean records.

**Q: Can I get a VA loan as a National Guard member?**
A: Yes, under several paths. Per the Johnny Isakson Act of 2020, you qualify if you served at least 90 days of qualifying full-time National Guard duty under 32 USC § 316, § 502, § 503, § 504, or § 505 (with the DD-214 reflecting the activation Code section). You also qualify with 90+ days of non-training Title 10 active duty, or with 6 creditable years in the National Guard either still serving or honorably discharged.

**Q: Can I get a VA loan as a Reservist?**
A: Yes. You qualify with at least 90 days of non-training active-duty service, or with 6 creditable years in the Selected Reserve either still serving or discharged honorably. The Title 32 90-day path added by the 2020 Act applies to the National Guard, not the Reserve.

**Q: Can I get a VA loan on active duty?**
A: Yes. Active-duty members generally qualify after 90 continuous days of service. Your COE will be supported by a Statement of Service from your unit commander or personnel officer instead of a DD-214.

**Q: Does an OTH discharge disqualify me?**
A: Not automatically. An Other Than Honorable discharge generally requires a VA Character-of-Discharge review, which is separate from any DoD discharge upgrade you may pursue. The 04-26-2024 Final Rule (effective 06-25-2024) materially expanded the circumstances under which VA can find the discharge "honorable for VA purposes," including a compelling-circumstances exception for mental/cognitive impairment, combat exposure, and similar factors. Talk to a VA-accredited representative or a county Veterans Service Officer.

**Q: Can a surviving spouse get a VA loan?**
A: Yes, in defined circumstances. Eligible categories include surviving spouses who are receiving DIC (Dependency and Indemnity Compensation) or eligible for it, surviving spouses of service members who died in service, surviving spouses of veterans who died from a service-connected disability, surviving spouses of veterans rated totally disabled at the time of death, and spouses of active-duty members who are MIA or POW. The COE process uses VA Form 26-1817 for unmarried surviving spouses.

**Q: Can I use my VA benefit more than once?**
A: Yes. You can reuse VA loan benefits if (a) you sold the home and paid the prior loan in full, (b) a qualified veteran-transferee assumed the loan and substituted their entitlement, or (c) you paid off the prior loan in full but kept the property, the once-per-lifetime "restoration without sale" option. Even if you don't restore, you may have remaining bonus entitlement that lets you take a second VA loan.

### Entitlement / Loan Limits

**Q: Is there a maximum VA loan amount?**
A: Not for borrowers with full entitlement. Since 01-01-2020 (Blue Water Navy Vietnam Veterans Act), VA imposes no loan limit on borrowers with full entitlement. The lender will still cap the loan based on your income, credit, residual income, and the property appraisal, but there's no federal VA-imposed cap. Borrowers with partial entitlement face a calculation based on the county conforming loan limit and previously used entitlement.

**Q: What is full entitlement?**
A: Full entitlement means you have $36,000 in basic entitlement available and zero used entitlement. You have full entitlement if you have never used your VA loan benefit, or if you've previously used it and have since had it fully restored (loan paid in full and home sold, the once-per-lifetime restoration without sale exercised, or substitution by a transferee). With full entitlement, you have no VA-imposed loan limit.

**Q: How do I get a second VA loan?**
A: Two paths. If you sold the prior home and paid the loan in full, your entitlement is restored and you can simply take a new VA loan. If you still own the prior home (or it has not been paid off), you may have remaining bonus entitlement to take a second VA loan, typically in a PCS retain-as-rental scenario. Calculate remaining bonus entitlement as 25% of the county loan limit minus previously used entitlement.

**Q: Can I keep my old house and use my VA benefit to buy a new one?**
A: Often yes, using bonus (second-tier) entitlement. The most common scenario is a PCS where you retain the old home as a rental and use bonus entitlement to buy at the new duty station. You need enough remaining entitlement to provide VA's 25% guaranty on the new loan; if not, a down payment fills the gap.

**Q: What's bonus entitlement?**
A: Bonus entitlement (also called second-tier or Tier 2 entitlement) is the additional guaranty VA provides beyond the basic $36,000 for loans over $144,000. It's not displayed on your COE; it's calculated as 25% of the applicable conforming loan limit, minus any previously used entitlement that has not been restored.

### Funding Fee

**Q: How much is the VA funding fee?**
A: For purchase loans, the funding fee is 2.15% of the loan amount on first use (with less than 5% down), or 3.3% on subsequent use. With 5% to less than 10% down, it's 1.5% regardless of first or subsequent use. With 10% or more down, it's 1.25%. IRRRL is 0.5%; cash-out refinance is 2.15% first use / 3.3% subsequent use; loan assumption is 0.5%. Current rates are effective for loans closed on or after 04-07-2023 per Circular 26-23-6.

**Q: Do I have to pay the funding fee in cash?**
A: No. You have three options: finance it into the loan (most common), pay it in cash at closing, or have the seller pay it as part of the 4% seller concession allowance.

**Q: Am I exempt from the funding fee?**
A: You're exempt if you receive VA compensation for a service-connected disability, are eligible to receive disability compensation but receive retirement or active-duty pay instead, receive DIC as a surviving spouse, are a service member with a proposed or memorandum disability rating before closing, or are an active-duty Purple Heart recipient. Exemption status typically appears on the COE.

**Q: Can I get a funding fee refund?**
A: Yes, in some cases. If you paid the funding fee at closing and are later determined exempt with an effective date that predates the loan closing, you can request a refund. Process the request through the St. Petersburg VA Regional Loan Center (which serves Alabama).

### Appraisal / MPRs

**Q: What does a VA appraiser look for?**
A: Two things. First, market value supported by comparable sales. Second, Minimum Property Requirements: safe and sanitary water and sewer; roof in good condition; functional permanent heating; safe electrical; no peeling lead-based paint on pre-1978 homes; no active termite infestation or visible WDO damage; safe access; functional smoke detectors; handrails on stairs over three risers; safe crawl space ventilation. The full MPR list is in VA Pamphlet 26-7, Chapter 12.

**Q: Will the seller fix things for a VA appraisal?**
A: Often yes. MPR repairs are typically minor (paint, smoke detectors, handrails, GFCI outlets, well water tests, termite treatment). Most sellers complete required repairs before closing. The contract usually addresses the seller's obligation to complete VA-required repairs. Escrow holdbacks for post-closing completion are sometimes used for weather-dependent repairs.

**Q: What's the difference between a VA appraisal and a home inspection?**
A: A VA appraisal establishes value and confirms the home meets the MPRs (a habitability standard). A home inspection is a much more thorough condition evaluation done by a licensed home inspector, checking systems, appliances, structural integrity in detail, and identifying issues that may not affect MPRs but matter to the buyer. The appraisal is required for the loan; the home inspection is optional but strongly recommended.

**Q: Why did my VA appraisal come in low?**
A: Common reasons include using comparable sales that weren't truly comparable (different size, condition, age), not having recent enough closed sales in the immediate market, missing renovations or value-adding features that weren't visible from the outside, a rapidly changing market where comps lag behind current pricing, and occasional appraiser error on square footage or property boundaries. The Tidewater Initiative gives you 48 hours to submit additional comparables before the appraisal is final.

**Q: What can I do about a low VA appraisal?**
A: Four options. First, the Tidewater Initiative, submit additional comparable sales within the 48-hour window before the appraisal is final. Second, after the Notice of Value is issued, you (the buyer) can submit a formal Reconsideration of Value request through your lender. Third, renegotiate the contract price down to the appraised value. Fourth, exercise the federal VA escape clause and walk away with your earnest money refunded.

### Closing Costs

**Q: What closing costs do VA buyers pay?**
A: Typical VA-buyer-paid closing costs include: VA appraisal fee, credit report, title insurance (lender's and owner's), recording fees, survey, hazard insurance, flood determination, WDO/termite inspection (Alabama), and lender origination capped at 1% of the loan amount. The funding fee is separate and can be financed. Discount points are separate. VA prohibits the borrower from paying certain lender-overhead fees if a 1% origination fee is charged.

**Q: Can the seller pay my closing costs?**
A: Yes. Sellers can pay all customary buyer closing costs without limit. Sellers can additionally provide up to 4% in concessions (over and above customary closing costs) for things like funding fee, prepaid taxes/insurance, debt payoff, and temporary buydowns. The 4% cap is on concessions, not on customary closing costs.

**Q: How much earnest money do I need?**
A: There's no federal VA rule. In north Alabama VA transactions, $500 to $5,000 is typical, with the upper end common in Madison County purchases above $400,000. The federal VA escape clause protects your earnest money if the appraisal comes in below the contract price, so higher earnest money signals seriousness without exposing you to uncapped risk on appraisal.

**Q: Do VA loans have PMI?**
A: No. VA loans do not require Private Mortgage Insurance. The VA funding fee replaces the PMI cost structure and is a one-time fee rather than a monthly premium.

### Offer Strategy

**Q: Are VA offers harder to get accepted?**
A: Not categorically. The "VA offers always lose" myth has roots in pre-2020 loan limit rules that no longer exist and older MPR interpretations that have been clarified. VA offers structured competitively (price, contingency timing, earnest money) are routinely accepted in current markets. Listing-agent objections to VA offers are usually based on outdated information.

**Q: Can I waive the appraisal contingency on a VA loan?**
A: You can waive a contractual appraisal contingency, but you cannot waive the federal VA escape clause, it's required by 38 CFR § 36.4303(k)(4) and operates regardless of the contract's contingency language. So even if you waive the contractual contingency, you retain the federal protection of getting your earnest money back if the appraisal comes in below the contract price.

**Q: Do VA loans take longer to close?**
A: No, not in 2025-2026. Current VA closings in north Alabama track 30 to 45 days, comparable to conventional and FHA. Pre-underwritten files can close in 21 to 30 days.

### Refinance

**Q: What is an IRRRL?**
A: Interest Rate Reduction Refinance Loan, sometimes called a VA Streamline. It's VA's program for refinancing an existing VA-guaranteed loan into a new VA-guaranteed loan with a lower rate or better terms. Streamlined processing typically means no new appraisal, no new credit underwriting, and no new COE. The funding fee is 0.5% and the loan must clear specific net-tangible-benefit, recoupment, and seasoning tests.

**Q: How soon can I refinance my VA loan?**
A: At least 210 days after the first payment due date AND after the sixth monthly payment is made, whichever comes later. This seasoning rule comes from the Economic Growth, Regulatory Relief, and Consumer Protection Act of 2018 (Pub. L. 115-174) and applies to both IRRRLs and VA cash-out refinances of existing VA loans.

**Q: Can I take cash out with a VA refinance?**
A: Yes, through a VA Cash-Out refinance. VA's federal rule allows up to 100% LTV (down from earlier rules permitting higher LTV), and most lenders cap cash-out LTV at 90% under investor overlays. The loan must satisfy a Net Tangible Benefit test and the same seasoning rules as IRRRLs when refinancing an existing VA loan. The funding fee is 2.15% first use / 3.3% subsequent use.

## 11. Common Misconceptions and Mistakes

This section documents the misunderstandings borrowers and listing agents most often bring to VA loans, with the corrected fact next to each.

### Eligibility misconceptions

- **"VA loans are only for first-time buyers."** False. VA loans can be used multiple times and are explicitly designed for reuse via restoration of entitlement.
- **"I had a VA loan once, so I can never get another one."** False. Entitlement is restorable when the prior loan is paid off; bonus entitlement may also allow a second simultaneous VA loan in PCS scenarios.
- **"Reservists can't get VA loans."** False. Reservists qualify with 90 days of non-training active duty or 6 creditable years in the Selected Reserve.
- **"OTH means I'm out forever."** False. OTH requires a VA Character-of-Discharge review, and the 06-25-2024 rule expanded the circumstances under which VA can find an OTH service "honorable for VA purposes."
- **"VA eligibility expires after I leave the military."** False. VA loan eligibility does not expire.

### Loan limit misconceptions

- **"There's a $144,000 VA loan limit."** False. The $144,000 figure is the threshold above which bonus entitlement applies; it's not a loan limit. Full-entitlement borrowers have no VA loan limit since 01-01-2020.
- **"VA loans cap out at the conforming loan limit."** False for full-entitlement borrowers. Full-entitlement borrowers can take VA loans above the CLL with no down payment, subject to lender approval based on income and credit.
- **"I can't get a jumbo VA loan."** False. Jumbo VA loans (above the CLL) are available for full-entitlement borrowers with no required down payment, though many lenders apply overlays.

### Funding fee misconceptions

- **"The funding fee is PMI."** False. The funding fee is a one-time fee, not a monthly premium, and it's paid to VA, not to a private mortgage insurer.
- **"All VA loans have the same funding fee."** False. The fee varies by loan type (purchase, IRRRL, cash-out, assumption), down payment, and first or subsequent use.
- **"I have to pay the funding fee in cash."** False. The fee can be financed, paid in cash, or paid by the seller as part of the 4% concession allowance.
- **"If I'm getting a disability rating, I should wait to close."** Partially false. If you have a proposed or memorandum rating before closing, you can be exempt; if the rating is finalized after closing with a retroactive effective date predating closing, you may be eligible for a refund.

### Appraisal / MPR misconceptions

- **"VA appraisals are always low."** False. VA appraisers use the same comparable-sales methodology as conventional appraisers and are independently assigned by VA, which is structurally protective of valuation accuracy.
- **"VA will require everything to be perfect."** False. MPRs are a habitability standard, not a perfection standard.
- **"Sellers won't accept VA offers because of repairs."** Generally false. Most MPR repairs are minor, and most sellers complete them. The myth often reflects listing agents' outdated views.
- **"VA appraisers fail homes for cosmetic issues."** False. Cosmetic issues are not MPR triggers. MPRs are about safety, structural soundness, and sanitation.

### Closing cost misconceptions

- **"VA loans have no closing costs."** False. VA buyers still pay typical closing costs (appraisal, title, recording, etc.). VA limits which lender-overhead fees the borrower can pay; it does not eliminate closing costs.
- **"VA loans cost less out of pocket than any other loan."** Sometimes true, sometimes not. Depends on funding fee status, lender pricing, and seller concessions. Run the math case by case.
- **"I can't pay any closing costs as a VA buyer."** False. VA borrowers can pay all customary closing costs. The 1% origination cap limits lender-overhead charges, not third-party closing costs.

### Offer strategy misconceptions

- **"Sellers will always pick conventional over VA."** Often false. Sellers care about offer strength (price, contingency timing, earnest money, financing certainty), not loan type per se. Strong VA offers compete with strong conventional offers.
- **"I have to waive contingencies to compete."** Not necessarily. The federal VA escape clause is non-waivable and protects earnest money on appraisal shortfalls regardless of the contractual contingency.
- **"Cash buyers always beat VA buyers."** Often, but not always. Sellers also weigh closing date certainty and contract terms.

### Refinance misconceptions

- **"I can refinance my VA loan immediately."** False. Both IRRRL and VA cash-out refinances of existing VA loans require 210-day seasoning and six payments (whichever is later) per 38 USC § 3709.
- **"All IRRRL solicitations in the mail are legitimate."** False. Many predatory mailers mimic VA branding. CFPB has taken enforcement action against multiple lenders for deceptive IRRRL marketing. Verify any solicitation before responding.
- **"Cash-out refi is the same under VA as conventional."** False. VA allows higher LTV (up to 100% federally, typically 90% under lender overlays) than conventional cash-out (typically 80%) and does not require PMI on the cash-out. Funding fee, NTB tests, and seasoning rules apply.

## 12. Do / Don't Guidance

Practical, attorney-style do/don't list for veterans approaching the VA loan process.

**Do.**

- Do request your COE early, through your lender via WebLGY is fastest.
- Do gather DD-214 (Member 4 copy preferred), NGB-22 with retirement points statements, or current Statement of Service before the lender conversation.
- Do review your COE carefully for entitlement, prior loan use, and exemption status.
- Do verify funding fee exemption status if you have a service-connected disability rating or a pending claim before closing.
- Do pair a buyer's agent who has actually closed VA transactions with a lender experienced in VA underwriting.
- Do read the VA Notice of Value carefully and understand the Reconsideration of Value process before accepting a low value.
- Do confirm the VA escape clause is in your contract before going under contract.
- Do verify any IRRRL solicitation by calling the lender directly and asking for a written Loan Estimate before responding.

**Don't.**

- Don't pay any third-party "VA loan eligibility specialist", COE retrieval is free through VA or your lender.
- Don't assume an OTH discharge is automatic disqualification without character-of-discharge review under the updated 06-25-2024 standards.
- Don't waive the appraisal contingency without understanding how the federal escape clause operates separately.
- Don't respond to unsolicited IRRRL refinance mailers without verifying the lender, the recoupment math, and the rate reduction.
- Don't conflate VA disability rating with VA loan eligibility, they are separate determinations that intersect only on the funding fee exemption.
- Don't take a listing agent's word that VA "won't work" on a property without confirming with your lender or a VA appraiser.
- Don't finance buyer-broker compensation into the loan, under Circular 26-24-14 it must be paid from liquid assets.
- Don't use the IRRRL to pay off a non-VA loan, that's a cash-out refinance, with different rules.

## 13. Compliance and Phrasing Caveats

This section codifies language requirements for VA-specific YMYL content built from this research.

### 13.1 Terminology requiring care

**"VA-approved" lender.** VA does not approve lenders in the sense the phrase suggests to consumers. VA participates in the loan guaranty process with private lenders that meet certain qualifications and obtain Lender Identification Numbers from VA. The accurate phrasing is "VA-participating lender" or "lender authorized to make VA-guaranteed loans." Avoid "VA-approved" without context, and never use it in a way that suggests VA endorses, vets, or guarantees the lender's quality.

**"VA-approved appraiser."** Acceptable, because appraisers on the VA fee panel are individually approved by VA. Distinguish from "VA-approved lender."

**"VA loan."** Common shorthand. The accurate term is "VA-guaranteed loan" or "VA-backed loan." Either is fine in consumer copy.

**"VA officer," "VA representative," or "VA specialist" in lender marketing.** Generally false and a source of CFPB enforcement risk. A loan officer is not a VA representative. Avoid this phrasing.

### 13.2 Statements that should never appear as guarantees

These statements imply outcomes that VA controls, not the lender, and should never appear without significant hedging or in the form they suggest:

- "You will qualify."
- "Your COE will arrive in [X] days."
- "Your appraisal will come in at value."
- "You will be approved."
- "You are guaranteed [rate / payment / terms]."
- "Your funding fee will be waived." (without qualification of pending rating verification)
- "You will receive [specific dollar amount] back from escrow."

Replace with conditional language: "may qualify," "can typically expect," "subject to VA verification," "depending on VA's determination of," "if VA confirms exemption status."

### 13.3 "No closing costs" and "no money out of pocket"

This is one of the most-flagged misleading-advertising patterns in VA mortgage marketing. CFPB enforcement actions have repeatedly cited lenders for advertising "no closing costs" or "no money out of pocket" without disclosing that:

- The funding fee is being financed into the loan balance;
- Closing costs are being absorbed into a higher interest rate;
- Specific TILA Regulation Z disclosures are required when advertising rates;
- The Mortgage Acts and Practices Advertising Rule (MAP Rule, 12 CFR § 1014) prohibits material misrepresentations.

Replacement language: "May reduce out-of-pocket cash at closing, actual costs depend on lender pricing, financing decisions, and your specific transaction. Discuss the trade-offs with a licensed loan officer."

### 13.4 Hedging language patterns

Where federal VA rule allows variation by lender, by VA case-by-case determination, or by transaction:

- "may"
- "can" / "could"
- "depending on entitlement use"
- "depending on lender overlays"
- "subject to VA verification"
- "in most cases"
- "typically"
- "generally"

Where federal VA rule is fixed and verifiable:

- "must"
- "is" / "are"
- "the federal rule is"
- "under 38 USC § X" or "under 38 CFR § Y"

### 13.5 VA's specific advertising guidance and required disclosures

VA-specific lender advertising is governed by:

- 38 USC § 3709 (refinance comparison disclosures);
- 38 CFR § 36.4307 (and subsequent rulemaking);
- 38 CFR § 36.4319 (servicing disclosures);
- VA Pamphlet 26-7 Chapter 8 (advertised fees);
- VA Circular 26-19-22 (IRRRL solicitation guidance, prohibition on advertising "skip a payment");
- The Federal Trade Commission MAP Rule (12 CFR § 1014);
- TILA / Regulation Z (12 CFR § 1026, particularly the advertising provisions in § 1026.24 for closed-end credit);
- NMLS and state mortgage license disclosure requirements;
- Equal Housing Lender requirements under the Fair Housing Act and ECOA.

### 13.6 IRRRL solicitation language patterns that have drawn enforcement

Per the 2020 CFPB sweep of consent orders (Section 1.9 above), avoid:

- VA logos, seals, or look-alike government formatting in mailers;
- Fake form numbers ("VA-1211 Benefit Allotment Notice," "Form 21-0760 Eligibility Notification," "Understanding your VA Benefit Statement");
- Statements that benefits will expire on a date certain;
- Claims that the consumer can "skip" 1 or 2 payments (VA prohibits this as an advertised benefit per Circular 26-19-22);
- Specific escrow refund amount promises;
- Hypothetical loan term comparisons that the lender is not actually prepared to offer;
- Misrepresentations of APR, payment, or rate without compliant TILA disclosures;
- Implications of VA endorsement or affiliation.

### 13.7 Required state-level disclosures (Alabama)

Any reader-facing VA loan content from Alabama lenders should include:

- NMLS license number for the loan officer and lender;
- Alabama state mortgage license disclosure (Alabama Department of Banking, license number);
- Equal Housing Lender logo and statement;
- Standard "subject to credit approval" disclosure;
- ECOA disclosure where rates or terms are quoted.

### 13.8 Routing rules for borrower questions outside the LO's lane

These are bright-line routing rules. The mortgage loan officer's lane is the loan; the VA's lane is benefits.

- **Eligibility / COE issues:** Route to the lender's WebLGY workflow, or the borrower can contact the Atlanta National Eligibility Center directly at (888) 244-6711 or P.O. Box 100034, Decatur, GA 30031.
- **Disability rating questions:** Route to the St. Petersburg VA Regional Loan Center at 1-877-827-3702 or VA.gov. The St. Petersburg RLC handles VA Loan Guaranty for Alabama; disability ratings and benefits adjudication are a separate VA function.
- **Discharge upgrade questions:** Route to a VA-accredited representative, an accredited Veterans Service Organization (VSO), the appropriate DoD Discharge Review Board, or a Board for Correction of Military Records. The VA discharge upgrade portal: https://www.va.gov/discharge-upgrade-instructions/introduction/.
- **Character-of-discharge review questions:** Same routing, VA-accredited representative, county Veterans Service Officer, accredited VSO.
- **DIC and survivor benefits questions:** Route to the St. Petersburg RLC for loan-eligibility intersection, but to the VA Pension and Fiduciary Service or the local VA regional office for benefits administration.
- **Foreclosure / loss mitigation questions on existing VA loans:** Route to the borrower's loan servicer first; if unresolved, the VA Loan Technician hotline at (877) 827-3702.
- **Property-related questions about non-loan-mechanics topics (zoning, taxes, easements, HOA disputes):** Route to local government, an Alabama real estate attorney, or the title insurer.

### 13.9 The line between mortgage advice and benefits advice

The loan officer's lane:

- Loan products (purchase, IRRRL, cash-out);
- Loan structures (down payment, term, ARM vs. fixed);
- Loan pricing (rate, points, lender fees);
- Closing cost negotiation strategy;
- Loan-specific federal rules (NTB, recoupment, seasoning);
- Funding fee mechanics (financing, exemption documentation routing).

NOT the loan officer's lane:

- Whether the borrower is eligible for a VA disability rating;
- Whether the borrower's discharge is "honorable for VA purposes";
- Whether the borrower's surviving-spouse status is established;
- Whether DIC will be approved;
- The merits of any VA benefits claim;
- Specific DoD discharge upgrade strategy.

A reasonable loan officer can describe the _mechanics_ of these processes, what forms are used, where to route, without crossing into providing benefits advice.

## 14. Suggested FAQ Q&A (FAQPage Schema-Ready)

These are written in answer-first AEO format, ready to drop into FAQPage schema markup. Organized by topic. Each answer is intended to be 2 to 4 sentences, with the first sentence directly answering the question for AI Overview / AEO retrieval.

### Eligibility FAQs

**Q: Who qualifies for a VA loan?**
A: Active-duty service members with at least 90 continuous days of service, veterans meeting era-specific minimum service requirements, qualifying National Guard and Reserve members, and certain surviving spouses qualify for VA loans. Eligibility is documented through a Certificate of Eligibility (COE), which lenders can typically request through VA's WebLGY system. The exact service requirement varies by service period and component. See VA's eligibility page at va.gov for the specific rules that apply to your service.

**Q: How do I get a VA Certificate of Eligibility?**
A: The fastest way is to ask your VA-participating lender to request your COE through VA's WebLGY system, which typically returns a determination in minutes for borrowers with clean records. You can also request it yourself online at VA.gov, or mail VA Form 26-1880 to the Atlanta National Eligibility Center. There is no fee to obtain a COE, never pay a third party for COE retrieval.

**Q: Can a National Guard member get a VA loan?**
A: Yes. Under the Johnny Isakson Act of 2020, National Guard members qualify with 90+ days of qualifying full-time National Guard duty under specific Title 32 Code sections (316, 502, 503, 504, or 505). They also qualify with 90+ days of non-training Title 10 active duty, or with 6 creditable years of National Guard service if continuing to serve or honorably discharged. The Title 32 90-day path was added in 2021 and significantly expanded Guard eligibility.

**Q: Can a Reservist get a VA loan?**
A: Yes. Reservists qualify with at least 90 days of non-training active-duty service or with 6 creditable years in the Selected Reserve while continuing to serve or after an honorable discharge. The Title 32 path that applies to the National Guard does not apply to the Reserve. Documentation typically includes retirement points statements and any active-duty orders.

**Q: Can I get a VA loan if I have an OTH discharge?**
A: Possibly, but it requires a VA Character-of-Discharge review. The 04-26-2024 Final Rule (effective 06-25-2024) materially expanded the circumstances under which VA can find OTH service "honorable for VA purposes," including a compelling-circumstances exception covering mental health, traumatic brain injury, military sexual trauma, and combat exposure. Speak with a VA-accredited representative or county Veterans Service Officer about your specific circumstances.

**Q: Can a surviving spouse use a VA loan?**
A: Yes, in defined circumstances. Eligible categories include surviving spouses receiving DIC, surviving spouses of veterans who died from a service-connected disability or in service, surviving spouses of veterans rated totally disabled at the time of death, and spouses of MIA or POW active-duty service members. The application process generally uses VA Form 26-1817 for unmarried surviving spouses.

**Q: Can I use a VA loan more than once?**
A: Yes. VA loan benefits can be reused as long as the prior entitlement is restored, most commonly by selling the home and paying off the prior VA loan. Once-per-lifetime, you can also restore entitlement after paying off the loan but keeping the property. Bonus (second-tier) entitlement can also allow a second VA loan in certain scenarios, such as a PCS where you retain the prior home as a rental.

### Entitlement / Loan Limit FAQs

**Q: Is there a maximum VA loan amount?**
A: For borrowers with full entitlement, no, VA imposes no loan limit since 01-01-2020 under the Blue Water Navy Vietnam Veterans Act. Your lender will still cap the loan based on your income, credit, residual income, and the property appraisal, but there is no federal VA cap. Borrowers with partial entitlement face a calculation tied to the county conforming loan limit.

**Q: What does "full entitlement" mean?**
A: Full entitlement means you have $36,000 in basic entitlement available and zero used entitlement. You have full entitlement if you have never used your VA loan benefit, or if you have previously used it and have since had it fully restored. With full entitlement, you can take a VA-guaranteed loan at any amount your lender approves with no down payment.

**Q: What is the conforming loan limit and does it apply to VA loans?**
A: The conforming loan limit (CLL) is the FHFA-set ceiling on the loan amount Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac will purchase. The 2026 baseline CLL is $832,750. The CLL does not directly limit VA loans for full-entitlement borrowers, but it is used to calculate remaining bonus entitlement for partial-entitlement borrowers. Lenders may also apply CLL-based pricing tiers for VA loans above the limit.

**Q: How is VA bonus entitlement calculated?**
A: Bonus entitlement equals 25% of the applicable conforming loan limit minus any previously used entitlement that has not been restored. For a borrower in a county with the 2026 baseline CLL of $832,750 who has $50,000 of used entitlement, the calculation is: ($832,750 × 0.25) − $50,000 = $158,187 in remaining bonus entitlement. Multiply that by 4 to estimate the maximum no-down-payment loan amount.

### Funding Fee FAQs

**Q: What is the VA funding fee?**
A: The VA funding fee is a one-time fee paid on most VA-guaranteed loans that helps fund the VA Loan Guaranty Program. The fee replaces what would otherwise be a taxpayer-funded program. Most borrowers either finance the fee into the loan or have the seller pay it as part of seller concessions. Many borrowers, including those with service-connected disabilities, are exempt.

**Q: How much is the VA funding fee for a purchase loan in 2026?**
A: For first-time use of the VA loan benefit with less than 5% down, the funding fee is 2.15% of the loan amount. With 5% to less than 10% down, it is 1.5%. With 10% or more down, it is 1.25%. Subsequent use with less than 5% down is 3.3%. These rates are effective for loans closed on or after 04-07-2023 per Circular 26-23-6 and remain in effect through 10-01-2030 per the Johnny Isakson Act.

**Q: What is the VA funding fee for an IRRRL?**
A: 0.5% of the new loan amount, regardless of first or subsequent use of the benefit. Funding fee exemptions still apply (service-connected disability, DIC-receiving surviving spouse, and others). The IRRRL funding fee is the lowest of any VA loan type.

**Q: Am I exempt from the VA funding fee?**
A: You are exempt if you receive VA compensation for a service-connected disability, are eligible to receive disability compensation but receive retirement or active-duty pay instead, receive DIC as a surviving spouse, are an active-duty member with a proposed or memorandum disability rating before closing, or are an active-duty Purple Heart recipient. Exemption status typically appears on your COE.

**Q: Can the seller pay my VA funding fee?**
A: Yes. The seller can pay the funding fee as part of seller concessions, which are limited to 4% of the home's reasonable value. The funding fee paid by the seller counts toward the 4% concession cap, alongside other concessions like prepaid taxes, hazard insurance, and debt payoff.

**Q: Can I get a refund of the VA funding fee?**
A: You may be eligible for a refund if you paid the funding fee at closing and were later determined exempt, typically when a service-connected disability rating is awarded with an effective date that predates the loan closing. Refund requests are processed through the VA Regional Loan Center with jurisdiction over your state, which is the St. Petersburg RLC for Alabama borrowers.

### Appraisal / MPR FAQs

**Q: What does a VA appraiser look for?**
A: A VA appraiser does two things: establishes the home's reasonable market value using comparable sales, and verifies that the property meets VA's Minimum Property Requirements (safety, structural soundness, and sanitation). MPRs include working utilities, a roof in serviceable condition, no peeling lead-based paint on pre-1978 homes, no active termite infestation, safe access, and other specific items detailed in VA Pamphlet 26-7 Chapter 12.

**Q: What is the difference between a VA appraisal and a home inspection?**
A: A VA appraisal is required for the loan and confirms value plus minimum habitability standards. A home inspection is a much more thorough condition evaluation done by a licensed home inspector, checking systems, appliances, structural integrity, and items that may not affect MPR but matter to the buyer's decision. The VA appraisal does not replace a home inspection, and a home inspection is strongly recommended on any VA purchase.

**Q: What is the VA Tidewater Initiative?**
A: The Tidewater Initiative is a VA appraisal procedure that activates when an appraiser concludes the appraisal will likely come in below the contract price. The appraiser must notify the lender (or buyer's agent) before issuing the Notice of Value, allowing 48 hours for additional comparable sales data to be submitted. It is named for the Tidewater region of Virginia where it was first piloted and is now national VA policy under VA Circular 26-17-18.

**Q: What can I do if my VA appraisal comes in low?**
A: You have four options. First, the Tidewater Initiative gives 48 hours to submit additional comparables before the appraisal is final. Second, after the Notice of Value is issued, you can submit a formal Reconsideration of Value through your lender. Third, renegotiate the contract price down to the appraised value. Fourth, exercise the federal VA escape clause and walk away with a full refund of your earnest money.

**Q: What is the VA escape clause?**
A: The VA escape clause is a federally required provision in every VA purchase contract under 38 CFR § 36.4303(k)(4). It states that the buyer cannot be penalized, including by forfeiting earnest money, if the contract price exceeds the VA's reasonable value of the property. The buyer can walk away with full earnest money returned, or proceed at the contract price by bringing the appraisal-gap difference in cash.

### Closing Cost FAQs

**Q: What closing costs do VA buyers pay?**
A: VA buyers typically pay the VA appraisal fee, credit report, title insurance, recording fees, survey, hazard insurance prepayment, and lender origination capped at 1% of the loan amount. The funding fee is separate and can be financed. Discount points are separate and optional. VA prohibits the borrower from paying certain lender-overhead fees (like processing or underwriting) when the lender charges the 1% origination.

**Q: Can the seller pay my VA loan closing costs?**
A: Yes. Sellers can pay all customary buyer closing costs without limit. Sellers can additionally provide up to 4% in seller concessions for the funding fee, prepaid taxes and insurance, debt payoff, and temporary rate buydowns. The 4% cap applies to concessions, not to customary closing costs.

**Q: Is there a 4% seller concession cap on VA loans?**
A: Yes, but only for "concessions," which are defined as anything of value added to the transaction at no additional cost to the buyer beyond customary closing costs. The 4% cap applies to items like seller-paid funding fee, prepaid taxes and insurance, debt payoff, and temporary buydowns. Customary buyer closing costs paid by the seller, like origination fee, title insurance, and recording fees, are not capped.

**Q: Do VA loans have PMI?**
A: No. VA loans do not require Private Mortgage Insurance. The VA funding fee replaces the PMI cost structure, and unlike PMI, the funding fee is a one-time fee rather than a monthly premium.

### Offer Strategy FAQs

**Q: Are VA offers harder to get accepted in competitive markets?**
A: Not categorically, but the perception persists. The "VA loans don't get accepted" myth has roots in pre-2020 loan limits that no longer exist and in older MPR interpretations that have been clarified. VA offers structured competitively on price, contingency timing, and earnest money are accepted at rates comparable to conventional offers in current markets.

**Q: How long does a VA loan take to close?**
A: VA closings in 2025-2026 typically take 30 to 45 days from contract to close, comparable to conventional and FHA timelines. Pre-underwritten VA files can close in 21 to 30 days. The streamlined VA appraisal process and modern lender workflows have largely eliminated the historical "VA takes longer" perception.

**Q: Can I waive the appraisal contingency on a VA loan?**
A: You can waive a contractual appraisal contingency, but you cannot waive the federal VA escape clause under 38 CFR § 36.4303(k)(4), it is required in every VA purchase contract. Even if you waive the contractual contingency to make your offer more competitive, you retain the federal protection of recovering your earnest money if the appraisal comes in below the contract price.

### Refinance FAQs

**Q: What is a VA IRRRL?**
A: An IRRRL (Interest Rate Reduction Refinance Loan) is VA's streamlined refinance program for replacing an existing VA-guaranteed loan with a new VA-guaranteed loan at a lower rate or better terms. IRRRLs typically don't require a new appraisal, new credit underwriting, or a new Certificate of Eligibility. The funding fee is 0.5%, and the loan must satisfy specific net-tangible-benefit, recoupment, and seasoning requirements.

**Q: How soon can I refinance my VA loan?**
A: At least 210 days after the first payment due date AND after the sixth monthly payment is made, whichever comes later. This seasoning rule comes from the Economic Growth, Regulatory Relief, and Consumer Protection Act of 2018 (codified at 38 USC § 3709) and applies to both IRRRL and VA cash-out refinances of existing VA loans.

**Q: What is the 36-month recoupment rule?**
A: For an IRRRL or Type I cash-out refinance, the lender must certify that all closing costs and fees (excluding taxes, escrow, and the funding fee) are recouped through reduced monthly principal and interest payments within 36 months of the new note date. The math: total costs ÷ monthly P&I savings must be 36 or less. If the recoupment period exceeds 36 months, VA will not guarantee the loan.

**Q: Can I refinance my non-VA loan into a VA loan?**
A: Yes, through a VA Cash-Out refinance (Type I or Type II). An IRRRL cannot refinance a non-VA loan, that requires the cash-out program. The seasoning rules under 38 USC § 3709 apply only when the loan being refinanced is itself a VA loan; refinancing a conventional or FHA loan into a VA loan does not have the 210-day seasoning requirement at the federal level (though investor overlays may impose seasoning).

**Q: How much equity can I take out with a VA cash-out refinance?**
A: VA's federal rule permits cash-out up to 100% of the home's reasonable value, but most lenders cap the LTV at 90% under post-2019 Ginnie Mae securitization overlays. The funded funding fee counts toward the LTV calculation. Loans above 90% LTV are typically limited to conforming loan amounts and may have additional credit overlays.

**Q: How do I avoid predatory IRRRL refinance offers?**
A: Verify any IRRRL solicitation by independently looking up the lender's NMLS license number and calling them directly to request a written Loan Estimate. Be suspicious of mailers using VA seals or formatting that suggests government endorsement, claims that benefits will expire, "skip a payment" promises, or specific escrow refund amounts. The CFPB and VA have sanctioned multiple lenders for these patterns. Report concerning solicitations to the CFPB at consumerfinance.gov/complaint or the VA Office of Inspector General at 800-488-8244.

## 16. Risks, Caveats, and Overclaiming Language to Avoid

This is the flagged list of statements that should never appear in reader-facing copy without significant hedging, and statements that should never appear at all.

### 16.1 Statements that should never appear without hedging

**Eligibility:**

- "You qualify for a VA loan." → "You may qualify; eligibility is determined by VA based on your service record."
- "Your COE will issue in [X] business days." → "COE timing varies; many borrowers receive their COE in minutes through WebLGY, while others take longer if records require manual review."
- "Your discharge will be reviewed favorably." → "VA reviews each Character-of-Discharge case on the specific facts; the 06-25-2024 rule expanded the circumstances under which OTH service can be found honorable for VA purposes."

**Entitlement:**

- "There's no maximum VA loan amount." → "Borrowers with full entitlement face no VA-imposed loan limit; lenders still cap the loan based on income, credit, residual income, and the appraisal."
- "You can borrow up to [X]." → Use only for borrowers whose entitlement and lender capacity have been verified.

**Funding fee:**

- "Your funding fee is waived." → "You may be exempt from the funding fee based on your COE and any pending disability rating; verify with your lender."
- "You'll get a [X] funding fee refund." → "You may be eligible for a refund if VA later issues a disability rating with an effective date predating your loan closing; the determination is made by VA, not the lender."

**Appraisal:**

- "Your appraisal will support the contract price." → "VA appraisals come in at, above, or below contract price. The Tidewater Initiative and Reconsideration of Value process give you opportunities to support a higher value, and the federal escape clause protects your earnest money if value is short."
- "VA appraisals never come in low." → "VA appraisals come in below contract price in some transactions, just like any other loan type; the federal escape clause protects you in that scenario."

**Refinance:**

- "You can save [$X] per month with a VA refinance." → "Refinancing may reduce your monthly payment depending on your current rate, the new rate, and closing costs; we'll provide a written Loan Estimate showing actual numbers."
- "You can refinance immediately." → "Refinancing an existing VA loan requires 210 days of seasoning and six monthly payments per 38 USC § 3709."

**Closing costs:**

- "VA loans have no closing costs." → Never use as written. Replace with "VA buyers may have lower out-of-pocket costs at closing depending on financing decisions, lender pricing, and seller concessions."
- "No money out of pocket." → Never use as written. CFPB has cited this language repeatedly. Replace with "may reduce or eliminate cash-to-close depending on transaction specifics."

### 16.2 Statements that should never appear at all

- "VA-approved" without context (implies VA endorses the lender);
- "VA representative" or "VA officer" describing a loan officer;
- Use of VA seals, logos, or government-look-alike formatting in marketing materials;
- "Skip a payment" or "skip 1 or 2 payments" as a refinance benefit;
- Specific escrow refund amount promises;
- "Your benefits expire on [date]" or any expiration claim regarding VA loan benefits (they do not expire);
- "Cash back to you at closing" beyond legitimate refunds of buyer-paid items;
- Any claim that the lender or its agents are affiliated with, endorsed by, or acting on behalf of the VA;
- Hypothetical loan terms (rates, payments) that the lender is not actually prepared to offer;
- Fake form numbers ("VA Form 1211," "Form 21-0760," etc.);
- "Most veterans qualify for X" without disclosing the qualification criteria.

### 16.3 Statements that require source citation when made

Any specific numeric claim, funding fee rate, conforming loan limit, county loan limit, seasoning period, recoupment period, residual income figure, MPR threshold, NTB factor, statutory citation, should carry an inline citation to a primary source (VA.gov, VA Pamphlet 26-7, 38 USC, 38 CFR, FHFA, or a current Circular). This applies to consumer-facing pages, not just internal research.

### 16.4 Disclaimers required on consumer-facing pages

Any reader-facing VA loan content should carry, at a minimum:

- Equal Housing Lender logo and disclosure;
- NMLS ID for the lender and the loan originator;
- Alabama state mortgage license number (Alabama Department of Banking);
- "Subject to credit approval" disclaimer;
- ECOA notice if rates or terms are quoted;
- Compliant TILA Reg Z disclosures if APR or interest rate is advertised;
- A statement that the page is informational, not legal or benefits advice;
- Routing line for benefits questions (VA, VSO, county VSO).
